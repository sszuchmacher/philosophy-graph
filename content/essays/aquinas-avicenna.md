Aquinas owed Avicenna one of the pillars of his metaphysics — the **distinction between essence and existence** — and much of his originality lies in *how he corrects* Avicenna's version of it. The agreement is deep; so is the reproach.

Both hold that in every creature, essence (what a thing is) and existence (that it is) are really distinct, and that God alone is the being whose essence *is* to exist. This is what secures the utter dependence of creation on God: creatures do not have existence of themselves but receive it. Aquinas takes this over from Avicenna, and it lets him read the God of Exodus — "I AM WHO I AM" — as pure *ipsum esse subsistens*, subsistent Being itself.

But Avicenna had put the point in a way Aquinas found misleading. Avicenna spoke as if existence were **added to** an essence, almost like an accident befalling a nature that is already, in some sense, there. Aquinas objects that this gets the metaphysics backwards. Existence is not an accident, one property among others; it is the deepest **act** of a thing, that by which it is anything at all. Essence is *potency*, the capacity to be a certain kind of thing; existence (*esse*) is the *act* that makes it actual.

The correction is subtle but decisive. By making *esse* the act of being rather than an added accident, Aquinas turns Avicenna's distinction into a whole metaphysics of participation: creatures *have* being, God *is* being, and everything that exists does so by sharing, in a limited way, in the pure existence that God is. He keeps Avicenna's insight and, he would say, finally understands it.
