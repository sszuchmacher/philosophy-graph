Malebranche read Descartes's *Treatise on Man* as a young Oratorian priest and was, by his own account, so overcome that his heart pounded and he had to set the book down. He devoted his life to developing Cartesian philosophy — but he fused it with the Augustinian theology of his order, so that Descartes is seen, throughout, through Augustine's eyes.

He accepts the Cartesian framework: the radical distinction of mind and body, the mechanical, geometric conception of matter, the priority of clear and distinct ideas. But he presses two Cartesian difficulties to startling, God-centered conclusions.

The first is **causation**. Descartes had never satisfactorily explained how utterly different substances — thinking mind and extended body — could interact. Malebranche's answer is **occasionalism**: they don't. No creature is ever a true cause of anything; created things are only "occasions" on which God, the sole real cause, acts. When I will my arm to rise, God moves it; when fire meets wax, God melts it. All causal power belongs to God alone.

The second is **knowledge**. How does the mind grasp ideas of things outside it? Malebranche answers, in a thoroughly Augustinian key, that **we see all things in God**: the ideas by which we know the world exist in the divine mind, and God illumines us with them. To perceive a truth is to participate in God's own ideas.

The result is Cartesian mechanism absorbed into Augustinian theocentrism. Descartes had put God at the start of his system as guarantor of clear ideas; Malebranche puts God at every point — the only cause of every event and the light of every thought. He carries Cartesianism to a conclusion Descartes would not have drawn: a universe in which nothing acts and nothing is known except in and through God.
