Pragmatism had two founders, and William James always credited the first: Charles Peirce, whose 1878 essay "How to Make Our Ideas Clear" James singled out, decades later, as the origin of the whole movement. James took Peirce's **pragmatic maxim** and made it famous — and in doing so transformed it almost past its author's recognition.

Peirce's maxim was a rule for clarifying the *meaning of concepts*: to grasp what a conception amounts to, trace the practical, experimental consequences it would have. It was a technical principle of logic and inquiry, austere and impersonal, aimed at making scientific ideas precise, framed in terms of the long-run findings of a community of investigators.

James broadens it into a general theory of **truth** and a philosophy of life. Where Peirce asked what a concept *means*, James asks whether a belief is *true* — and answers with the pragmatic test: a belief is true if it "works," if it leads us satisfactorily through experience, if its practical consequences for the believer are good. Truth becomes something that *happens* to an idea in the course of its verification, and the "cash value" of an idea its practical payoff in lived experience.

He also loosens the framework in a personal, humanistic direction Peirce distrusted. Where Peirce was rigorous, logical, and communal — truth as the ideal endpoint of scientific inquiry — James is psychological, individual, and generous, willing to count religious and moral beliefs as true if they enrich a person's life (as in his "will to believe"). Consequences "for the believer" can include emotional and spiritual ones, not just experimental predictions.

Peirce was so alarmed by what James had done to his idea that he renamed his own doctrine "**pragmaticism**" — a word, he said, "ugly enough to be safe from kidnappers" — to distinguish his logical pragmatism from James's popular, humanistic version.

So the continuity is also a divergence. James takes Peirce's precise rule for the meaning of concepts and expands it into a sweeping, warm-blooded account of truth and belief that carried pragmatism to the world — at the price of the rigor its founder had built in.
