Amid the moderns, Hegel felt a deep kinship with Aristotle, and he ended his greatest system with Aristotle's words. The *Encyclopedia* closes by quoting, in Greek, the passage from Aristotle's *Metaphysics* on God as **thought thinking itself** — for Hegel the highest expression of what his own philosophy was after.

The affinity has several strands. Against the mechanical, mathematical nature of Descartes and Newton, Hegel prized Aristotle's world of **immanent teleology** — a reality that develops toward ends latent within it, form realizing itself in matter, potentiality passing into actuality. Hegel's dialectic is a teleological unfolding of just this kind: the Concept develops toward its own full actualization, exactly the Aristotelian movement of *dynamis* into *energeia*, writ across all of reality and history.

But the deepest debt is Aristotle's **Nous** — the divine self-thinking thought. Aristotle's God is pure activity, an intellect whose object is itself, thought contemplating thought. Hegel takes this as the very model of his **Absolute Spirit**: reality as a self-thinking process that, through nature and human history, comes at last to know itself completely — Spirit thinking Spirit. Philosophy, at its summit, is the Absolute's self-knowledge, and this is Aristotle's *noēsis noēseōs* realized as the goal of the whole cosmic and historical process.

Yet Hegel does not merely repeat Aristotle. Aristotle's Nous was static and self-enclosed, contemplating itself in changeless eternity, aloof from the world it moved only as final cause. Hegel sets the self-thinking thought in **motion and history**: his Absolute achieves self-knowledge not from the start but as the *result* of a long development, working through nature and the whole labor of human culture and coming to itself in time.

So Hegel reanimates the Aristotelian Nous — thought thinking itself as the highest reality — while turning Aristotle's eternal, motionless intellect into a living, developing, historical Spirit. The oldest Greek vision of God as self-contemplating mind returns, at the end of German Idealism, as the destination of the whole dialectical journey.
