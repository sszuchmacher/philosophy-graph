Lévi-Strauss revered Rousseau, calling him "our master, our brother" and the founder of the human sciences — a striking homage from the great structuralist to the eighteenth-century philosopher of feeling. His anthropology is shot through with Rousseauian themes.

The deepest debt is Rousseau's founding **anthropological gesture**: the attempt to understand humanity by imagining or observing it in its most "natural," least civilized state, and the use of the "savage" as a mirror in which European society could see, and criticize, itself. Rousseau's *Discourse on Inequality* had turned to natural man to expose the corruptions and artificial inequalities of civilization. Lévi-Strauss makes this the very posture of the anthropologist — and in *Tristes Tropiques*, his melancholy masterpiece, the study of small-scale societies becomes, as in Rousseau, a critical reflection on the pathologies and destructiveness of the West.

From Rousseau too comes a refusal of European **self-satisfaction and progress**. Lévi-Strauss shares Rousseau's suspicion that "civilization" is not simple advance but also loss — that so-called primitive societies possess forms of wisdom and balance the modern West has destroyed in itself and now spreads over the earth as it eradicates cultural diversity. His famous lament over the homogenizing, entropic force of Western expansion is Rousseauian in spirit: a mourning for what progress annihilates.

He also credits Rousseau with a proto-structuralist insight into the human mind — Rousseau's analysis of **pity** and of the capacity to identify with others, his sense that the human being is defined by the ability to put oneself in another's place, which Lévi-Strauss reads as an early recognition of the symbolic, relational nature of human thought.

But Lévi-Strauss also transforms Rousseau by stripping away the sentimental and evolutionary story. He does not believe in a literal historical "state of nature" or a fall; his "natural" structures are the timeless, unconscious patterns of the human mind, not a lost paradise. The Rousseauian critique of civilization is kept but rebuilt on structuralist rather than developmental foundations.

So Lévi-Strauss continues Rousseau as the acknowledged ancestor of anthropology — inheriting the study of humanity through its simplest societies, the "savage" as a critical mirror for the West, the suspicion of progress, and the mourning of lost cultural diversity — while recasting these Enlightenment-Romantic themes in the cool, systematic key of structuralism. Rousseau supplied the humane, self-critical impulse; Lévi-Strauss gave it a scientific method and a tragic modern voice.
