Heidegger's *Being and Time* is a work of fundamental ontology, austere and systematic — yet its most gripping analyses, of anxiety, death, guilt, conscience, and authenticity, are lifted almost bodily from Kierkegaard, the Danish Christian of a century earlier. Heidegger acknowledges the debt in footnotes, if grudgingly, and the existential heart of his masterpiece is Kierkegaardian through and through.

From Kierkegaard, Heidegger takes the primacy of the concrete, **existing individual** over abstract system — the insistence that what matters is not humanity in general but this singular being who must live its own life. Kierkegaard's protest against Hegel, that existence cannot be dissolved into a totality of thought, becomes Heidegger's starting point in **Dasein**, the being whose own being is at issue for it.

Specific analyses carry Kierkegaard's fingerprints. **Anxiety** (*Angst*), which for Kierkegaard was the "dizziness of freedom" before infinite possibility, becomes in Heidegger the fundamental mood that discloses Dasein's freedom and its being-toward-nothing. The distinction between **authentic** and **inauthentic** existence — living as one's own or losing oneself in the anonymous crowd — reworks Kierkegaard's contrast between genuine selfhood and the leveling "public." Even Heidegger's "they" (*das Man*), the impersonal "one does what one does," echoes Kierkegaard's critique of the crowd as untruth.

But Heidegger **secularizes and ontologizes** what in Kierkegaard was religious. Kierkegaard's categories were stages on the way to *faith*: anxiety and despair drive the individual toward God, and authentic selfhood is finally selfhood *before God*. Heidegger strips out the theology. Anxiety discloses not God but Being and nothingness; authenticity is achieved not by a leap of faith but by resolutely facing one's own death and finitude, owning one's existence without any religious beyond.

So the continuity is a transposition. Heidegger takes Kierkegaard's passionate analyses of the individual's existence — anxiety, death, the flight into the crowd, the call to become oneself — and rebuilds them as structures of Being, converting a Christian summons to faith into a philosophy of finite, worldly existence. Existentialism's vocabulary is largely Kierkegaard's; Heidegger gave it an ontological grammar and cut its ties to God.
