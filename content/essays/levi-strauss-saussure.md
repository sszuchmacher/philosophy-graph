Claude Lévi-Strauss founded **structural anthropology** by taking the method Ferdinand de Saussure had devised for **language** and extending it to the whole of human culture. Structuralism, as a movement, is essentially this generalization of Saussurean linguistics beyond language.

From Saussure, Lévi-Strauss took a cluster of revolutionary ideas. First, that meaning arises not from the intrinsic content of individual elements but from their **differences and relations within a system** — Saussure had shown that a sign has value only through its opposition to other signs, that language is "a system of differences without positive terms." Second, the priority of the **synchronic** system (the structure at a moment) over the diachronic (its historical development). Third, and most fundamental, the distinction between the underlying **structure** (Saussure's *langue*, the system of rules) and its individual manifestations (*parole*, particular utterances) — and the conviction that this structure is **unconscious**, operating beneath the awareness of those who use it.

Lévi-Strauss applies all of this to culture. **Kinship systems**, he argues, work like a language: marriage rules are a system for the exchange of women between groups, structured by underlying rules the participants follow without consciously grasping, exactly as speakers follow grammar. **Myths** are analyzed as structured systems whose meaning lies not in their content but in the pattern of **binary oppositions** (raw/cooked, nature/culture, life/death) and their transformations — the myth means through its structure, as a sentence means through its grammar. Beneath the diversity of human cultures, he seeks the universal, unconscious structures of the human **mind** itself, which orders experience through binary contrasts.

The Saussurean model gives Lévi-Strauss his most radical thesis: that behind the varied surface of customs, myths, and institutions lie a small number of universal structural patterns, unconscious and mind-imposed — that culture, like language, is a system of signs to be decoded, and that anthropology can become a rigorous science by seeking the *langue* beneath the *parole* of every practice.

So Lévi-Strauss continues Saussure by carrying structural linguistics out of language and into anthropology — reading kinship, myth, and culture as sign-systems governed by unconscious structures of difference and opposition. In doing so he launched **structuralism** as a movement that would sweep through anthropology, literary theory, psychoanalysis, and philosophy — the ambition to find, everywhere in human life, the hidden systematic structures Saussure had discovered in language.
