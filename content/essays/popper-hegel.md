In *The Open Society and Its Enemies*, Popper devoted some of his fiercest polemic to **Hegel**, whom he cast as a principal philosophical ancestor of modern **totalitarianism** — a villain standing between Plato and Marx in the story of the "enemies" of the open society.

Popper's critique has a philosophical and a political dimension, tightly linked. Philosophically, he attacks Hegel's **dialectic** and his **historicism**. Hegel's philosophy of history presents history as the necessary, rational self-development of Spirit toward freedom — a grand law-governed process with a preordained direction. Popper classes this as "historicism": the false and dangerous belief that history obeys discoverable laws and moves toward an inevitable goal. And he regards the dialectical method — reasoning through contradiction, the reconciliation of opposites — as a means of evading rational criticism, a slippery logic that can "prove" anything and its opposite and that abandons the ordinary standards of consistency. He famously derided much of Hegel's prose as pretentious, obscurantist verbiage designed to impress rather than to say anything testable.

Politically, Popper charges that Hegel glorified the **state** and the **nation** at the expense of the individual. Hegel's exaltation of the state as the "march of God in the world," the highest realization of ethical life to which the individual is subordinate; his idea that world-historical peoples and great men embody the movement of Spirit; his subordination of the individual to the collective and to history — all of this, Popper argues, provided the intellectual materials for both the **nationalist** and the **authoritarian** strands of twentieth-century totalitarianism, feeding fascism and, via Marx, communism alike. The Hegelian worship of the state and of historical necessity is, for Popper, the enemy of the open society, which prizes the individual, freedom, and rational criticism.

Popper's reading of Hegel is notoriously **harsh and contested** — many scholars regard it as a caricature that misreads Hegel's actual political philosophy and unfairly conflates him with later nationalist abuses. Popper's Hegel is more a symbol of the tendencies he opposed than a careful exegesis. But as a statement of Popper's own values, the critique is clear.

So Popper's relation to Hegel is a wholesale critique from the standpoint of the open society and critical rationalism. He condemns Hegel's historicist philosophy of history and dialectical method as false, uncriticizable, and obscurantist, and his political philosophy as a glorification of state and nation that subordinates the individual and prepares the way for totalitarianism. Against Hegel's Spirit marching through history toward a necessary end, Popper sets fallible individuals, piecemeal reform, and a society open to criticism and change — placing Hegel, alongside Plato and Marx, among the great philosophical enemies of freedom.
