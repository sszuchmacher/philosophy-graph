Habermas is, in a deep sense, a Kantian — but a Kantian who has passed through Hegel, Marx, pragmatism, and the "linguistic turn." His mature philosophy reinterprets Kant's central projects — universal morality, the grounding of reason, the ideal of enlightenment — by relocating them from the solitary transcendental subject to the intersubjective medium of **language and communication**.

The Kantian inheritance is pervasive. Habermas defends, against relativists and post-structuralists, the possibility of **universal norms** and rational justification; he upholds the **Enlightenment** project of reason and autonomy as an unfinished task rather than a discredited illusion; and his ethics is thoroughly Kantian in spirit — universalist, deontological, centered on the impartial testing of moral norms.

But he transforms Kant by **proceduralizing and intersubjectivizing** him. Kant had grounded morality in the individual reason: each rational agent, alone, tests maxims against the categorical imperative, asking whether he could will them as universal law. Habermas argues that this monological procedure is too solitary. His **discourse ethics** relocates the test into actual (or idealized) **dialogue**: a norm is valid only if it could win the agreement of *all those affected* in a free and rational discussion, where everyone can speak and only the force of the better argument decides. The categorical imperative becomes a principle of communicative agreement — validity is what could be justified to everyone, not what one lonely reason can universalize.

Correspondingly, Habermas replaces Kant's **transcendental** grounding with a **quasi-transcendental** one drawn from the structure of communication itself. Where Kant located the conditions of reason in the faculties of the mind, Habermas locates them in the **presuppositions of speech** — the "ideal speech situation," the unavoidable assumptions of freedom, equality, and truthfulness that anyone makes in seriously arguing and seeking agreement. Reason's norms are built into the practice of reaching understanding.

So Habermas reinterprets Kant by carrying the Kantian universalism, the ethics of respect for persons, and the Enlightenment faith in reason out of the isolated subject and into intersubjective communication. The transcendental ego becomes the community of speakers; the categorical imperative becomes discursive agreement; the a priori conditions of reason become the presuppositions of dialogue. It is Kant rebuilt for a post-metaphysical, linguistic age — the moral universalism and rational autonomy preserved, but grounded now in what people owe one another when they argue rather than in the structure of a single mind.
