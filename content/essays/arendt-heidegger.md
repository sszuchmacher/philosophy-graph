Arendt studied with Heidegger as a young woman — they were briefly lovers, and the entanglement, personal and philosophical, lasted a lifetime — and she was formed by his thought even as she came to define herself against it. Her political philosophy is, in part, a **critique** of her teacher, turning his own concepts against the direction he took them.

From Heidegger, Arendt inherited deep resources: the analysis of human existence as thrown into a world and disclosed through concernful engagement, and a phenomenological attentiveness to the structures of human existence. Her whole vocabulary of world, disclosure, and appearance bears his stamp.

But her critique is sharp and, after 1933, morally charged — for Heidegger's embrace of Nazism was for Arendt not an accident but a symptom of a flaw in his thought. Two objections stand out. First, Heidegger's **authenticity was solitary and world-fleeing**. His authentic Dasein achieves itself in a private confrontation with its own death and finitude, withdrawing from the "they," the public world of others, which he treated as the realm of inauthenticity and fallenness. Arendt inverts this valuation completely. For her, the human being realizes itself precisely *in* the public world, through **action and speech among a plurality of others**; the shared world of appearance is not a fall from authenticity but the very space where freedom and human meaning arise. She also counters his **being-toward-death** with **natality** — making birth, not death, the fundamental human fact and the ground of new beginnings.

Second, Heidegger's thought had **no place for plurality and politics**. It thought "man" (Dasein) in the singular, and its resoluteness pointed to no shared world of equals acting together — a void that, Arendt suggests, left it politically rudderless and helped make possible his catastrophic engagement. Against this she insists that the basic condition of politics is **plurality**: not Man but *men*, distinct and equal, inhabit the earth.

So Arendt's relation to Heidegger is a critique that keeps his tools and reverses his conclusions. She takes his phenomenology of human existence and his concepts of world and disclosure, replaces being-toward-death with natality, and above all replaces his solitary, world-fleeing authenticity with a philosophy of plural human beings acting together in a public realm. Heidegger's deepest failure, for her, was to think existence without plurality and without the world of politics; her life's work is the philosophy of exactly what he left out.
