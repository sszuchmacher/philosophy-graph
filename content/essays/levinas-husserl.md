Levinas was one of the first to bring Husserl's phenomenology to France — his early study and translation work introduced French readers to the movement — and phenomenological method remained the ground beneath even his most anti-traditional ideas. He learned from Husserl how to *describe* experience with rigor, and he never abandoned that discipline.

From Husserl, Levinas takes the method of **phenomenological description** and the concept of **intentionality** — consciousness as always directed toward, always constituting its objects. His whole philosophy proceeds by careful description of concrete experiences: enjoyment, dwelling, fatigue, the caress, and above all the encounter with the face of the Other. This is Husserlian phenomenology in technique, attending to how phenomena give themselves to experience.

But Levinas comes to find Husserlian intentionality too narrow, and his originality lies in describing an experience that *breaks* its frame. Husserl's intentionality is fundamentally a relation of a knowing subject to an object it constitutes and comprehends — the object is "there for" consciousness, grasped, made present, absorbed into the subject's meaning-giving. Levinas argues that the encounter with the **Other person** cannot be captured this way. The face of the Other is not an object I constitute or comprehend; it *overflows* the idea I have of it, resists being reduced to a content of my consciousness, and confronts me with an ethical demand that comes from beyond me. Here intentionality meets something it cannot contain — an "infinity" that shatters the subject's comprehending grasp.

He also draws on Husserl's own late analyses — of time-consciousness, of the passive syntheses beneath active constitution, of the body — to find, within phenomenology, dimensions of experience prior to and deeper than objectifying knowledge.

So Levinas continues Husserl by using phenomenological description as his lifelong method, while turning it against Husserl's intellectualism. Where Husserl made the constituting, comprehending consciousness central, Levinas uses phenomenology to describe an encounter — the face of the Other — that exceeds all comprehension and grounds ethics. The method is Husserl's; the discovery, Levinas claims, is what Husserl's method reveals once it reaches what consciousness cannot possess.
