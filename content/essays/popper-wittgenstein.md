Popper's antagonism to **Wittgenstein** was personal and philosophical — it culminated in a famous confrontation at Cambridge in 1946, where (by legend) Wittgenstein brandished a poker at Popper during a heated argument. Behind the anecdote lies a deep opposition about the nature and purpose of philosophy.

Popper's central objection targets the view — shared, in different forms, by the early Wittgenstein, the logical positivists he inspired, and the later Wittgenstein — that philosophical problems are really **pseudo-problems**, confusions of **language** to be dissolved rather than genuine problems to be solved. The *Tractatus* had suggested that philosophical puzzles arise from misunderstanding the logic of language; the later Wittgenstein held that philosophical problems are conceptual confusions arising when "language goes on holiday," to be *dissolved* by describing ordinary usage, so that philosophy "leaves everything as it is" and issues in no doctrines. In both phases, philosophy becomes a kind of linguistic therapy, not a search for truth about the world.

Popper regarded this as trivializing and false. There *are* genuine philosophical problems, he insisted — real problems about the growth of knowledge, scientific method, determinism, the mind, the open society — that are not mere puzzles of language and cannot be dissolved by attending to how words are used. His famous provocation at the 1946 meeting was to come armed with a list of real philosophical problems, against Wittgenstein's claim (as Popper reported it) that there are only linguistic puzzles. To reduce philosophy to the diagnosis of linguistic confusion is, Popper thought, to abandon philosophy's genuine task and to retreat from the world into words.

He also rejected the **verificationism** and criterion of meaning the Vienna Circle drew from the *Tractatus* — the doctrine that metaphysical statements are literally meaningless. Popper argued that the demarcation between science and non-science is not a matter of *meaning* (meaningful vs. meaningless) but of *testability* (falsifiable vs. not), and that "meaningless" metaphysics can be meaningful, valuable, and even the seedbed of science. The positivists, following Wittgenstein, wanted to *eliminate* metaphysics as nonsense; Popper wanted to *demarcate* science from metaphysics while respecting the latter.

So Popper's relation to Wittgenstein is a fundamental critique of the linguistic turn in philosophy. Against the Wittgensteinian view — in both the *Tractatus* and the *Investigations* — that philosophical problems are confusions of language to be dissolved, Popper insists that there are real problems to be solved and that philosophy's job is to solve them, not to diagnose linguistic misuse. And against the verificationist theory of meaning Wittgenstein inspired, he substitutes falsifiability as the mark of science. The poker was a symbol: two irreconcilable visions of what philosophy is for — therapy that dissolves problems, or bold conjecture that tries to solve them.
