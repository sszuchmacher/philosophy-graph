Irigaray engages Hegel critically, targeting both his dialectic and, most memorably, his reading of **Antigone** — to expose how Western philosophy's supreme system of thought is built on the exclusion and subordination of the feminine.

Hegel had given the figure of Antigone a famous place in the *Phenomenology of Spirit*. Antigone, who defies the state to bury her brother, represents for Hegel the "divine law" of the family, kinship, and the feminine — set against the "human law" of the state, the public, and the masculine. In the dialectic, this feminine principle of the family is a lower, more immediate ethical moment that must be **superseded** (*aufgehoben*) by the higher, public, political world of the state and Spirit. Woman, tied to the family and kinship, belongs to a stage that history and Spirit leave behind; she is the "eternal irony of the community," consigned to the private household while men make history.

Irigaray reads this as emblematic of the whole tradition. Hegel's dialectic, she argues, cannot think the feminine as anything but a subordinate moment to be sublated; woman is confined to nature, family, and immediacy, and denied a place as a subject in the movement of Spirit. The feminine is what must be overcome and left behind so that (masculine) Spirit can advance. Antigone, buried alive in the play, is buried again by Hegel — entombed in the private sphere, silenced, made a mere stage on the way to the male political world.

More broadly, Irigaray attacks the Hegelian **dialectic** itself as a logic of the "same" that cannot accommodate genuine **difference**, especially sexual difference. The dialectic works by negation and sublation, absorbing the other into a higher unity — reducing the other to a moment of the same, exactly the operation by which the feminine is denied autonomous being. A logic that resolves all difference into a totalizing unity of Spirit has no room for an irreducibly other feminine subject; it can only subordinate or absorb her.

So Irigaray's relation to Hegel is a feminist critique of the tradition's master-dialectician. Through his treatment of Antigone she exposes how the feminine is systematically confined to family and nature and sublated by masculine Spirit and the state; and through her critique of the dialectic of sublation she argues that Hegel's logic of the same cannot think sexual difference at all. Hegel's system, for Irigaray, is the philosophical monument to the burial of the feminine — and her demand for a positive thinking of sexual difference is aimed squarely at the dialectic that could only bury it.
