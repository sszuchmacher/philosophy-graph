Maimonides, the greatest Jewish philosopher of the Middle Ages, faced the problem his Muslim and later Christian counterparts also faced: the recovered Aristotle was the most powerful account of the natural world available, and it did not obviously agree with revealed scripture. His *Guide for the Perplexed* is written precisely for the believer caught between the two — the student learned in both Torah and philosophy who feels their apparent contradiction as a torment.

His method is bold reinterpretation of *scripture* in Aristotle's light. Where the Bible speaks of God's hand, God's anger, God descending, Maimonides insists these must be read as **metaphor**; to take them literally, ascribing body or passion to God, is a graver error than idolatry. Aristotelian philosophy, showing that God must be one, incorporeal, and unchanging, becomes a guide to the Bible's true meaning.

Yet Maimonides does not simply capitulate to Aristotle. On the decisive question — whether the world is **eternal** (as Aristotle held) or **created** (as Torah teaches) — he breaks with the Philosopher. Aristotle's arguments for eternity, he says, are not demonstrative but merely probable; since neither side can be proved, and creation better accords with revelation, the believer may hold to creation without irrationality. Philosophy sets the limits of what reason can settle, and within those limits faith may decide.

The result is a delicate synthesis: Aristotle governs physics and the doctrine of God's incorporeality; Torah governs where reason falls silent. Maimonides reinterprets the tradition so that Aristotle and Moses can coexist — a reconciliation so influential that Aquinas, facing the identical tension in Christianity, studied "Rabbi Moses" closely and borrowed his strategy of limiting Aristotle's arguments to make room for creation and faith.
