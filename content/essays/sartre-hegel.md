Sartre's *Being and Nothingness* is deeply marked by Hegel — above all by the **dialectic of recognition** and the master-slave struggle — absorbed largely through Alexandre Kojève's famous Paris lectures that shaped a whole French generation.

The clearest debt is Sartre's analysis of **the Other** and "the Look." Hegel had argued, in the *Phenomenology*, that self-consciousness comes to itself only through another self-consciousness — that I become aware of myself as a self only by being recognized by, and struggling with, another. Sartre takes this over and darkens it. When the Other looks at me, I experience myself as an *object* for them, my freedom suddenly frozen into a thing seen from outside; I feel shame, the recognition that I exist for another consciousness. The encounter with the Other is a struggle over who is subject and who is object — a conflict for recognition that echoes Hegel's life-and-death struggle of self-consciousnesses.

But Sartre pointedly refuses Hegel's resolution. For Hegel, the struggle for recognition is ultimately *reconciling*: through it, self-consciousnesses can achieve mutual recognition, and the dialectic moves toward a higher unity, a "we," the reconciliation of individuals in Spirit and ethical community. Sartre denies that this reconciliation is possible. Because each consciousness is an absolute freedom that objectifies the other simply by looking, human relations are locked in perpetual conflict: I cannot be a free subject *and* recognized as such by another free subject at the same time. Love, desire, and every relationship oscillate between making the other an object and being made one. "Hell is other people."

So Sartre continues Hegel's insight — that the self is constituted through the Other, that recognition is fundamental — while rejecting the Hegelian synthesis. Where Hegel's dialectic of recognition drives toward a reconciled "we" and the overcoming of conflict in Spirit, Sartre freezes it at the moment of struggle, with no synthesis available: an existentialist Hegel without the reconciliation, in which the conflict of consciousnesses is not a stage on the way to unity but the permanent structure of being-with-others.
