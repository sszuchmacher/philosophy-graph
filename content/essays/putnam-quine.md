Putnam was a student of Quine's and worked his whole career within the broadly naturalistic, science-respecting analytic tradition Quine had shaped — even as he restlessly revised his own views and, on several points, turned against his teacher. The continuity is a shared framework repeatedly renegotiated from inside.

From Quine, Putnam took the **naturalism** — the conviction that philosophy is continuous with science, that there is no first philosophy standing above and judging science from outside, and that ontological questions are to be answered by looking to our best scientific theories. Their most famous joint legacy is the **Quine-Putnam indispensability argument** for the reality of mathematical objects: we should believe in the entities (like numbers) that are indispensable to our best scientific theories, since we are already committed to those theories; mathematics earns its ontological keep by being woven inextricably into physics. This argument, bearing both their names, is a direct expression of Quinean holism — the web of belief, in which mathematics and empirical science stand or fall together.

Putnam also shared Quine's suspicion of the **analytic-synthetic distinction** (though he complicated it), his holism, and his respect for logic and formal rigor.

But Putnam departed from Quine on crucial matters, and the departures show how a continuous tradition evolves by internal criticism. Against Quine's behaviorism about meaning and his indeterminacy of translation, Putnam developed **semantic externalism** — the Twin Earth argument that meaning depends on the external world, not just on behavior or inner states — restoring a robust notion of reference that Quine's indeterminacy had threatened. Against Quine's flirtation with eliminating the mental, Putnam pioneered **functionalism** in the philosophy of mind. And where Quine held a rather deflationary view of truth, Putnam wrestled his whole life with realism and truth, moving from scientific realism through "internal realism" and back toward a common-sense realism.

So Putnam continues Quine as an heir who inherited the naturalistic, holistic, science-first framework — and their shared indispensability argument remains a landmark — while pushing against Quine's behaviorism and indeterminacy from within. He is the Quinean who kept the naturalism but restored meaning, reference, and the mind that Quine's austerity had put in doubt, exemplifying how the analytic tradition advances less by revolution than by gifted students amending their teachers.
