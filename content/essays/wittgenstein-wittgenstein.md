*This connection is a special case: a philosopher in dialogue with his own earlier work. It is included to show that the model of the graph allows a node to relate to itself across distinct stages.*

In 1921, the young Wittgenstein believed he had solved, in essence, the problems of philosophy. The *Tractatus* proposes a precise picture of how language means: propositions are **pictures** of facts. A sentence has sense because its structure mirrors the structure of a possible state of affairs in the world, just as a map mirrors a territory. What cannot be pictured — ethics, the mystical, the meaning of life — cannot be said; about it one must keep silent.

Twenty years later, the same man demolishes that theory. And the most penetrating critic of the *Tractatus* turns out to be its own author.

> ...the meaning of a word is its use in the language.

The turn is in that sentence. Meaning is no longer a picture the word projects onto the world but something we do: a practice. Words are like tools; their sense lies in how we use them within shared forms of life. Wittgenstein calls these **language games**: requesting, promising, joking, calculating, praying. There is no single essence of language, as he believed in his youth, but a family of uses that resemble one another without sharing a common feature.

Why classify this as *critique* and not simple change of mind? Because the *Philosophical Investigations* dispute, sometimes word by word, the theses of the *Tractatus*. The late Wittgenstein diagnoses the early one: his error was to be bewitched by one picture of language — the picture of the picture — and to confuse a particular case with the essence of all. "A picture held us captive," he writes.

The case raises a question as interesting in modeling as it is philosophically: when someone's thought changes at the root, are we still facing one person, or two interlocutors sharing a name?
