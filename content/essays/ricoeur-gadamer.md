Ricoeur and Gadamer were the two great figures of twentieth-century hermeneutics, and Ricoeur developed his own theory in close dialogue with Gadamer's *Truth and Method* — sharing its foundations while enriching it in ways Gadamer had not.

From Gadamer, Ricoeur takes the core hermeneutic vision: that understanding is historically situated and shaped by **tradition**, that we always interpret from within a horizon of prejudices, and that understanding a text involves a **fusion of horizons** rather than the neutral recovery of an author's intention. He accepts Gadamer's rehabilitation of tradition and prejudice and his critique of the Enlightenment ideal of a presuppositionless interpretation.

But Ricoeur presses beyond Gadamer on a crucial point: the role of **method, distance, and critique**. Gadamer had opposed "truth" to "method," warning that the objectifying methods of the human sciences (and the "alienating distanciation" they involve) distort the living event of understanding, which is a matter of belonging to a tradition and being addressed by a text. Ricoeur worries this leaves hermeneutics defenseless against ideology and unable to incorporate the genuine achievements of the structural and critical sciences of texts. He argues that **distanciation** — the way a written text becomes detached from its author and original context, standing as an autonomous work — is not merely an obstacle but a **positive, productive** feature of interpretation. It is precisely because the text is distanced from its origin that it opens up a "world in front of the text," a range of possible meanings the reader can appropriate.

This lets Ricoeur reconcile what Gadamer had opposed: **explanation and understanding**. The objective, methodical analysis of a text's structure (as in structuralism) is not the enemy of understanding but a necessary stage *within* it — we explain more in order to understand better. Ricoeur thus mediates between Gadamer's belonging-to-tradition and the critical, methodical rigor of the human sciences, and between hermeneutics and the "critique of ideology" that Habermas urged against Gadamer.

So Ricoeur continues Gadamer's philosophical hermeneutics — the situatedness in tradition, the fusion of horizons, the primacy of the text's disclosed world over authorial intention — while correcting its anti-methodological one-sidedness. He restores a positive place for distance, explanation, and critique, arguing that method and truth, far from opposed, belong together in the labor of interpretation. Gadamer showed that understanding is rooted in tradition; Ricoeur showed how the disciplined, distancing methods of textual science can serve rather than betray it.
