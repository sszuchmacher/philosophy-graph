Popper deeply admired **Kant** — he called him the philosopher who had come closest to the truth about knowledge — but his own philosophy is, in key respects, a **critical correction** of Kant, salvaging Kant's central insight while abandoning the certainty Kant claimed for it.

The insight Popper takes from Kant is that the mind is **active** in knowledge — that we do not passively receive nature's imprint but bring to experience our own organizing structures. Kant's "Copernican revolution" held that objects conform to our forms of intuition and categories, not the other way around; the understanding, in a phrase Popper loved, "prescribes its laws to nature." Popper embraces this anti-empiricist point wholeheartedly: observation is never pure or theory-free; we always approach nature with expectations, hypotheses, frameworks — "the theory comes first." Our knowledge is shaped by the questions and conjectures we bring, not read off from bare data. In this, Popper is a proud Kantian against naive empiricism.

But Popper sharply criticizes Kant's conclusion that these organizing structures yield **synthetic a priori knowledge** that is **certain, necessary, and unrevisable**. Kant believed that the mind's contributions — Euclidean geometry, the causal principle, Newtonian physics — were fixed once and for all, the necessary and universal framework of any possible experience, known with a priori certainty. Popper argues that this was Kant's great mistake, and that it was refuted by history: Einstein's relativity overthrew the Newtonian framework, and non-Euclidean geometries displaced Euclid's supposed necessity. If the frameworks Kant thought were the necessary, certain a priori conditions of all experience could turn out to be *false* and be replaced, then they were never certain or necessary at all.

Popper's correction is to keep Kant's active mind while making its contributions **conjectural and fallible**. Yes, we prescribe our frameworks to nature — Kant was right — but these frameworks are not a fixed, certain a priori; they are bold **hypotheses**, invented by us, which nature can *refute*. The categories and theories we impose are guesses that may be wrong and may be overthrown.

So Popper's relation to Kant is a critique that saves the insight and drops the certainty. He inherits Kant's revolutionary thesis that the mind actively structures experience and that knowledge is not built from pure observation — but he rejects Kant's claim that these structures are a fixed, necessary, certain synthetic a priori. History showed the frameworks fallible; Popper concludes that our organizing theories are conjectures we impose and nature refutes. Kant made the mind active but its contributions certain; Popper made the mind active and its contributions forever open to correction.
