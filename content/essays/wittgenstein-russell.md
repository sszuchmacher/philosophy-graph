Wittgenstein came to Cambridge in 1911 to study with Russell, and the encounter transformed both men. Russell quickly recognized in his intense young student a genius who would carry his own logical project further than he could — "perhaps the most perfect example I have ever known of genius as traditionally conceived," he wrote. The early Wittgenstein is unthinkable without Russell.

From Russell (and through him Frege), Wittgenstein inherited the new **mathematical logic** and the conviction that its analysis of language holds the key to philosophy. Russell's project of logical analysis — dissolving philosophical problems by revealing the true logical form beneath misleading surface grammar, as in his theory of descriptions — became the method Wittgenstein would radicalize in the *Tractatus*. The idea that the structure of language mirrors the structure of the world, and that logic lays this bare, is a Russellian inheritance.

But even in the *Tractatus* Wittgenstein goes beyond and against his teacher. Where Russell's logical atomism was an ongoing empirical-metaphysical inquiry, Wittgenstein claims to give the definitive account of the logical form of any possible language and world, and reaches a conclusion Russell found mystical and troubling: that the propositions of logic are empty tautologies saying nothing, that the limits of language are the limits of the world, and that the most important things — ethics, the meaning of life, the mystical — cannot be *said* at all, only *shown*, so that philosophy ends in silence.

Russell, who wrote the introduction to the *Tractatus*, admitted he did not fully understand or accept its final turn toward the unsayable, and Wittgenstein felt Russell had misunderstood the book's point. Their philosophical intimacy cooled.

So Wittgenstein continues Russell as the pupil who took his master's logic and method of analysis and drove them to a systematic conclusion Russell himself would not follow — using Russellian tools to map the limits of language, then declaring that the deepest matters lie beyond those limits. Russell gave Wittgenstein the logic; Wittgenstein turned it into a work that both fulfilled and unsettled the Russellian program, and that would define one pole of twentieth-century philosophy.
