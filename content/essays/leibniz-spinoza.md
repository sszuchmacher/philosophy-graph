Leibniz visited Spinoza in 1676 and read the *Ethics* in manuscript, and for the rest of his life he circled the Dutch philosopher with a mixture of fascination and horror. Spinoza represented the abyss into which Leibniz feared modern philosophy was sliding — a single, necessary, impersonal God-substance, with no genuine individuals, no freedom, no providence — and much of Leibniz's metaphysics is an attempt to escape it.

Spinoza had concluded that there is only **one substance**, God-or-Nature, of which all things are mere modes. Leibniz recoils from the consequence: if finite things are only modifications of one being, then individuals are not real, everything happens by blind necessity, and there is no room for freedom, moral responsibility, or a caring God.

His answer is the exact opposite extreme: not one substance but **infinitely many**. The world is composed of countless **monads** — simple, indivisible, mind-like substances, each a genuine individual, each a unique "perpetual living mirror of the universe." Where Spinoza dissolves individuals into the one, Leibniz multiplies them without limit, restoring the reality of distinct souls and things.

To keep these many substances from collapsing back into mechanism, Leibniz adds his famous devices: the monads are "windowless," never truly acting on one another, their inner states coordinated by a **pre-established harmony** that God set up at creation. And crucially, God chooses this world **freely**, as the best among infinitely many possible worlds — against Spinoza's God, who acts by sheer necessity with no choice and no alternatives.

The stakes are moral and religious as much as metaphysical. By replacing the one necessary substance with a plurality of freely created individuals in a world chosen as the best, Leibniz aims to save everything Spinoza's system destroyed: real individuals, contingency, freedom, and a providential, personal God. His whole system is, in part, the great anti-Spinoza.
