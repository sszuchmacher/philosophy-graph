Merleau-Ponty and Sartre were close allies — co-founders of the journal *Les Temps modernes*, comrades in postwar French thought — before politics and philosophy drove them apart. Their philosophical quarrel turns on the nature of **freedom** and the relation of consciousness to the body and the world.

Sartre's *Being and Nothingness* had drawn an absolute divide between **being-for-itself** (consciousness, a pure nothingness, wholly free) and **being-in-itself** (the dense, inert being of things). From this dualism followed his doctrine of *radical freedom*: consciousness is separated by a nothingness from everything given, so it is always and unconditionally free, the author of the meaning of its situation. Even in chains, Sartre insisted, one is free, because freedom lies in the consciousness that confers meaning.

Merleau-Ponty finds this dualism false and its conclusion untenable. The sharp Sartrean split between transparent consciousness and opaque thing leaves out precisely what he cares about most: the **body** and the ambiguous middle ground of perception. We are neither pure freedom nor mere thing but **embodied subjects**, woven into the world through perception and habit, and our freedom is therefore always *situated*, conditioned, and partial — never the absolute, all-or-nothing freedom Sartre proclaimed.

Against Sartre's vision of a consciousness that confers all meaning from a position of detachment, Merleau-Ponty insists that meaning arises in the *interworld* between body and things, that the world already solicits and shapes us before we choose, and that our sedimented habits, our bodily situation, and our history genuinely limit and orient our freedom. Freedom is real but never total; it works within a field it does not create from nothing.

The disagreement had political weight too: Sartre's all-or-nothing freedom and his later hardening into a certain Marxism struck Merleau-Ponty as abstract and even dangerous, blind to the ambiguity and contingency of real historical action.

So Merleau-Ponty's critique targets the Cartesian residue in Sartre — the pure, worldless, absolutely free consciousness set against inert being. In its place he sets the embodied, situated subject, whose freedom is genuine but always entangled with a body, a world, and a history. Existentialist freedom is corrected by the phenomenology of the flesh.
