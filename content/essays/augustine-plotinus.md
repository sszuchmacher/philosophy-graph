Augustine tells us in the *Confessions* that at the crisis of his life he read "certain books of the Platonists" — almost certainly Plotinus and Porphyry in Latin translation — and that they changed everything. They gave him the intellectual means to break free of the crude materialism that had held him, and to conceive God and the soul as immaterial, spiritual realities.

From Neoplatonism Augustine took a whole architecture. The Plotinian ascent — turning inward, away from the senses, then upward toward an immaterial source — becomes his own famous movement: "Do not go outward; return within yourself; in the inward man dwells truth." The One beyond being becomes the Christian God; the intelligible realm of Forms becomes ideas in the divine mind; the soul's homecoming becomes the restless heart finding rest in God.

Even his solution to the problem of evil is Plotinian: evil is not a substance or a rival power but a *privation*, a lack of good, a turning of the will from the higher toward the lower.

> I entered, and I saw with the eye of my soul the unchangeable Light.

But Augustine also marks the limit, and this is what makes it *baptism* rather than mere borrowing. The Platonists, he says, saw the homeland from afar but could not reach it, for they knew the Word but not the Word made *flesh*. They had the ascent but not the Incarnation, the goal but not the humility and grace that alone let a fallen soul arrive. Augustine takes the whole Neoplatonic ladder and grounds it in Christ — keeping the metaphysics, adding the one thing he says it fatally lacked.
