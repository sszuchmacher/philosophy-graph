Julia Kristeva developed her influential theory of language and subjectivity within the **Lacanian** framework — she was part of the Lacanian intellectual world in Paris — and her key concepts are, in large part, a creative extension of and supplement to Lacan.

From Lacan, Kristeva takes the account of the subject as constituted through entry into the **Symbolic order** — the realm of language, law, and the paternal function, structured by the signifier, into which the child passes and thereby becomes a speaking subject. She accepts the broad Lacanian picture: subjectivity is bound up with language, the subject is split and formed through its accession to the Symbolic, and the unconscious is structured in relation to the signifier.

But Kristeva's great addition is to insist on something Lacan's Symbolic-centered theory underplays: a dimension of signification *prior* to and beneath the Symbolic, which she calls the **semiotic**. The semiotic is the pre-linguistic, bodily, rhythmic, affective register — the drives, rhythms, and tones rooted in the infant's relation to the mother's body, before the acquisition of structured language and the entry into the paternal Symbolic. Where Lacan's account is organized around the paternal signifier, Kristeva foregrounds this **maternal**, bodily, rhythmic substratum.

Crucially, she argues that the semiotic does not simply vanish upon entry into the Symbolic; it persists and continues to erupt within language — as rhythm, intonation, wordplay, the musical and disruptive dimension of speech, most visibly in **poetic language**. Signification, for Kristeva, is the interplay of *both* registers: the structured, grammatical Symbolic *and* the disruptive, bodily semiotic that surges through and against it. The semiotic can subvert and renew the Symbolic order — which gives poetry and avant-garde art a revolutionary charge.

This lets Kristeva reintroduce the **body, affect, and the maternal** into a Lacanian theory that had been organized around the paternal signifier and often seemed to leave the body and the pre-Oedipal behind. She also develops, from within this framework, her theory of **abjection** — the horror at what threatens the boundaries of the self, rooted in the primal separation from the maternal body.

So Kristeva continues Lacan by working within his theory of the subject constituted through language and the Symbolic, while supplementing it with the **semiotic** — the pre-linguistic, bodily, maternal, rhythmic dimension that precedes and perpetually disrupts the Symbolic. Lacan gave her the split, language-constituted subject and the primacy of the signifier; Kristeva restored to that subject the maternal body, the drives, and the disruptive music of the semiotic, and made their interplay the very life of language and the source of poetic and psychic revolution.
