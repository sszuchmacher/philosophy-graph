Hannah Arendt's most surprising and original appropriation is her reading of **Kant** — not the Kant of the moral law or the theory of knowledge, but the Kant of the *Critique of Judgment*, from whom she drew, late in life, the outline of a political philosophy Kant himself never wrote.

Arendt was suspicious of the Kant of the categorical imperative. His moral philosophy, grounded in universal rules that each rational agent derives alone by reason, seemed to her too solitary, too abstract, and too indifferent to the concrete world of human plurality — even, she worried, dangerously close to the mentality of the functionary who does his "duty" regardless. Political life, for Arendt, is not about applying universal rules but about acting and judging among a plurality of distinct people in a shared public world.

So she turned to Kant's account of **aesthetic judgment** and looked there for the model of *political* judgment. In judging beauty, Kant had shown, we make claims that are neither merely subjective ("this pleases *me*") nor governed by a determinate rule — yet we claim they should hold for others, we "woo" their agreement, we judge with an "enlarged mentality" that takes account of the possible judgments of everyone else. Kant's judging spectator does not deduce from rules but reflects on particulars and appeals to a community of judgment (a *sensus communis*).

Arendt reinterprets this as the very structure of **political judgment**. Political thinking, she argues, means judging particulars — this event, this deed — without subsuming them under universal rules, by "representing" in one's imagination the standpoints of others, thinking from their positions, and so reaching a judgment that can claim a public, shared validity while respecting human plurality. This "enlarged mentality" is the faculty of the citizen, and it belongs above all to the **spectator** who judges the public spectacle, not only to the actor.

So Arendt reinterprets Kant by finding his unwritten political philosophy in the third *Critique* rather than the second. She takes his analysis of reflective aesthetic judgment — judging particulars without rules, with an enlarged mentality that solicits the agreement of a community — and transposes it into a theory of political judgment fit for a world of plural, distinct human beings. Against the solitary, rule-governed morality of the categorical imperative she sets a Kantian faculty of judgment that is public, imaginative, and plural — Kant's aesthetics remade as the missing account of how free people judge and act together in the common world.
