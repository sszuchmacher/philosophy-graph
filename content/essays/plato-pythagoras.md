Long before Plato, the Pythagoreans had made a strange and beautiful claim: that the ultimate nature of things is **number**. They had found that musical harmony is ratio — that concordant intervals correspond to simple whole-number relations of string length — and generalized boldly: the cosmos is ordered by number and proportion, and the soul is immortal, passing through many bodies.

Plato absorbs this current and transforms it. His **Forms** — Justice itself, Beauty itself, the Equal itself — are, like Pythagorean numbers, changeless, non-sensible objects grasped by intellect rather than sight, more real than the physical things that imitate them. Mathematics becomes the training ground of the philosopher precisely because it turns the mind from the visible toward the eternal; above the door of the Academy, tradition says, stood the warning that no one ignorant of geometry should enter.

The debt runs deep in the *Timaeus*, where the physical world is literally built from triangles and the elements assigned geometric solids — matter given mathematical structure. And the Pythagorean doctrine of the transmigrating, immortal soul becomes the backbone of Plato's arguments in the *Phaedo* and *Meno* that learning is really *recollection* of what the soul knew before birth.

Yet Plato is no mere disciple. Where the Pythagoreans said things *are* numbers, Plato inserts a distinction: sensible things *participate* in Forms, of which mathematical objects are an intermediate class. He keeps the Pythagorean intuition — that reality is intelligible structure, not stuff — while giving it the metaphysics of Forms. Number points the way to the Form; it is not the last word.
