Socrates wrote nothing. He spent his life in the Athenian marketplace asking people what they meant by justice, courage, piety — and showing, through relentless questioning, that they did not know. He left behind no doctrine, only a method, an example, and a death: executed by the city for corrupting the youth.

Everything we call Platonism grows from Plato's effort to carry that voice forward. Almost all his works are **dialogues**, and in nearly all of them the questioner is *Socrates*. The early dialogues seem close to the historical man: they end in *aporia*, impasse, with no definition secured — the pure Socratic elenchus, deflating false confidence.

But gradually the character changes. The Socrates of the *Republic* and *Phaedo* no longer merely refutes; he propounds a positive metaphysics — the theory of **Forms**, the immortality of the soul, the tripartite city and psyche. Scholars call this the *Socratic problem*: where does the master end and the pupil begin?

Plato never resolves it for us, and perhaps the ambiguity is deliberate. By speaking through Socrates, he lends his own boldest doctrines the authority of the man martyred for the examined life. The continuity is also a transformation: Socrates asked and unsettled; Plato answers and builds.

What remains constant is the conviction that gave Socrates his life and death — that the care of the soul, the search for genuine knowledge of the good, matters more than anything the city can give or take. Plato's whole system is an argument that this conviction was not naïve but true.
