Russell discovered Frege late — Frege's work had been almost entirely ignored — and became his great champion, carrying the German logician's revolutionary achievements into the English-speaking world and building on them the edifice of analytic philosophy.

From Frege, Russell took the new **mathematical logic** that Frege had invented in his *Begriffsschrift*: the logic of quantifiers and variables that finally superseded Aristotle's syllogistic and made it possible to analyze the structure of complex statements — "all," "some," relations, multiple generality — with precision. This logic is the indispensable tool of everything Russell did.

He also took Frege's **logicism** — the thesis that arithmetic can be derived from logic alone, that mathematics is, at bottom, logic. Russell embraced this program and, with Whitehead, devoted the monumental *Principia Mathematica* to carrying it out in detail. Where Frege had made the first great attempt, Russell and Whitehead built the vast formal system meant to complete it. He absorbed too Frege's **anti-psychologism** — that logic and meaning are objective, not facts about how we happen to think — and his concern with the analysis of *meaning* and *reference* as the route to clarity.

But the relationship is famous for a darker moment. Just as Frege was completing the second volume of his life's work grounding arithmetic in logic, Russell wrote to him with **Russell's paradox** — the contradiction in the set of all sets that are not members of themselves — which struck at the foundations of Frege's system. Frege replied with heartbreaking honesty that the ground had been cut from under his edifice. Russell's discovery both continued Frege's project and revealed its crisis; much of *Principia*'s complexity (the theory of types) is Russell's attempt to repair the damage.

So Russell continues Frege as the heir who inherited and propagated the logicist program and the new logic — making Frege's obscure genius the foundation of a whole tradition — while also being the one who exposed its deepest flaw and labored to rebuild it. Through Russell, Frege's revolution in logic became the shared method of twentieth-century analytic philosophy.
