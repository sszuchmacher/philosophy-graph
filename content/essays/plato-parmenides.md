From Parmenides, Plato inherited the most valuable and most dangerous idea in Greek philosophy: that true reality is not what the senses show but what thought grasps — something ungenerated, changeless, and fully itself. The **Forms** are Parmenidean through and through: eternal, unmoving, apprehended by reason alone.

But Parmenides had drawn a ruinous conclusion. If only changeless Being is real, then the many changing things simply *are not* — mere illusion — and Being is strictly *one*, since any distinction would require non-being to separate one thing from another. That leaves no room for a world, or for knowledge of it.

Plato's reinterpretation is to *pluralize* Eleatic Being. There is not one Form but many — a Form for justice, for beauty, for each kind — and sensible things are not sheer illusion but *copies* that participate in the Forms, real in a lesser, derivative way. Being comes in degrees.

The decisive break comes in the dialogue named for the old master. In the *Sophist*, the Eleatic Stranger confesses he must commit parricide: he must **contradict Father Parmenides** and show that non-being, in a sense, *is* — that *is not* can mean *is different from*, not *does not exist at all*. Only by admitting difference into the heart of Being can there be many Forms, and false statements, and a world to talk about.

So Plato keeps Parmenides's greatest gift — the intelligible, changeless object of knowledge — while killing the monism that made it barren. He turns the one frozen Being into a structured realm of many Forms, woven together by sameness and difference.
