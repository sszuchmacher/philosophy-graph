One of Derrida's most famous readings takes on Plato — and specifically the moment, in the dialogue *Phaedrus*, where Plato has Socrates condemn **writing**. This reading, "Plato's Pharmacy," is a model of deconstruction and a demonstration that the hierarchy privileging speech over writing goes back to the very origin of Western philosophy.

In the *Phaedrus*, Plato tells a myth in which writing is presented to a king as a gift — a *pharmakon* — and rejected. Writing, Socrates argues, is dangerous: it weakens memory, it is a mere lifeless image of living speech, it cannot defend itself or answer questions, it circulates apart from its "father" the author. Genuine knowledge lives in the **speech** of a present, thinking soul; writing is a secondary, derivative, even harmful copy of it. This, for Derrida, is the founding gesture of Western **phonocentrism** and the **metaphysics of presence**: truth is tied to the living presence of speech and the speaking subject, and writing is exiled as absence, death, and mere supplement.

Derrida's deconstruction turns on the word Plato uses: ***pharmakon***, which means both "**remedy**" and "**poison**" (and also "drug," "potion"). Plato needs to fix writing as poison, the harmful supplement — but the word he uses inescapably *also* means remedy, and Derrida shows that the text cannot control this doubleness. The very language in which Plato tries to subordinate writing undermines the neat opposition: writing is both cure and poison, and the boundary between speech and writing, memory and its mere image, will not hold. Reading closely, Derrida shows that Plato's own text depends on and is contaminated by what it tries to expel — that "living" speech already has the features (repetition, the sign, absence) attributed to "dead" writing.

The stakes go far beyond the *Phaedrus*. Derrida argues that this Platonic subordination of writing to speech inaugurates a hierarchy that structures the entire Western tradition — presence over absence, soul over body, origin over copy, the proper over the supplementary. To deconstruct Plato's condemnation of writing is to reach the root of the metaphysics of presence itself, and to show that "writing" in Derrida's enlarged sense (the trace, difference, the supplement) was there, disavowed, at the very beginning.

So Derrida's relation to Plato is a foundational **critique** that locates the target of deconstruction at the origin of philosophy. He does not refute Plato so much as read him against himself, showing through the double sense of *pharmakon* that Plato's attempt to privilege speech and expel writing is undone by his own text. The hierarchy Derrida spends his career unsettling is traced back to Plato, and the deconstruction of that first gesture becomes emblematic of the deconstruction of the whole tradition it founded.
