Deleuze was central to the revival of Bergson, who had fallen into deep neglect; his book *Bergsonism* and his method of reading made Bergson essential again, and Bergsonian concepts run through all his work — his philosophy of time, difference, and the virtual is unthinkable without them.

From Bergson, Deleuze takes first **duration** and the critique of spatialized thinking. Bergson had distinguished real, lived time — duration, a continuous, qualitative, ever-differentiating flow — from the homogeneous, spatialized time of science that chops becoming into static instants. Deleuze makes this central: reality is fundamentally temporal, a process of becoming and continuous differentiation, and the philosopher's task is to think in terms of duration and movement rather than fixed states and identities.

Most importantly, Deleuze draws from Bergson his crucial concept of the **virtual**. Bergson had distinguished the virtual from the actual — the virtual is fully real without being actual, a domain of tendencies and potentials that actualize themselves by *differentiating*, creating genuinely new forms rather than realizing pre-given possibilities. Deleuze seizes on this: the virtual/actual pair becomes the backbone of his metaphysics of difference. Against the tired opposition of the possible and the real (where the possible merely resembles a real that copies it), Deleuze offers the virtual and its actualization — a productive process that creates the new, differences that make a difference.

Deleuze also takes Bergson's method of **intuition** and his conception of **multiplicity** — Bergson's distinction between discrete, numerical multiplicities and continuous, qualitative ones, which Deleuze develops into his own theory of multiplicities as the true nature of things, in place of essences and unities.

So Deleuze continues Bergson by making duration, the virtual, and multiplicity load-bearing concepts of his philosophy of difference and becoming. He inherits the critique of spatialized time, the primacy of a differentiating temporal flow, and above all the virtual as the reality that actualizes itself in creative difference. Bergson gave Deleuze the tools to think becoming and novelty without falling back on fixed identities or the sterile logic of the possible — the temporal, differential ontology that, fused with Nietzsche's affirmation and Spinoza's immanence, makes Deleuze the great modern philosopher of creative becoming.
