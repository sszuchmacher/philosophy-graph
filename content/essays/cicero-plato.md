Cicero was not an original metaphysician and never pretended to be. His genius was as a transmitter: a Roman statesman who set out, in a torrent of dialogues, to carry Greek philosophy into Latin — and much of what he carried was Plato.

The debt is formal as well as doctrinal. Cicero writes *dialogues* in conscious imitation of Plato, and two of his most important — *On the Republic* and *On the Laws* — take their titles and themes from Plato's *Republic* and *Laws*, transposing the search for the just state and the best constitution into a Roman setting. Where Plato imagined an ideal city ruled by philosopher-kings, Cicero, a practical politician, looks to the actual Roman republic and its mixed constitution as justice made real.

He also inherits, through the skeptical Academy of his own day, Plato's method more than his fixed conclusions: the practice of arguing every question on both sides, weighing probabilities rather than claiming certainty. And he transmits Platonic themes that would prove immortal — the immortality of the soul in the *Dream of Scipio*, a natural law grounded in divine reason, the conviction that justice is real and not merely convention.

Cicero's importance is hard to overstate, precisely because he wrote in Latin. For centuries, before the Greek texts returned to the West, Europe knew Plato's world largely *through Cicero* — his vocabulary, his summaries, his dialogues. It was Cicero's lost *Hortensius* that would set the young Augustine on fire for philosophy. Plato thought in Greek; Cicero gave the Latin West the words to think those thoughts, and much of the ancient wisdom that outlived Rome outlived it in his prose.
