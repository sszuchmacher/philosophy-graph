Russell stood self-consciously in the tradition of British **empiricism**, and Hume was for him its greatest and most rigorous representative — the philosopher who followed the empiricist principles to their honest, unsettling conclusions. Much of Russell's epistemology is an attempt to carry Hume's project forward with the new tools of logic.

From Hume, Russell inherits the core empiricist commitment: that knowledge is grounded in **experience**, in sense-data and the immediate deliverances of perception, and that we must build our account of the world from these acquaintance-given foundations. His distinction between "knowledge by acquaintance" and "knowledge by description," and his attempt to construct physical objects as logical functions of sense-data, are a modern, logically sophisticated version of the empiricist program of tracing all knowledge back to what is given.

Above all, Russell took Hume's **problem of induction** with complete seriousness, and regarded it as unsolved. Hume had shown that our belief that the future will resemble the past cannot be justified by reason or experience without circularity — inductive inference has no rational foundation, resting only on habit. Russell agreed that this was a genuine and unanswered scandal at the heart of empirical knowledge and science. He restated it forcefully — the chicken fed every day until the day its neck is wrung — and confessed that empiricism could not, by its own resources, close the gap.

Russell was also drawn to Hume's **skeptical honesty** — the willingness to follow arguments to conclusions that offend common sense, to admit how little can be strictly proved about the self, causation, and the external world — and he shared Hume's naturalistic, irreligious temper.

But Russell brought to the empiricist tradition what Hume lacked: the **logical analysis** made possible by modern logic, which let him state the problems with new precision and attempt reconstructions (of matter, of the self) that Hume could only gesture at.

So Russell continues Hume as a rigorous modern empiricist — inheriting the grounding of knowledge in experience, the unsolved problem of induction, and the skeptical, naturalistic honesty — while equipping the old empiricism with the machinery of symbolic logic. Hume set the problems; Russell tried to attack them with sharper instruments than the eighteenth century possessed.
