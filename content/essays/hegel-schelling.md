Hegel and Schelling had been friends, roommates, and collaborators, jointly championing the Absolute against Fichte. Then, in the preface to the *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807), Hegel turned on his old ally with a single devastating image, and their friendship never recovered.

Schelling's "philosophy of identity" had proclaimed the Absolute as the point of pure **indifference** where all oppositions vanish — where subject and object, nature and spirit, are simply *one*, their differences dissolved. Hegel's objection is that this makes the Absolute a blank. To insist only that all is one, that in the Absolute every distinction disappears, is to offer an empty abstraction that explains nothing and lets us grasp nothing determinate.

> To pit this single assertion, that in the Absolute all is one, against the organized whole of knowledge... is to palm off its Absolute as the night in which, as one says, all cows are black.

The gibe is that Schelling's Absolute, in which every difference is blotted out, is like a darkness in which nothing can be distinguished — a featureless unity reached by a shot from a pistol rather than by patient labor.

Against this Hegel sets his central demand: the Absolute must be grasped not as bare identity but as a **process**, a self-developing whole that passes *through* difference and contradiction and reintegrates them. The true is the whole — but the whole only as the result of its own development, containing all its distinctions within it, not erasing them.

This is the birth of the fully **dialectical** conception. Where Schelling reached the Absolute by intuition, in one immediate leap to identity, Hegel insists on the long, mediated route: the Absolute realizes itself only through the entire movement of consciousness developing, negating, and sublating its oppositions. The critique of Schelling's featureless night is Hegel announcing his own method — and declaring German Idealism's culmination to be not intuition of unity but the systematic, dialectical labor of the concept.
