John Rawls revived political philosophy in the twentieth century, and he did so by reviving **Kant** — recasting the social contract tradition on Kantian foundations. Rawls called his own view "Kantian constructivism," and *A Theory of Justice* is, at its core, an attempt to give Kant's ethics a workable form for political philosophy.

From Kant, Rawls takes the foundational ideas of **autonomy**, the **equal dignity of persons**, and the priority of the **right over the good**. Kant had held that persons are ends in themselves, never to be used merely as means, and that moral principles are those a rational agent could give to itself as universal law — autonomy as self-legislation. Rawls builds his theory of justice on exactly this respect for persons as free and equal rational beings, and he shares Kant's conviction that principles of justice must be prior to and independent of any particular conception of the good life, so that a just society does not impose one vision of the good but secures fair terms among people who differ.

His famous devices reinterpret Kant's procedure. The **original position** and the **veil of ignorance** — in which we choose principles of justice without knowing our own place, talents, or conception of the good — are a modeling of Kant's **universality** and impartiality. By stripping away knowledge of our particular circumstances, the veil forces us to choose principles that respect everyone equally, that we could will for all — a proceduralized version of the categorical imperative's demand that we act on principles valid for every rational being.

But Rawls transforms Kant significantly. He "proceduralizes" Kant, replacing the abstract test of universalizability with a concrete choice-situation and the machinery of rational choice. He detaches Kantian autonomy from Kant's metaphysics — the noumenal self, the transcendental apparatus — recasting it in terms of the reasonable choices of free and equal citizens, and in his later work grounds justice not in a comprehensive Kantian moral philosophy but in a "political" conception citizens of different worldviews can share. And where Kant's ethics was individual, Rawls builds an institutional theory of the **basic structure** of society.

So Rawls reinterprets Kant by carrying the respect for persons as free and equal ends, the priority of the right, and the demand for universal, impartial principles into a systematic theory of social justice. The categorical imperative becomes the choice behind the veil of ignorance; Kantian autonomy becomes the fair agreement of free and equal citizens; and Kant's metaphysically freighted ethics becomes a constructive procedure for deriving principles of justice — Kant made political, and made to work.
