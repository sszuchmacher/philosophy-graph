Anselm had shown that reason could be turned, rigorously, upon the truths of faith. Abelard, a generation later, seized that method and pressed it with a boldness — and a combativeness — that made him the most brilliant and dangerous logician of his age.

Where Anselm sought necessary arguments in a spirit of prayer, Abelard makes **dialectic** — the art of logical analysis — the sovereign tool of theology, and wields it with a lawyer's aggression. His notorious *Sic et Non* ("Yes and No") lines up contradictory statements from the Church Fathers on scores of questions and leaves the contradictions exposed, forcing the reader to resolve them by careful distinction and analysis of terms. Authority alone will not settle matters; reason must.

> By doubting we come to inquiry, and by inquiry we perceive the truth.

That sentence marks how far the mood has shifted. For Anselm, understanding presupposes belief; for Abelard, systematic doubt becomes the engine of inquiry — not doubt *of* the faith, but the recognition that the tradition's own authorities disagree and only reason can adjudicate.

Abelard's genius was in language and logic. In the debate over universals he cut between the crude realism of his teachers and pure nominalism, arguing that universals are neither things nor mere words but the *significations* of words — an early conceptualism. In ethics he located sin in **intention** and consent rather than the outward deed.

The continuity with Anselm is real: both put reason at the service of faith, both trusted logic to illuminate doctrine. But Abelard sharpens the instrument to a point that alarmed the authorities — he was condemned twice — and in doing so he set the argumentative, question-and-distinction style that the great scholastic *summae* would perfect.
