Bergson does not often name Spinoza as a master, and in temperament they seem opposites — the geometric rationalist of the seventeenth century and the philosopher of intuition, life, and flowing time. Yet a deep current connects them: both are philosophers of a single, all-encompassing, active reality that expresses itself in everything, and Bergson's thought can be read as Spinoza's immanent, productive nature *set into time*.

From the Spinozist tradition Bergson inherits a vision of reality as **one continuous, generative whole** — not a collection of separate substances or dead mechanisms, but a single creative power immanent in all things, of which particular beings are expressions. Spinoza's *natura naturans*, nature-as-active-producing, prefigures Bergson's *élan vital*, the vital impetus that surges through all of life, differentiating itself into countless forms. Both reject a transcendent creator standing outside the world in favor of a creativity that works from within it.

But Bergson transforms Spinoza on the decisive point of **time**. Spinoza's substance is timeless, geometric, necessary — everything follows from God's nature with the eternal necessity of a mathematical theorem, and time is not fundamental but a confused perception of eternal relations. This is exactly what Bergson attacks in the whole rationalist tradition: the reduction of reality to timeless, spatialized structure. For Bergson, real time — **duration** (*durée*) — is the very heart of reality: an irreversible, creative flowing in which genuine novelty comes into being, unforeseeable and unrepeatable. The universe is not a completed system deducible from eternal principles but an ongoing creation.

So Bergson takes Spinoza's immanent, unified, productive reality and *temporalizes* it. Where Spinoza's one substance unfolds by timeless logical necessity, Bergson's one creative flow unfolds through lived duration, inventing new forms as it goes. The single active reality remains; the geometric eternity becomes living time.

The continuity, then, is the shared monism of an immanent creative power expressing itself in all things — Spinoza's *Deus sive Natura* reborn as the *élan vital*. Bergson's originality is to inject into that vision the reality of time and creative novelty that Spinoza's eternal, necessary substance had excluded, turning a static infinity into a living, evolving one.
