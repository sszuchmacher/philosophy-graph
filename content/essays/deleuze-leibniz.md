Deleuze's late book *The Fold: Leibniz and the Baroque* (1988) reclaims Leibniz — the arch-rationalist of pre-established harmony and the best of all possible worlds — as a philosopher of the **fold**, of infinite continuity and perspective, remaking him into an ally of Deleuze's own thought.

The organizing concept Deleuze draws out is the **fold** (*le pli*). Leibniz's world is one of infinite folding: matter is folded and refolded to infinity — never dividing into separate atoms but curving into ever-smaller pleats — and the soul, the monad, contains the whole world folded up within it, expressing it from its own point of view. Deleuze makes the fold a general figure — for the relation of soul and matter, inside and outside, the way the world is enveloped in each perceiving unit and unfolded in phenomena. It becomes a model for thinking continuity and the complication of the one and the many without dividing them into separate pieces.

Deleuze also prizes Leibniz's **perspectivism**. Each monad expresses the entire universe from a particular point of view, so there are infinitely many perspectives on one world, and the point of view is constitutive rather than secondary. Deleuze reads this as a philosophy of perspective and singularity that resonates with his own — though he pushes it further than Leibniz allowed.

For here the reinterpretation becomes a friendly subversion. Leibniz had insisted that all these perspectives converge on a single, harmonious, "compossible" world, chosen by God as the best — incompatible possibilities are excluded, and everything fits. Deleuze wants to **liberate the divergences**: he affirms "incompossibility" and divergent series *within* the world, a Baroque universe where incompatible perspectives and bifurcating paths are affirmed rather than excluded by a harmonizing God. He keeps Leibniz's infinite folding and perspectivism but removes the theological guarantee of convergence.

So Deleuze reinterprets Leibniz into a philosopher of the fold, of infinite continuity, and of constitutive perspective — mining the most systematic of rationalists for concepts (folding, envelopment, the point of view, the virtual world expressed in each monad) that serve a metaphysics of difference and multiplicity. He takes Leibniz's baroque infinity and keeps its folds and perspectives while cutting loose the divine harmony that held them together — turning the philosopher of the best of all possible worlds into a thinker of divergent, incompossible worlds affirmed all at once. It is Deleuze's characteristic gift: to find, in a canonical figure read against himself, a hidden ally for the philosophy of difference.
