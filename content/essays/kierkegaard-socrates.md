For all his war on the philosophical tradition, Kierkegaard revered one philosopher above all: **Socrates**. His first book was a dissertation *On the Concept of Irony, with Continual Reference to Socrates*, and he returned to the figure throughout his life, calling Socrates a kind of ally in the task of becoming a genuine individual.

What Kierkegaard prized was Socratic **irony** and the Socratic method — the refusal to lecture people into truth, and the use instead of indirect, questioning, unsettling communication that throws individuals back on themselves. Socrates claimed to know nothing and to teach nothing; he only questioned, exposing the emptiness of others' confident "knowledge" and forcing each person to confront their own ignorance. Kierkegaard adopts this as his own strategy of **indirect communication** — the pseudonyms, the irony, the refusal to hand over doctrine — because he holds, with Socrates, that the truths that matter most cannot be transmitted like information but must be personally, inwardly appropriated by each existing individual.

Socrates is also, for Kierkegaard, the great witness that **existence** and inwardness matter more than objective doctrine. Against the Hegelian professors who *have* a system, Socrates *is* a certain kind of person — one whose life and death embody the pursuit of the good, who cared infinitely about how he himself existed.

But there is a threshold Socrates cannot cross, and it defines the limit of the continuity. Socratic inwardness reaches the ethical and the recollection of eternal truth the soul already possesses. **Faith** is something more — and other. The Christian paradox, that the eternal God entered time in a particular man, is not a truth the individual can draw out of himself by recollection, as Socrates drew geometry from the slave boy; it must be *given*, received in a leap, believed against the understanding.

So Socrates stands, in Kierkegaard, at the very door of faith — the highest reach of human wisdom, the model of the ironic, questioning, existing individual — but he stands *outside* it. Kierkegaard takes Socratic irony and inwardness as far as they will go, and then insists that faith begins precisely where Socrates must stop.
