Jürgen Habermas was the heir of the Frankfurt School — Adorno's assistant and successor — who saved critical theory from what he saw as the dead end into which Adorno and Horkheimer had driven it. His whole enormous project is, in large part, a **critique** of his teachers' pessimism from within their own tradition.

The problem Habermas identifies is the **self-defeating** character of Adorno's *Dialectic of Enlightenment*. Adorno and Horkheimer had argued that reason itself — all of it — is at root **instrumental**, a tool of domination: the Enlightenment's rationality, in mastering nature, becomes a total system of control that swallows even the critical thought meant to oppose it. But this leaves critical theory in a trap. If *all* reason is domination, then the critique itself, being an exercise of reason, is also just domination — and there is no rational standpoint left from which to criticize, no ground for hope. Adorno's thought ends in an impasse: a totalizing critique that saws off the branch it sits on, retreating into negative dialectics and despair, unable to say why domination is *wrong* or how it might be overcome.

Habermas's diagnosis is that Adorno was working with an impoverished concept of reason. He had inherited a picture of reason as the relation of a lonely **subject** to objects it seeks to know and control — the "philosophy of the subject," subject-centered reason — which is indeed inherently oriented to mastery.

Habermas's solution is a **paradigm shift**: from subject-centered reason to **communicative reason**. Reason is not only, or fundamentally, the instrumental relation of a subject mastering objects; it is realized between subjects, in **communication** oriented toward mutual understanding. When people argue, justify claims, and seek rational agreement, they exercise a kind of reason that is not domination but its opposite — a cooperative reaching of understanding governed by the "unforced force of the better argument." Here critical theory finds the normative ground Adorno lacked: the structure of communication itself contains standards of rationality, freedom, and justice that let us criticize distorted, coerced forms of life.

So Habermas continues critical theory by rescuing it from Adorno's cul-de-sac. He accepts the critique of instrumental reason but denies that instrumental reason is all there is; by locating a second, communicative rationality between subjects, he restores the rational and normative foundation for social critique that his teachers had dissolved. Where Adorno saw only the darkening of reason into domination, Habermas finds, in the everyday practice of seeking understanding, an emancipatory reason on which a reconstructed critical theory can stand.
