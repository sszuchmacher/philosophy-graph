Derrida's relationship to Levinas is one of profound, if complex, discipleship. His long early essay "Violence and Metaphysics" was a searching critique of Levinas — but a critique that drew Derrida ever deeper into Levinas's orbit, and his later work on ethics, hospitality, and responsibility is unmistakably Levinasian. Between them developed a rare philosophical friendship, and Derrida delivered a moving eulogy at Levinas's death.

From Levinas, Derrida takes above all the primacy of the **relation to the Other** and the critique of the Western philosophical drive toward **totality** and the reduction of the other to the same. Levinas had argued that Western ontology, from the Greeks on, seeks to comprehend, grasp, and assimilate everything to the knowing subject — reducing the genuinely *other* to a theme, an object, a moment of the same — and that this comprehension is a kind of "violence." Against it he set the ethical relation to the Other, whose face exceeds all comprehension and commands an infinite responsibility that precedes and grounds knowledge. Ethics, not ontology, is first philosophy.

This resonates powerfully with Derrida's own suspicion of totalizing systems, of the metaphysics of presence, and of philosophy's ambition to master and make present. Levinas's **infinity** — the other that overflows every concept — is close kin to Derrida's insistence on what escapes the system, the excess no structure can contain. Derrida's later ethical-political writings on **hospitality, the gift, forgiveness, and justice** are deeply indebted to Levinas: the idea of an unconditional openness to the absolutely other, of a responsibility that is infinite and cannot be discharged, of justice as that which exceeds every calculable law.

But the continuity passes through critique. In "Violence and Metaphysics" Derrida pressed hard questions: whether Levinas can articulate the absolute Other *at all* without using the language of the very Greek ontology (Being, the same, presence) he tries to escape; whether a pure ethics free of ontology and totality is possible, or whether some "violence" of language and comprehension is inescapable in any speech about the Other. Derrida suggested that Levinas cannot simply step outside the tradition — that even the appeal to the Other must negotiate with the metaphysics it opposes.

So Derrida continues Levinas as a critical heir whose objections deepened rather than dissolved the debt. He absorbs Levinas's ethics of the Other, the critique of totality, the priority of responsibility and the encounter with what exceeds comprehension — and makes them central to the ethical turn of his later thought — while insisting, in deconstructive fashion, that this ethics can never fully purify itself of the language and "violence" of the tradition. Levinas gave Derrida the Other; Derrida gave that ethics a deconstructive conscience.
