Spinoza was steeped, from his Amsterdam education, in the medieval Jewish philosophical tradition, and Maimonides towered over it. The *Guide for the Perplexed* had done for Judaism what Aquinas did for Christianity — reconciling Aristotelian reason with revealed scripture. Spinoza inherits Maimonides's rationalism and then turns it against the very revelation Maimonides sought to defend.

The debt is deep. From Maimonides, Spinoza takes the drive to purify the idea of God of all anthropomorphism — no body, no passions, no human traits — and the conviction that scripture's crude images must yield to philosophical truth. He takes the method of reading the Bible for its rational meaning rather than its literal surface. And he takes the intellectual love of God as the summit of human life.

But where Maimonides had used allegory to *save* the harmony of Torah and reason — always stopping short of conclusions that would break with the faith — Spinoza removes the brakes. If God has no human attributes, Spinoza asks, why suppose God is a person who wills, commands, chooses a people, or works miracles at all? He follows the logic of negative theology to its end: God is not a transcendent lawgiver but the immanent, necessary order of nature itself, *Deus sive Natura*.

And in his *Theological-Political Treatise*, Spinoza turns Maimonides's careful scriptural interpretation into the first thoroughgoing **historical criticism of the Bible** — reading it as a human document, composed and edited over time, whose authority is political and moral, not a source of metaphysical truth.

So the continuity is a radicalization. Maimonides used reason to interpret revelation; Spinoza uses the same rationalist tools to dissolve revelation's special authority altogether. The medieval attempt to harmonize Athens and Jerusalem becomes, in his hands, the modern subordination of Jerusalem to Athens — and the beginning of secular biblical scholarship and the critique of religion.
