Adorno kept a wary distance from Nietzsche's politics and his appropriation by the right, yet Nietzsche was one of the deepest presences in his thought — a fellow master of the aphorism, of suspicion, and of the merciless critique of morality and culture. In *Dialectic of Enlightenment* and *Minima Moralia*, Nietzsche's influence runs everywhere.

The core continuity is the practice of **genealogy and the critique of morality and reason**. Nietzsche had taught that ideals — truth, morality, altruism, the ascetic denial of the self — are not what they claim to be, that they have a hidden history in power and resentment, and that Enlightenment and Christian morality mask instincts they will not avow. Adorno takes over this **hermeneutics of suspicion** and this refusal to take culture's self-image at face value. His critique of bourgeois morality, of the culture industry, and above all his thesis that **Enlightenment reason itself turns into domination** is profoundly Nietzschean: Nietzsche had already seen that the drive to knowledge and the moral will to truth conceal a will to mastery, and that civilization exacts a terrible cost in the repression of instinct.

Adorno also shares Nietzsche's **stylistic** commitments — the fragment and the aphorism as forms that resist the false totality of the system, the conviction that the closed system lies by its very form. *Minima Moralia*'s aphoristic "reflections from damaged life" are unthinkable without *Twilight of the Idols* and *The Gay Science*.

But Adorno reads Nietzsche **through Marx and against Nietzsche's own conclusions**. Where Nietzsche diagnosed the sickness of modern culture and prescribed an aristocratic self-overcoming — the affirmation of hierarchy and the strong — Adorno turns the same diagnostic power toward emancipation and solidarity with the suffering. He keeps Nietzsche's unmasking of reason-as-domination while refusing Nietzsche's contempt for the weak; the point, for Adorno, is not to affirm domination more honestly but to criticize it more radically.

So Adorno continues Nietzsche as an indispensable ancestor of the critique of Enlightenment, morality, and culture — inheriting the genealogical suspicion, the insight that reason harbors domination, and the aphoristic resistance to system — while redirecting it. Nietzsche's dynamite is defused of its aristocratic, anti-egalitarian charge and reloaded in the service of a critical theory that sides with the victims of the domination Nietzsche was content to expose.
