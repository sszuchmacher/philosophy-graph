Mill was raised to be Bentham's heir. His father, James Mill, was Bentham's closest disciple, and the young John Stuart was subjected to a rigorous utilitarian education designed to make him the intellectual champion of the cause. He inherited, and never abandoned, the core Benthamite creed: that the rightness of actions and institutions is measured by their tendency to promote **happiness** — the greatest happiness of the greatest number.

But Mill's mental breakdown at twenty, and his recovery through poetry and feeling, convinced him that Bentham's utilitarianism, as it stood, was too crude to be livable — and much of his work enriches it from within while keeping its foundation.

His most famous revision concerns the **quality of pleasures**. Bentham had treated all pleasures as commensurable, differing only in quantity — intensity, duration, and the like — so that, notoriously, a simple game could be as valuable as poetry if it produced as much pleasure. Mill rebels. Pleasures differ in **kind**, not just amount; the pleasures of the intellect, the imagination, and the moral sentiments are intrinsically **higher** than those of mere sensation. His test is the verdict of those who have experienced both:

> It is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied; better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.

This introduces a qualitative, even aristocratic, standard into the democratic calculus of pleasure — one Bentham's arithmetic could not accommodate.

Mill also deepens the doctrine in other ways: he ties utility to human dignity and self-development, gives sympathy and the sense of unity with others a central role in the "sanction" of morality, and, in *On Liberty*, builds protections for individuality and freedom that a crude sum-of-pleasures utilitarianism might override.

So Mill is the loyal heir who saves utilitarianism by refining it. He keeps Bentham's foundation — happiness as the standard, consequences as the measure — while answering the charge that it is a "doctrine worthy only of swine." The higher pleasures, human dignity, and liberty are grafted onto the Benthamite root, turning a reforming calculus into a humane liberal philosophy.
