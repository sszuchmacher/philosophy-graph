For a thinker so critical of the Western tradition, Levinas reserved a surprising and crucial role for Descartes — not for the *cogito* but for one neglected moment in the *Meditations*: Descartes's **idea of the infinite**.

In the Third Meditation, Descartes had argued that he finds within himself an idea of God as infinite and perfect — and that this idea could not have been produced by his own finite mind, since the finite cannot generate the infinite. The idea of the infinite must therefore have been placed in him by an infinite being that exceeds him. The container is smaller than what it contains.

Levinas seizes on this structure as the formal key to his whole ethics. What fascinates him is a relation in which the mind holds an idea whose object *overflows* it — an "idea of the infinite" that cannot be reduced to or encompassed by the thinking that has it. This is exactly, he argues, the structure of the encounter with the **Other**. The face of the other person presents an infinity I cannot contain, comprehend, or reduce to my own thought — like Descartes's infinite, it exceeds the finite subject and comes to me from beyond. The other's ethical claim "does not fit" inside my consciousness.

But Levinas **reinterprets** Descartes decisively. Descartes used the idea of the infinite as a step in a *proof of God*, then hurried on to secure knowledge of the external world. Levinas keeps the formal relation — a thought that contains more than it can contain — and relocates the "infinite" from God-as-object-of-proof to the **ethical** transcendence of the Other. The infinite is not an object I demonstrate but the face that summons me to responsibility. Descartes's epistemological curiosity becomes the structure of ethics as first philosophy.

So Levinas retrieves from Descartes a discarded jewel and resets it. The Cartesian idea of the infinite — the finite mind's relation to what surpasses it — becomes the model for the human being's relation to the Other, whose face means more than any concept can hold. Descartes glimpsed the formal structure of transcendence while proving God; Levinas makes that structure the very heart of the ethical relation.
