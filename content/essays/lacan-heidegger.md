Less famous than his debts to Saussure and Hegel, Lacan's engagement with **Heidegger** is nonetheless real — Lacan met Heidegger, translated a short text of his, and wove Heideggerian themes into his account of speech, truth, and the being of the subject.

From Heidegger, Lacan draws above all a certain conception of **language and truth**. Heidegger had understood language not as a mere instrument that a subject uses, but as the "house of Being," the medium in which world and truth are disclosed — and he understood truth as *alētheia*, un-concealment, a revealing that is always also a concealing. Lacan echoes this in his insistence that **speech** is not simply the tool of a pre-existing subject but the very element in which the subject and its truth come to be — that the subject is constituted in and by language (the Symbolic), and that the truth of the subject, like Heideggerian truth, emerges in a speech that simultaneously reveals and hides, especially the "full speech" of analysis in which desire is disclosed.

Lacan also takes from Heidegger a way of thinking about **being and lack, death and finitude**. The Heideggerian themes of being-toward-death, of anxiety, and of the nothingness at the heart of Dasein resonate with Lacan's account of the subject as constituted around a lack, a want-of-being (*manque-à-être*), oriented by a relation to death and absence.

And Lacan shares Heidegger's **de-centering of the Cartesian subject** — the rejection of the self-transparent, self-grounding ego of modern philosophy. Just as Heidegger dissolved the subject into Dasein's being-in-the-world, Lacan dissolves the ego (which he treats as an imaginary construction, a site of misrecognition) in favor of a subject that is "spoken" by the unconscious and the Symbolic, decentered from any sovereign self-consciousness. Lacan's rewriting of Descartes — "I think where I am not, therefore I am where I do not think" — is a Heideggerian-flavored subversion of the cogito.

But Lacan bends Heidegger to psychoanalytic ends and remains suspicious of Heidegger's own pathos of Being. The Heideggerian vocabulary of speech, truth as unconcealment, lack, and finitude is put in the service of a theory of desire and the unconscious that has no equivalent in Heidegger.

So Lacan continues Heidegger by borrowing his conceptions of language as the site of truth's disclosure, of the subject's constitution in speech, and of being founded on lack and finitude — and by joining Heidegger's assault on the sovereign Cartesian subject. He fuses these with Saussure and Hegel into his own account of the decentered, desiring, language-borne subject, making Heidegger one of the philosophical pillars of a psychoanalysis of the speaking being.
