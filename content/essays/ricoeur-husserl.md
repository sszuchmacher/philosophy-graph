Paul Ricoeur began as a phenomenologist in Husserl's line — he translated Husserl's *Ideas* into French while a prisoner of war — and phenomenology remained the ground of his lifelong project, even as he transformed it into a hermeneutics.

From Husserl, Ricoeur took the method of careful **descriptive analysis** of experience and the concept of **intentionality** — consciousness as directed toward meaning. His early work on the will and human action is straightforward phenomenology, describing the structures of willing, deciding, and acting with Husserlian rigor. Throughout his career he retained the phenomenological attention to meaning, to the first-person structures of experience, and to the constituting activity of consciousness.

But Ricoeur came to believe that phenomenology, by itself, is **insufficient** — that consciousness is not transparent to itself in the way Husserl's project of pure description assumed. We do not have immediate, self-evident access to ourselves and our meanings; we understand ourselves only **indirectly**, through the detour of **interpretation** — through the symbols, texts, myths, metaphors, and narratives in which human meaning is deposited. This is his great move: **grafting hermeneutics onto phenomenology**. Phenomenology describes the structures of meaning; hermeneutics is needed because those meanings reach us only through signs that must be interpreted, and because the self is not given directly but must be recovered through the works in which it expresses itself.

So Ricoeur's watchword is that there is no self-understanding that is not mediated by signs, symbols, and texts. The Husserlian dream of a self-transparent consciousness giving itself its own meanings directly is replaced by a "wounded cogito" that knows itself only by interpreting the cultural world it has produced and inhabits.

So Ricoeur continues Husserl by keeping phenomenological description and the concern with meaning as his foundation, while correcting Husserl's ideal of transparent self-consciousness. He turns phenomenology into **hermeneutic phenomenology**: consciousness still intends meaning, but it must reach itself and its world by the long road of interpreting symbols and texts. Husserl's direct intuition of essences gives way to Ricoeur's patient interpretation of the signs in which the human spirit has left its traces — phenomenology completed, as Ricoeur saw it, by hermeneutics.
