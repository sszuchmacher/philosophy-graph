Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of the body draws as much on Heidegger as on Husserl, taking Heidegger's account of human existence as practical, worldly engagement and giving it the concrete, embodied grounding Heidegger himself had left strangely bloodless.

From Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty takes the foundational idea of **being-in-the-world**: that human existence is not a self-enclosed consciousness confronting an external world, but is always already *out there*, absorbed and engaged in a meaningful world of tasks and things. Heidegger had shown that we understand the world first through *practical involvement* — the hammer used, ready-to-hand — rather than detached theoretical contemplation, and that Dasein is defined by its projects and its situation. This anti-Cartesian picture, dissolving the subject/object split into engaged existence, is Merleau-Ponty's constant background.

But Merleau-Ponty makes concrete and corporeal what remains oddly abstract in Heidegger. For all his talk of practical dealings and being-in-the-world, Heidegger says remarkably little about the **body** — Dasein handles tools, but its flesh, perception, and sensory life go almost unmentioned. Merleau-Ponty fills exactly this gap. Being-in-the-world, he insists, is *bodily* being-in-the-world: it is through our perceiving, moving, skillful bodies that we are engaged with things and situated in a world. The practical understanding Heidegger described is realized as bodily know-how, motor intentionality, the grip our bodies have on their surroundings.

He thus grounds the Heideggerian analysis in perception and embodiment. Where Heidegger described the structures of Dasein's engagement in fairly formal ontological terms, Merleau-Ponty gives a rich phenomenology of how an incarnate subject actually inhabits space, grasps objects, and perceives a world — supplying the flesh that Heidegger's existential analytic lacked.

So the continuity is a corporeal completion. Merleau-Ponty takes Heidegger's being-in-the-world and engaged existence and shows that its true subject is the living body. He keeps the overcoming of the Cartesian split and the primacy of worldly existence, while insisting that this engagement is first of all bodily perception — turning Heidegger's ontology of care into a phenomenology of the flesh.
