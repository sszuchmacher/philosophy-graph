Rawls placed himself explicitly in the tradition of the **social contract**, naming Locke, Rousseau, and Kant as his forebears. From Locke in particular he inherited the framework of contractarian liberalism and several of its core commitments, even as he rebuilt the contract on new foundations.

From Locke, Rawls takes the basic **contractarian method**: the idea that the legitimacy of social arrangements is to be understood by asking what free and equal persons would **agree** to. Legitimate institutions rest on a kind of consent among those subject to them, rather than on divine right, tradition, or brute power. Rawls's original position — the hypothetical situation in which principles of justice are chosen by agreement — is a descendant of Locke's state of nature and social contract, a device for modeling what free and equal people could rationally accept.

He also inherits Locke's **liberalism**: the primacy of individual liberty and basic rights, the insistence that government exists to serve the governed and is constrained by principles it may not violate, and the protection of a sphere of individual freedom against the state. Rawls's first principle of justice — the priority of equal basic liberties for all — is squarely in the Lockean liberal tradition.

But Rawls transforms the contract decisively. Locke's contract was quasi-historical, grounded in natural law and natural rights persons possess prior to agreement, with a special emphasis on **property**. Rawls's contract is frankly **hypothetical** — a thought-experiment (the original position behind the veil of ignorance) designed to model fairness, not an account of actual consent. And Rawls departs sharply from the Lockean stress on property rights toward **distributive justice**: his difference principle permits inequalities only if they benefit the least advantaged, a strongly egalitarian conclusion Locke's property-centered liberalism would not reach. He grounds the whole in Kantian ideas of the person rather than Locke's natural law and theology.

So Rawls continues Locke as an heir of the social-contract tradition — the legitimacy of institutions through the agreement of free and equal persons, and the liberal priority of individual liberty and rights. He inherits the contractarian method and the defense of basic liberties while transforming the contract into a hypothetical device for modeling fairness and reorienting liberalism from the protection of property toward distributive justice. Locke gave him the contract and the priority of liberty; Rawls made the contract a test of fairness and turned liberalism egalitarian.
