Mary Wollstonecraft admired Rousseau intensely — his passion, his cult of nature and feeling, his vision of authentic human freedom. Which made his treatment of women all the more infuriating, and her *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* is in large part a sustained assault on it.

The target is Rousseau's *Emile*, his great treatise on education. For the boy Emile, Rousseau prescribes an education for **autonomy** — the cultivation of independent reason, judgment, and self-command, so that he may become a free man. But in the book's fifth part he invents **Sophie**, Emile's intended wife, and prescribes for her the exact opposite. Woman, Rousseau declares, is made to please and to be subordinate to man; Sophie is to be trained in charm, modesty, docility, and cunning, her reason kept weak and her whole being shaped to depend on and gratify her husband. Her virtue is to be merely relative to his.

Wollstonecraft's fury is directed at the **inconsistency**. Rousseau's own principles proclaim that virtue must be founded on reason, that dependence corrupts, and that no one truly flourishes in servitude — yet he condemns half the human race to precisely the dependence and unreason he denounces in men. If reason is the ground of virtue and freedom, she argues, then it is a *human* faculty, not a male one; women have it too, and to deny them its cultivation is to deform them.

The apparent weakness and vanity of women, she insists, is not natural but *manufactured* — the predictable result of an education that trains them to be pleasing objects rather than rational beings. Give women the education for autonomy Rousseau reserves for Emile, and their supposed frivolity would vanish.

So Wollstonecraft wields Rousseau against Rousseau. She accepts his premises — the primacy of reason, the corruption of dependence, education for freedom — and simply refuses to exempt women from them. The critique is a founding text of modern feminism: it takes the Enlightenment's universal claims about reason and rights and insists, against one of its own heroes, that they must actually be *universal*.
