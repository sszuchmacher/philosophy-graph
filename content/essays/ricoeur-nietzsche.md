Ricoeur is not usually thought of as a Nietzschean, yet one of his most influential coinages captures a debt to Nietzsche: the "**hermeneutics of suspicion**," his name for the interpretive strategy shared by the three great "masters of suspicion" — **Marx, Nietzsche, and Freud**.

In his study of Freud, Ricoeur distinguished two fundamental attitudes in interpretation. One is a **hermeneutics of trust** (or restoration) — the interpretation that approaches a text or symbol as bearing a meaning to be received, recovered, and heard, as one might interpret sacred scripture, listening for a message. The other is a **hermeneutics of suspicion** — the interpretation that treats the surface meaning as a *disguise*, a mask concealing something the text does not avow, and that seeks to *unmask* it, to expose the hidden reality behind the illusion.

Nietzsche is a supreme practitioner of this suspicious reading. His **genealogy** treats moral values, truth, and religion not as what they claim to be but as symptoms — expressions of hidden forces (the will to power, resentment, weakness) that the conscious mind conceals from itself. To interpret, for Nietzsche, is to see through: behind "morality" lies domination or *ressentiment*; behind "truth" lies a will to power. Marx unmasks ideology as class interest; Freud unmasks the dream as disguised desire; Nietzsche unmasks values as the cunning of the will. All three teach us to distrust the immediate consciousness and read it as a cipher.

Ricoeur takes this seriously and incorporates it — the hermeneutics of suspicion is, for him, an indispensable achievement, a permanent lesson that consciousness is not transparent and that we must interpret against the grain of what a text says of itself. But he refuses to let suspicion be the *whole* of interpretation. His own project seeks to hold suspicion and trust together in tension: the unmasking of false consciousness *and* the restorative listening for meaning, demolition *and* the recovery of a possible world the text opens.

So Ricoeur continues Nietzsche by naming and honoring his method — the genealogical unmasking of consciousness as disguise — and making it one of the two poles of modern interpretation. He inherits Nietzsche's great insight that meaning is often a mask to be seen through, and grants it a permanent place in hermeneutics — while insisting that suspicion must be completed by trust, the demolition of illusions by the patient recovery of the truths that symbols and texts still have to give.
