Russell began his philosophical life as an idealist. In the 1890s British philosophy was dominated by **Hegelianism** — the absolute idealism of Bradley and McTaggart — and the young Russell, like his friend Moore, was raised in it: reality is a single spiritual Absolute, relations are "internal" (every thing is constituted by its relations to everything else), and the world of separate, independent things is mere appearance.

Around 1898, Russell and Moore staged a **revolt** against this idealism that founded the analytic tradition, and Russell's rebellion was as much logical as doctrinal. He came to see Hegelian idealism as a vast, vague, mystical edifice built on bad logic — and he traced its errors to a specific technical mistake about **relations**.

The idealists held that all relations are "internal" — grounded in the natures of their terms — which leads, by a chain of reasoning, to monism: if everything is constituted by its relations to everything else, then nothing is truly separate and reality is one indivisible whole. Russell argues that relations are **external**: "a is to the left of b" is a genuine, irreducible relation between two independent things, not grounded in some inner nature or in the Absolute. Once relations are external, the monistic edifice collapses. Reality is a plurality of independent things standing in real relations — a world that can be *analyzed* into its constituents rather than dissolved into an indivisible whole.

This licenses the method that defines Russell's philosophy: **analysis** — breaking complex wholes down into their simpler parts and logical structure. Where Hegelian idealism said the truth is the whole and analysis falsifies by dividing, Russell says the way to knowledge is precisely to divide, to lay bare the atomic constituents and their relations. Logical atomism is the direct antithesis of Hegelian holism.

So Russell's relation to Hegel is a founding **inversion**. He was formed by Hegelian idealism and defined analytic philosophy by overturning it — replacing monism with pluralism, internal relations with external, the mystical Absolute with a world of distinct facts, and the holism that scorned analysis with a method that made analysis the whole of philosophical work. The twentieth-century analytic tradition begins as the negation of Hegel.
