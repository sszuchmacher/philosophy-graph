Deleuze called Spinoza "the Christ of philosophers" and himself, half-seriously, a Spinozist. Spinoza is, with Nietzsche, the deepest source of his thought, and his two books on Spinoza reinterpret him into a philosopher of **immanence, affects, and life**.

From Spinoza, Deleuze takes above all the plane of **immanence**. Spinoza's single substance — God-or-Nature, of which all things are modes, with nothing transcendent beyond it — is for Deleuze the supreme achievement of Western philosophy: a world with no transcendent creator, no beyond, no hierarchy of eminent causes, in which everything is an expression of one immanent, productive reality. Deleuze makes "immanence" the banner of his whole philosophy, and Spinoza the thinker who pushed it furthest.

He reinterprets Spinoza's **modes** and **expression** as a dynamic ontology: the one substance *expresses* itself in infinite modes, and Deleuze reads this expressive relation as productive and differential — being as expression, univocal (said in one and the same sense of everything) yet endlessly differentiated. Spinoza's univocity of being becomes a pillar of Deleuze's metaphysics of difference.

Most influentially, Deleuze foregrounds Spinoza's theory of the **affects** and the body. Spinoza had asked what a body *can do* — defining things by their powers to affect and be affected rather than by fixed essences — and mapped how encounters increase or decrease a body's power of acting (joy and sadness). Deleuze turns this into an "ethology," an ethics of the affects and of increasing one's power to act, and a model of thinking bodies (and everything else) in terms of capacities, intensities, and relations rather than substances and identities. Spinoza's *Ethics* becomes a practical philosophy of joyful encounters and the composition of powers.

But this is bold **reinterpretation**. Deleuze downplays Spinoza's geometric rationalism and rigid necessitarianism, reading him instead through the lens of expression, affect, intensity, and life — closer to Nietzsche than to seventeenth-century rationalism. Where Spinoza's substance was static and deductive, Deleuze's Spinoza is dynamic and productive, a philosophy of the powers of existence.

So Deleuze reinterprets Spinoza into the founding philosopher of immanence and the affects. He takes the single substance and its expressive modes, the univocity of being, and the ethics of what a body can do, and rebuilds them as a vital, dynamic metaphysics of difference and power — Spinoza's God-or-Nature reborn as a plane of immanence traversed by intensities and affects. With Nietzsche's affirmation and Bergson's duration, Spinoza's immanence forms the third great pillar of Deleuze's thought.
