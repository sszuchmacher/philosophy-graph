Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* was written, above all, against **utilitarianism** — the tradition of Bentham and Mill that had dominated Anglophone moral and political thought. Rawls's central ambition was to provide a systematic alternative that could defeat utilitarianism on its own ground, and much of the book is a sustained critique of the Millian view.

Utilitarianism holds that the right social arrangement is the one that maximizes the **aggregate** welfare — the greatest total (or average) happiness across society. Rawls's fundamental objection is that this **does not take seriously the distinctness of persons**. By summing satisfactions across individuals and seeking the greatest total, utilitarianism treats society as if it were a single person maximizing its own good, and is willing, in principle, to sacrifice the interests of some for greater gains to others — to accept the suffering or subordination of a minority if it increases the overall sum. It has no principled barrier against injustice to individuals, so long as the totals come out ahead. Persons, for Rawls, are separate and inviolable, each with a claim the general welfare cannot override.

Against this he sets **justice as fairness**, with its lexically prior first principle guaranteeing **equal basic liberties** that cannot be traded away for greater aggregate welfare, and its **difference principle**, which permits inequalities only if they benefit the *least advantaged*. Rawls argues that parties in the original position, choosing behind the veil of ignorance and not knowing whether they might be the ones sacrificed, would **reject** utilitarianism precisely because it would license their own subordination for the greater good; they would instead choose principles that protect everyone's basic liberties and improve the position of the worst-off.

Rawls's quarrel is not with Mill's liberalism — he shares Mill's commitment to liberty and to the worth of individuals — but with the utilitarian **foundation**. Indeed Rawls thought Mill's own deepest liberal commitments could not be securely grounded in the utilitarian principle, which might, on the numbers, permit their violation.

So Rawls's relation to Mill is a foundational critique from a fellow liberal. He rejects the utilitarian aggregation Mill inherited from Bentham, charging that it ignores the separateness of persons and licenses the sacrifice of some for the greater total, and replaces it with principles — equal liberty and the difference principle — that give each person inviolable claims. Where utilitarianism asks how to maximize total welfare, Rawls asks what free and equal persons could fairly agree to, and builds a theory designed to protect individuals against the very sacrifices utilitarianism would permit.
