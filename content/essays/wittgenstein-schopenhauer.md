Beneath the logical surface of the *Tractatus* lies a current few first readers expected: **Schopenhauer**. As a youth Wittgenstein had been deeply impressed by *The World as Will and Representation*, and its influence surfaces precisely where the *Tractatus* turns from logic to ethics, the self, and the mystical.

The book's early parts give a rigorous logical account of how language pictures the world of facts. But at its close Wittgenstein makes a move that is Schopenhauerian in spirit. He distinguishes the **world** — the totality of facts, everything that can be said — from the **value, meaning, and the mystical**, which lie *outside* the world and cannot be put into factual language. Ethics, the meaning of life, the will as bearer of good and evil — these cannot be *said*; they can only be *shown*, and about them "one must be silent."

This maps onto Schopenhauer's division of world as **representation** (the realm of appearance, of subject and object, that we can describe) and world as **will** (the deeper reality behind it). Wittgenstein's world of facts corresponds to representation; what lies beyond, the ethical and the mystical, echoes the Schopenhauerian beyond that objective knowledge cannot reach. Even his remarks on the **self** are Schopenhauerian: the "I" is not an object *in* the world but a limit of the world, the metaphysical subject for which the world is "my world" — Schopenhauer's subject that is presupposed by all representation but never found within it.

And the ethical attitude the *Tractatus* gestures toward — that the will cannot change the facts of the world, only one's stance toward it, so that the happy and the unhappy live in "different worlds" — has the flavor of Schopenhauer's resignation and his priority of the will over circumstance.

So Wittgenstein continues Schopenhauer in the unexpected register of a logical treatise's final pages. He fuses the new logic of Frege and Russell with a Schopenhauerian metaphysics of the limits of the world — keeping the distinction between the sayable realm of facts and an inexpressible beyond of value, will, and the mystical, and locating the self at the world's edge rather than within it. The *Tractatus* is, among other things, Schopenhauer rewritten in the language of modern logic.
