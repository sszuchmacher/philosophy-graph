Epicurus took his physics almost wholesale from Democritus: the universe is nothing but **atoms and void**, and everything — the soul included — is a temporary configuration of particles. He was not shy about the debt, though he quarreled over details. But he put the old materialism to a wholly new use.

For Democritus, atomism was an account of *what is*: a cool, disinterested picture of a purposeless world. For Epicurus it is a *therapy*. The point of knowing that all is atoms and void is to be cured of fear — above all the two great fears that poison human life: fear of the gods and fear of death.

If the world is atoms colliding by natural law, the gods do not manage it with thunderbolts and punishments; they dwell serenely apart and take no interest in us. And if the soul is itself atomic, then at death it simply disperses. There is no afterlife to dread, no one left to suffer. **Death is nothing to us**: where we are, death is not; where death is, we are not.

Epicurus even adjusts the physics to make room for freedom. Against Democritus's strict determinism he adds the famous **swerve** — atoms occasionally deviate unpredictably — so that the will is not the prisoner of a fixed chain of collisions.

The continuity, then, is a conversion of purpose. The same atoms that gave Democritus a mechanistic cosmos give Epicurus the ground of *ataraxia*, tranquility. Physics is studied not for its own sake but to quiet the mind and leave it free for the modest, reliable pleasures of a life without fear.
