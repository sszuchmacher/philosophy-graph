Augustine received Plato largely at second hand — through Cicero, through the Neoplatonists, through the Latin air of late antiquity — but the encounter shaped Christian thought for a thousand years. His central move is to take Plato's realm of eternal Forms and *relocate* it: the Forms become **ideas in the mind of God**.

Plato had faced an awkward question: where do the Forms — Justice itself, Beauty itself, the perfect circle — exist? He spoke of them as if in "a place beyond the heavens," subsisting on their own. Augustine answers that they subsist as the eternal thoughts of the Creator, the archetypes according to which He made all things. The Forms are not a second divine realm alongside God; they are God's own ideas.

This single adjustment solves several problems at once and makes Platonism safe for monotheism. It explains why the world is intelligibly ordered — it was made on rational patterns. And it explains how we can know eternal truths with a certainty the changing senses could never supply: by a kind of **divine illumination**, God lighting the mind so that it glimpses the immutable truths that dwell in Him. Mathematics and moral standards are certain because they are grounded in God.

The reinterpretation preserves everything Augustine loved in Plato — the reality of eternal, non-sensible truth, the soul's ascent from appearances toward it — while removing what a Christian could not accept: an eternal order independent of God. Plato's Forms, hovering uncertainly in their own heaven, are brought home into the divine mind. For the entire Middle Ages, to be a Platonist was, in effect, to be an Augustinian.
