Voltaire was not a systematic philosopher but a supremely effective *popularizer*, and one of his greatest services was to carry John Locke — along with Newton — across the Channel and make him fashionable in France. His *Philosophical Letters* of 1734 presented English empiricism, tolerance, and constitutional liberty to a French public still living under absolutism and Cartesian orthodoxy.

From Locke, Voltaire took and broadcast a whole outlook. Against the Cartesian doctrine of innate ideas, he championed Locke's picture of the mind as shaped by **experience** — a modest, empirical account of knowledge that refused to pronounce on what it could not observe. He especially prized Locke's caution about the soul: Locke had suggested we cannot be sure that matter is incapable of thought, and Voltaire seized on this as a model of intellectual humility and a rebuke to dogmatic metaphysics. Locke, he wrote admiringly, wrote the *history* of the human mind instead of inventing a romance of it.

But it was Locke the theorist of **toleration** and **liberty** that Voltaire most wanted France to hear. He held up English religious tolerance, its commerce, and its limited government as a standing reproach to French persecution and arbitrary power. The *Philosophical Letters* are empiricism turned into cultural criticism: praise of England is coded condemnation of the Old Regime.

The relationship is thus one of transmission rather than original development. Voltaire adds little to Locke's philosophy; what he adds is *reach*. He strips the empiricism of its technical apparatus, dramatizes its consequences for freedom and tolerance, and injects it into the bloodstream of the French Enlightenment.

Through Voltaire's wit and enormous audience, Locke's ideas — experience over innate certainty, tolerance over persecution, limited government over absolutism — became weapons in the French war of ideas, helping prepare the ground on which the Revolution would be argued.
