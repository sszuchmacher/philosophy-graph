Sellars is best known as a Kantian, but he also, only half-jokingly, described his philosophy as a kind of "incipient **Hegelian** meditation," and he is one of the routes by which Hegelian themes re-entered analytic philosophy, preparing the way for the later "Pittsburgh Hegelians."

The Hegelian strand emerges from Sellars's critique of the **Myth of the Given**. Once you deny that knowledge rests on any bare, self-justifying foundation of unconceptualized data, you are pushed toward a **holism** about concepts and justification that is deeply Hegelian. A concept, for Sellars, has content only through its **inferential relations** to other concepts — its place in a whole web of moves in the "space of reasons." To grasp any concept is to grasp its role in a system; there are no atoms of meaning standing alone. This holism — that the parts have their identity only through the whole, that meaning is a matter of position in a total network of inferential relations — echoes Hegel's insistence that no determination is intelligible in isolation and that "the true is the whole."

Sellars also shares Hegel's emphasis on the **social and historical** character of the conceptual order. The space of reasons is a public, communal, rule-governed practice, into which individuals are initiated; concepts are not private possessions but belong to a shared, developing framework — a naturalistic descendant of Hegel's Geist, the rational structure realized in a community's practices.

And his grand project — the reconciliation of the "manifest image" and the "scientific image" of the world — has a Hegelian shape: not choosing one over the other but seeking a higher synthesis, a single stereoscopic vision in which the normative world of persons and the causal world of science are integrated.

So Sellars continues Hegel in an analytic key. From his anti-foundationalism flows a Hegelian holism of concepts and justification, a stress on the social-historical space of reasons, and a synthesizing ambition — all pursued with the tools of logic and philosophy of language rather than dialectic. By killing the Given and making conceptual content a matter of inferential role within a communal whole, he reopened analytic philosophy to Hegel, and his students would make the debt explicit.
