Kant had drawn a limit around knowledge. We know only **phenomena**, the world as it appears to us under our forms of intuition and our categories. Beyond lies things as they are in themselves — the *thing in itself* — forever beyond our reach. It was a gesture of humility: reason learning to recognize its own limits.

Hegel finds in that gesture a contradiction that destroys it.

> To draw a limit presupposes that one is, in some sense, already beyond it.

The argument has a remarkable elegance. To assert that there exists something unknowable beyond the limit of knowledge, Kant has already had to *think* that beyond: he has had to conceive the thing in itself well enough to declare it unknowable. But then it is not really outside thought. The limit Kant draws, by the very fact that he can draw it, has already been crossed. An absolute limit one could speak of would no longer be absolute.

Hence the relation is one of *radicalization*. Hegel does not abandon the Kantian project: he takes it further than Kant dared. If there is no outside of thought, then the distinction between phenomenon and thing in itself must dissolve. Reality does not hide behind appearances; it unfolds *through* them, in a process. What for Kant was an impassable wall is for Hegel only a station along the road by which Spirit comes to recognize itself in everything that exists.

The price is the inverse of Kant's. Where Kant gained humility and lost access to the absolute, Hegel recovers the absolute at the risk of an immoderate ambition: a knowing that, in the end, leaves nothing outside.
