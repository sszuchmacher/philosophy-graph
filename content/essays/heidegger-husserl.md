Heidegger was Husserl's student and chosen heir — Husserl secured him his chair — and *Being and Time* is dedicated to Husserl "in friendship and admiration." It is also the book in which Heidegger radicalizes phenomenology so thoroughly that it breaks with his master, and the friendship with it.

From Husserl, Heidegger takes the **method**: phenomenology, the return "to the things themselves," the patient description of phenomena as they show themselves, and above all the notion of **intentionality**, that consciousness is always directed toward, always engaged with, its world. This is his inheritance and his tool.

But he transforms its whole orientation. Husserl's phenomenology was **transcendental** and centered on **consciousness**: through the *epoché*, it bracketed the existing world to describe the structures of pure consciousness and the transcendental ego for which any world is constituted. It remained, Heidegger thought, within the Cartesian tradition — a philosophy of the knowing subject contemplating objects.

Heidegger throws out the bracketing and the subject. The proper theme of phenomenology, he insists, is not consciousness but **Being** — the forgotten question of what it means *to be* — and the proper starting point is not a pure transcendental ego but **Dasein**, the concrete human being always already thrown into a world, absorbed in practical dealings, caring about its existence. Where Husserl's subject *contemplates* objects present before it, Heidegger's Dasein is primarily a *doer*, handling tools, involved in a meaningful world of concerns — "being-in-the-world" before any detached knowing.

This is phenomenology turned from **epistemology** to **ontology**, from the analysis of consciousness to the analysis of existence and Being. Husserl sought certain knowledge by describing pure consciousness; Heidegger uses phenomenological description to reawaken the question of the meaning of Being through an analysis of the being who asks it.

Husserl, reading *Being and Time*, felt betrayed — he concluded that his brilliant pupil had abandoned rigorous transcendental phenomenology for a philosophical anthropology and a relapse into metaphysics. He was right that Heidegger had broken away. Heidegger had taken Husserl's method and turned it against Husserl's goal — using phenomenology not to ground knowledge in consciousness but to reopen the deepest question of Western thought.
