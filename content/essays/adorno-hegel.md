Adorno was steeped in Hegel and never stopped thinking dialectically — yet his central philosophical work is titled ***Negative Dialectics***, and the "negative" names a fundamental break. Adorno keeps Hegel's method and turns it against Hegel's deepest conviction.

What Adorno keeps is the **dialectic** itself: thinking in terms of contradiction, mediation, and totality, the refusal to take concepts and objects as simply given, the insistence that everything is bound up with its other and with the whole. He also keeps Hegel's critique of immediacy — the recognition that there is no bare given, that all experience is mediated by concepts and history.

What he rejects is Hegel's **synthesis** — the moment of *Aufhebung* in which contradictions are resolved and reconciled in a higher unity, and, ultimately, the great claim that the whole is rational, that "the true is the whole," that the dialectic culminates in the Absolute reconciling all difference. Adorno's counter-slogan is deliberately blasphemous: "**the whole is the false**." For Adorno, Hegel's reconciling synthesis is a philosophical lie that mirrors a social one — the way the existing order absorbs and neutralizes everything that opposes it, presenting a false, coerced reconciliation as if it were rational fulfillment. To declare the whole rational is to bless a world of domination and suffering.

At the heart of the critique is what Adorno calls **identity-thinking** — the drive, which he finds culminating in Hegel, to make the object fully match the concept, to leave no remainder, to subsume every particular under the universal. This, for Adorno, is intellectually false (concepts never exhaust their objects) and morally sinister (it is the logical form of domination, the refusal to let the particular be). Against it he champions the **non-identical** — that in the object which escapes the concept, the particular that resists absorption. Negative dialectics is dialectic that keeps the tension open, honors the non-identical, and *refuses* the consoling synthesis.

So Adorno's relation to Hegel is a critique from within — a dialectic turned against the dialectician. He preserves Hegel's method, mediation, and sense of totality, while amputating the affirmative conclusion that made the whole rational. Where Hegel's dialectic comes to rest in the Absolute, Adorno's stays restless and negative, siding always with the suppressed particular against the totalizing system — a Hegelianism that has passed through Auschwitz and can no longer believe that the real is rational or that the whole redeems its parts.
