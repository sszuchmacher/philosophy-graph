Gadamer was Heidegger's student, and his masterwork *Truth and Method* — the founding text of philosophical hermeneutics — develops Heidegger's thought into a full theory of **understanding**. Gadamer took the early Heidegger's analysis of interpretation and turned it into a philosophy of how we understand texts, art, history, and one another.

The decisive inheritance is Heidegger's account of understanding as an **existential** structure — not a special method of the human sciences but a basic mode of human being-in-the-world. In *Being and Time*, Heidegger had shown that understanding is not primarily a cognitive act performed by a detached subject; it is the way Dasein always already exists, projecting possibilities, situated in a world it did not choose. Crucially, he analyzed the "**fore-structure**" of understanding: we never interpret from nowhere, but always on the basis of prior expectations, a "fore-having" and "fore-conception" that we bring to whatever we seek to understand. There is no presuppositionless interpretation.

Gadamer builds his whole hermeneutics on this. From Heidegger's fore-structure he derives his rehabilitation of **prejudice** (*Vorurteil*): the pre-judgments we inherit from **tradition** are not obstacles to understanding, to be cleared away (as the Enlightenment demanded), but the very condition that makes understanding possible. We understand always from within a horizon shaped by our history. Understanding a text is a **fusion of horizons** — a meeting between the horizon of the interpreter and that of the text, in which both are transformed.

From Heidegger too comes the emphasis on **language** as the medium in which understanding happens and world is disclosed ("being that can be understood is language"), and the anti-methodological thrust: understanding is not a technique we apply to master an object but an *event* that happens to us, in which we are addressed by the text and drawn into dialogue.

But Gadamer also broadens Heidegger. Where the later Heidegger grew ever more concerned with Being, poetry, and a certain mysticism, Gadamer turns the hermeneutic insight outward into the humane practice of interpreting the tradition — art, the classics, law, the ongoing conversation of culture — with a generosity and openness to dialogue that is his own.

So Gadamer continues Heidegger by taking his existential analysis of understanding — the fore-structure, the situatedness, the linguistic disclosure of world — and developing it into philosophical hermeneutics: a theory of how, through tradition, prejudice, and the fusion of horizons, we come to understand the meanings handed down to us. Heidegger reopened understanding as a mode of being; Gadamer made it the ground of a whole philosophy of the humanities.
