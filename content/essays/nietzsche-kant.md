Kant believed he had freed morality from religion by grounding it in pure reason. The moral law — the **categorical imperative** — binds every rational being unconditionally, independent of God, desire, or consequence; duty is duty because reason commands it. Morality stands on its own rational feet.

Nietzsche claims to see through this. Kant's rational morality, he argues, is **Christian morality in disguise** — the same self-denying, life-negating value system, merely stripped of its theology and given a philosophical alibi. Kant kept every substantive Christian value (selflessness, duty, the suppression of inclination, universal equal moral worth) and simply replaced "God commands it" with "reason commands it." The categorical imperative, Nietzsche says, "smells of cruelty" — of the old ascetic hostility to the instincts, dressed in the robes of pure reason.

Several things enrage him. The exaltation of **duty against inclination** repeats the Christian war on the natural drives, treating what one *wants* to do as automatically suspect. The demand for a **universal** law binding all rational beings equally continues the Christian leveling that denies rank and difference among human types. And the very idea of a disinterested law valid for everyone Nietzsche regards as a fiction: all values are the expression of particular forms of life, conditions of the flourishing or decline of some type of human being.

Behind Kant's austere edifice Nietzsche detects the **ascetic ideal** and *ressentiment* — the same forces he unmasks in Christianity in the *Genealogy of Morals*. Kant, he sneers, is a "catastrophic spider" who used reason to smuggle the old morality past the death of God.

The critique belongs to Nietzsche's larger campaign: the death of God, he insists, must also mean the death of the Christian *morality* that Kant tried to save by rationalizing it. You cannot keep the value system, drop its theological foundation, and pretend nothing has changed. Kant's rational ethics is the last hiding place of a morality whose god is already dead — and it too must be overcome in the revaluation of all values.
