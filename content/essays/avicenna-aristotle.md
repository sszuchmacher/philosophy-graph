For the medieval world, Islamic and Christian alike, Avicenna was not merely a reader of Aristotle but a philosopher who reorganized and completed him. Working in eleventh-century Persia, he absorbed the whole Aristotelian corpus and rebuilt it into a system of his own — one later Latin readers often could not disentangle from Aristotle himself.

His great innovation is the **distinction between essence and existence**. Aristotle had analyzed things into form and matter, potency and act; Avicenna adds a deeper cut. In any created thing, *what* it is — its essence, its "horseness" or "humanity" — is one thing, and *that* it is — the fact of its existence — is another, superadded to the essence. A thing's essence does not include its existence; existence must come to it from outside.

From this follows his famous proof of God. Everything whose essence does not entail its existence is merely **possible**, or contingent — it needs a cause to exist. Trace the chain of contingent beings and it must terminate in a being whose very essence *is* to exist: the **Necessary Existent**, God, who alone exists of Himself and gives being to all else.

This is Aristotle transformed. Aristotle's God was a final cause, drawing the cosmos by being loved; Avicenna's is the source of existence itself, from whom reality flows through a chain of intelligences. The essence–existence distinction, foreign to Aristotle, becomes the backbone of medieval metaphysics.

When these ideas reached the West in Latin translation, they arrived *as* Aristotle — and thinkers from Aquinas onward would spend enormous effort sorting the genuine Philosopher from his brilliant Persian systematizer, adopting the essence–existence distinction even while correcting its details.
