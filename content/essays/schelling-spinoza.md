German Idealism had a Spinoza problem. In the "pantheism controversy" of the 1780s, Spinoza's name stood for a single, all-encompassing substance — God-or-Nature — that seemed to swallow all individuality and freedom into blind necessity, and to amount, many said, to atheism and fatalism. Fichte's philosophy of the free, self-positing I was in part a flight from this Spinozist abyss.

Schelling turns back toward Spinoza and openly restores him — but transformed by Kant and Fichte. From Spinoza he takes the great monist vision: there is one absolute reality, and everything finite is a mode or expression of it. His **philosophy of identity** revives *Deus sive Natura* — a single Absolute in which nature and spirit, object and subject, are ultimately one and the same. Against Fichte's isolation of the I, Schelling wants Spinoza's one immanent reality embracing both.

But he means to correct what German Idealism found unacceptable in Spinoza. Spinoza's substance was static, inert, and lifeless — a geometrical order in which nothing genuinely develops and all is fixed by necessity. Schelling injects into it the very things Spinoza lacked: **life, activity, and development**. His Absolute is not a frozen substance but a living, productive power, unfolding dynamically through nature and history, striving toward self-consciousness.

So the continuity is a resurrection with a difference. Schelling takes Spinoza's monism — one infinite reality of which all things are expressions — and fuses it with the post-Kantian emphasis on activity, freedom, and becoming. Spinoza's dead substance becomes a living, self-developing Absolute.

This "dynamized Spinozism" set the agenda for what followed. Hegel would take up the same project — Spinoza's one substance, but made alive and rational — and press the correction further, insisting that the Absolute be understood not merely as living substance but as **subject**. Schelling reopened Spinoza for German Idealism; the quarrel over how to complete him would define its final chapter.
