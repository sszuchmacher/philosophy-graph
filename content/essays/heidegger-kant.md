Shortly after *Being and Time*, Heidegger published a startling book on Kant — *Kant and the Problem of Metaphysics* — that reads the *Critique of Pure Reason* against the grain, transforming the great work of epistemology into a forerunner of his own ontology of finite human existence.

The standard reading takes the first *Critique* as a theory of knowledge: an account of how the mind, through the forms of intuition and the categories, constitutes experience and thereby limits knowledge to appearances. Heidegger rejects this as too narrow. The *Critique*, he argues, is really about the **finitude** of human being and the possibility of metaphysics — an inquiry into how a finite creature can have any relation to beings at all.

His interpretation centers on two things the tradition had marginalized. First, **imagination**: Heidegger seizes on Kant's obscure doctrine of the "transcendental imagination" — the hidden faculty mediating between sensibility and understanding — and makes it the common root of both, the deep source from which our whole relation to the world springs. Second, **time**: he argues that this imagination is essentially *temporal*, so that time, not the categories, lies at the very ground of the mind's power to encounter beings. Kant, on this reading, was on the track of Heidegger's own thesis in *Being and Time* — that Being is understood through time.

Heidegger also stresses that Kant, in the second edition, drew back from these radical implications, "shrinking back" from the abyss he had glimpsed — the groundless temporal imagination at the root of human finitude. Heidegger reads the retreat as a failure of nerve and completes what Kant flinched from.

This is bold, even willful, **reinterpretation** — Heidegger admits he is doing "violence" to the text to expose its unspoken tendency. He turns Kant's critical epistemology into a "laying of the ground for metaphysics" rooted in finitude and time, making the philosopher of the knowing subject an unwitting ancestor of the analysis of Dasein. Kant becomes a stage on Heidegger's own path toward the question of Being.
