Hume revived, in the eighteenth century, the ancient skepticism that Sextus Empiricus had systematized — the Pyrrhonian tradition of suspending judgment in the face of reason's failure to secure its own foundations. But he domesticated it, producing a skepticism fit to live with rather than a paralysis.

The kinship is deep. Like the ancient Pyrrhonists, Hume argues that our most basic beliefs cannot be rationally justified. That the future will resemble the past, that there are causal powers in nature, that external objects and a continuing self exist — none can be established by reason or experience without circularity. This is the Pyrrhonian result: reason, pressed hard, undermines itself, and no belief about how things really are can be demonstrated.

But Hume parts ways with the ancients over what follows. The Pyrrhonist recommended full *epochē*, suspension of judgment on everything, as the road to tranquility. Hume thinks this psychologically impossible and, if attempted, absurd — a skepticism so extreme that no one could act on it for a moment. Nature does not let us suspend judgment; when we leave the study and return to life, we cannot help believing in cause, world, and self.

His solution is a **mitigated skepticism** — Pyrrhonism with manners. We acknowledge, in philosophical honesty, that our beliefs lack rational foundation; but we recognize that **custom**, habit, and natural instinct carry us on regardless, and wisely so. We keep believing and acting, chastened but functional, our doubts confined to the study while common life proceeds. Where the ancient skeptic sought serenity by relinquishing belief, Hume finds a livable balance by relinquishing only the *pretension* that belief is rationally grounded.

So Hume carries Sextus's arguments into modern philosophy while blunting their practical edge — a skepticism that humbles reason without stopping life, and that leaves habit, not demonstration, as the true guide of human existence.
