Irigaray belongs to the generation of French feminists who came after Beauvoir and defined themselves, in part, *against* her — and the disagreement marks one of the deepest divides in feminist theory: between the feminism of **equality** and the feminism of **sexual difference**.

Beauvoir had analyzed woman as the **Other** — man as the essential Subject, woman as the inessential other defined in relation to him — and her implicit remedy was for women to claim the status of Subject that men had reserved for themselves: to become full, free, transcending subjects, to gain access to the projects, freedom, and universality that patriarchy had monopolized. Beauvoir's ideal, in this reading, is a kind of **equality**: women should be able to be and do what men are and do; the goal is to overcome the difference that has been used to subordinate them and to admit women to the (implicitly masculine) universal of free human subjectivity.

Irigaray finds this a trap. To ask women to become subjects on the existing model, she argues, is to ask them to become like **men** — to enter a "neutral" universal that is, in fact, secretly masculine. The very ideal of the free, transcending, universal Subject was constructed by and for men; to pursue equality on those terms is to erase women's specificity and assimilate them to the male norm, leaving the masculine standard itself unquestioned. Beauvoir, in Irigaray's view, still measures woman against a masculine measure — even her liberation is liberation *into* a man's world and a man's conception of the subject.

Against equality Irigaray sets **sexual difference**. The task of feminism is not for women to become equal to (that is, the same as) men within a masculine universal, but to affirm and articulate a genuinely **feminine** subjectivity, language, and desire that has never yet been allowed to exist — to think and live sexual difference positively rather than erasing it. "Sexual difference," she famously says, is one of the major philosophical issues, if not *the* issue, of our age. Women need their own symbolic, their own genealogy, their own cultural forms, not admission to men's.

So Irigaray's relation to Beauvoir is a critique that reframes the goal of feminism. She accepts Beauvoir's diagnosis that woman has been the Other, but rejects the remedy of equality-as-sameness, arguing that it smuggles in the masculine norm and erases the feminine. Where Beauvoir sought to free women *into* the universal of free subjectivity, Irigaray charges that this universal is covertly male and calls instead for the affirmation of a distinct feminine subjectivity and sexual difference. Beauvoir wanted women to become subjects; Irigaray wants women to become subjects *as women*, in a difference the masculine tradition, and even Beauvoir, could not think.
