Feyerabend's epistemological anarchism has a respectable liberal ancestor, and he knew it: **John Stuart Mill**, whose *On Liberty* Feyerabend admired and repeatedly invoked as the philosophical foundation of his own defense of pluralism and freedom in knowledge.

From Mill, Feyerabend takes above all the argument for the **proliferation of ideas** and the value of dissent. In *On Liberty*, Mill had defended freedom of thought and discussion on the grounds that truth is best served by the widest possible competition of opinions: even false views are valuable because they challenge received truths, prevent them from becoming "dead dogma," and force us to hold our beliefs for good reasons rather than mere habit; and we can never be certain that a suppressed opinion is not true. Truth emerges, or stays alive, only through the clash of contrary views.

Feyerabend transposes this straight from politics into the **philosophy of science and method**. His central methodological principle — the **proliferation** of theories, the deliberate multiplication of alternatives, including views that contradict well-established theories — is Mill's defense of dissent applied to scientific knowledge. Just as Mill argued that society needs a diversity of opinions to keep truth vital, Feyerabend argues that science needs a diversity of competing theories: a reigning theory can be genuinely tested and criticized only against rivals, and even "refuted" or unorthodox alternatives must be kept alive and developed, because they may expose the flaws of the orthodoxy or turn out to be right. Monism — the dominance of a single theory or method — is, for both, the death of critical vitality.

Feyerabend also inherits Mill's broader **liberalism and pluralism** — the defense of individuality, of the freedom to live and think differently, of a society that does not impose a single way of life or a single conception of truth. This underlies his provocative political conclusion in *Science in a Free Society*: that science should not be given a privileged, state-enforced monopoly over knowledge, but should coexist, on equal terms, with other traditions, leaving individuals and communities free to choose — a direct application of Millian liberty to the authority of science.

So Feyerabend continues Mill by taking the liberal case for freedom of thought and the competition of opinions and making it the foundation of his philosophy of science. Mill's argument that truth requires the free proliferation and clash of dissenting views becomes Feyerabend's principle of theoretical proliferation and his defense of pluralism against method and scientific orthodoxy. Where Mill defended the liberty of opinion in society, Feyerabend defends the liberty of theory in science and the liberty of traditions in a free society — grounding epistemological anarchism in the classic liberalism of *On Liberty*. Mill gave him the argument that diversity and dissent are the lifeblood of truth; Feyerabend carried it into the heart of science and turned it against the tyranny of a single method.
