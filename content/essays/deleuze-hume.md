Deleuze's very first book, *Empiricism and Subjectivity* (1953), was on Hume — an unexpected starting point for a thinker later known for Nietzsche, Spinoza, and Bergson, and one that planted seeds that would grow throughout his work. He reads Hume against the grain, turning the sober Scottish empiricist into a radical philosopher of relations, invention, and what Deleuze would later call "transcendental empiricism."

The key move is Deleuze's emphasis on Hume's doctrine that **relations are external to their terms**. Hume had shown that when we connect ideas — by resemblance, contiguity, and above all causation — the connection is not contained in the ideas themselves; the mind *adds* it, through habit and the principles of association. Deleuze seizes on this: relations do not derive from the nature of things but are external, added, invented. This becomes a lifelong Deleuzian principle — the priority and exteriority of relations over their terms, against every philosophy that grounds relations in essences or substances.

From this Deleuze draws a striking picture of the **subject**. For Hume, the mind begins as a mere collection of disconnected perceptions; the self is not given in advance but is *constituted* — "invented" — when the principles of association organize the flux of impressions into a coherent subject with beliefs and expectations. The subject is an effect, a product of processes that exceed it, not a prior foundation. Deleuze reads this as a profound anti-Cartesian insight: subjectivity is *produced* within a field of impressions and associative principles, not the origin of experience but its result.

Deleuze also prizes Hume's emphasis on **belief and invention** over knowledge, and on the imagination as a spring of human life — and he reinterprets Humean empiricism not as the passive reception of sense-data but as a creative, inventive activity, the fabrication of relations, beliefs, and institutions that go beyond the given.

So Deleuze reinterprets Hume into a philosopher of externality, invention, and the constituted subject. He takes the doctrine that relations are external to their terms and the account of the self as produced by associative habit, and turns them into founding principles of his own thought: the primacy of relations, the subject as an effect rather than an origin, and empiricism as the creative fabrication of the new. The dry skeptic of the textbooks becomes, in Deleuze's hands, an early theorist of the productive, relational, subject-constituting processes his whole philosophy would explore.
