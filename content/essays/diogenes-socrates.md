Plato is said to have called Diogenes "a Socrates gone mad," and the phrase catches the relationship exactly. The Cynics claimed descent from Socrates through his follower Antisthenes, and nearly everything in Diogenes is a Socratic theme pushed past the limit of respectability.

Socrates had questioned the conventions of the city, professed to know nothing, valued virtue over wealth and reputation, and needed almost nothing to be happy. But he did it *within* Athens — a citizen who served in the army, argued in the agora, and, at the end, refused to escape his own execution out of respect for the laws. His subversion wore the manners of the *polis*.

Diogenes tears the manners off. If virtue is the only good and convention worthless, why honor any of it? He lived in a barrel, ate in the marketplace, performed every bodily function in public, and "defaced the currency" both literally and as a motto for overturning custom. Where Socrates *asked* whether our conventions make sense, Diogenes simply *ignored* them, using shamelessness as an argument in action.

> I am looking for an honest man.

Even the search for genuine virtue amid human pretense is Socratic — but Socrates pursued it in dialogue, Diogenes with a lantern at noon and a sneer. The radicalization exposes something latent in the master: if the examined life really is worth more than the city's goods, its logical end is not the courteous gadfly but the *dog* — *kuōn*, from which "Cynic" comes — who owes the city nothing and answers to nature alone.
