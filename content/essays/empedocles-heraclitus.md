Heraclitus had seen the world as a battlefield of opposites, held together by their very antagonism — war is the father of all things, and the harmony of the cosmos is a tension of contraries like that of the bow and the lyre. But he left the vision poetic, oracular, unsystematic.

Empedocles takes that intuition and gives it a mechanism. The Heraclitean strife of opposites becomes, in his hands, two named cosmic powers — **Love and Strife**, attraction and repulsion — working on the four eternal roots. What was a brooding insight about the unity of opposites turns into something like a physics.

Where Heraclitus said that all things flow, Empedocles specifies *how*: the roots are pulled into combination by Love and torn apart by Strife, and the world cycles between total union and total dispersal. Tension is no longer a mood of the cosmos but an alternation of measurable forces.

> A twofold tale I shall tell: at one time they grew to be one out of many, at another they grew apart to be many out of one.

Something is gained and something lost in the translation. Gained: a model you can reason with, a cosmos governed by two intelligible principles rather than a riddle. Lost: the vertigo of Heraclitus's deeper insight that the opposites are not merely combined but *identical* — that the way up and the way down are one. Empedocles keeps the war of opposites; he tames it into mechanics.
