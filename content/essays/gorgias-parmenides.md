Parmenides had used pure reason to prove that Being *is* — that what is necessarily exists, is one, and is knowable by thought, since the same thing is for thinking and for being. Reason, following its own thread, arrives at reality.

Gorgias turns the whole apparatus inside out. In a virtuoso performance — *On Non-Being* — he apes Eleatic reasoning to reach three escalating negations. **First: nothing exists.** Second: even if something existed, it could not be known. Third: even if it could be known, it could not be communicated to another.

Each thesis parodies a Parmenidean move. Where Parmenides argued that thought and being coincide, Gorgias drives a wedge between them at every joint: being and thinking come apart, thinking and speaking come apart, my mind and yours come apart. Words are not things; the hearer receives sounds, not the speaker's experience.

Is it serious philosophy or a rhetorical joke? Both — and that is the point. Gorgias was a master of persuasion who held that speech has almost magical power over the soul precisely *because* it is not tethered to truth. If nothing can be known or transmitted, then all we ever have is *logos*, persuasive words, and rhetoric rather than metaphysics becomes the queen of the arts.

The inversion is total. Parmenides used argument to anchor us in Being; Gorgias uses the same style of argument to cut every anchor, leaving language floating free. It is skepticism as showmanship — and a permanent challenge to anyone who thinks reason must land on reality.
