Baudrillard began as a Marxist sociologist, and his early work extended Marx's critique of the commodity into the analysis of consumer society. But he soon turned into one of Marxism's most radical critics from the left, arguing that Marx's own categories are too **productivist** and remain trapped inside the very system they claim to oppose.

His critique, developed in *The Mirror of Production*, is that Marx accepted, without questioning, the fundamental terms of capitalist political economy — **production, labor, use-value, need**. Marx criticized capitalism for exploiting labor and distorting production, but in doing so he made *labor* and *production* the essence of the human being ("man produces himself") and the measure of value and liberation. Baudrillard argues this is a trap: by exalting production and labor as human essence, Marxism merely mirrors the productivist ideology of capitalism itself. It criticizes capitalism in capitalism's own terms, sharing its obsession with production, utility, and the economy, and so cannot truly escape or think beyond it. Even "use-value," which Marx treated as the real, natural substratum beneath the distortions of exchange-value, Baudrillard argues is itself a construct, an alibi, no more natural than exchange-value.

More devastatingly, Baudrillard argues that Marx's whole framework has been **overtaken by history**. Marx analyzed a world of *production*, where value derives from labor and the reality of use. But contemporary society, Baudrillard claims, is no longer organized around production but around **consumption, signs, and simulation**. Value is now determined by **sign-value** — the differential play of signs, brands, and meanings by which commodities signify status and identity — not by labor or utility. We live in a world of codes and simulacra where the Marxist categories of labor-value no longer grip; the economy has become semiotic, a system of signs referring to signs.

So Baudrillard's relation to Marx is a critique that starts inside Marxism and breaks decisively with it. He accepts Marx's ambition to unmask the deep logic of capitalist society, but charges that Marx remained a prisoner of the productivist paradigm — mirroring capitalism's own exaltation of production, labor, and use-value — and that this paradigm no longer describes a world dominated by consumption, signs, and simulation. Marx analyzed the production of commodities; Baudrillard analyzes the production of *meanings* and the reign of sign-value, arguing that we have passed from an economy of production into an order of signs and simulacra that Marxism, tied to labor and the real, cannot comprehend.
