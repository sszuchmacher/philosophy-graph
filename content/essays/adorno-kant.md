Adorno lectured on Kant repeatedly and wrestled with him all his life, and his relation is a sustained **critique** of what he saw as the coercive, dominating tendency hidden in Kant's philosophy — even as he honored Kant's honesty and depth.

Adorno's target is the Kantian **constituting subject**. In Kant's theory of knowledge, the mind actively imposes its forms — space, time, the categories — on the raw material of sensation; the object is in large part *constituted* by the knowing subject, shaped to fit the subject's concepts. For Adorno this is a philosophical expression of **domination**. The Kantian subject masters the object, forcing it under universal concepts and its own legislating forms, leaving the thing-in-itself — the object as it is in its own right — unknowable and excluded. This is "identity-thinking" at its transcendental root: the subject's insistence that the object conform to the concept, with no remainder allowed. Adorno reads the mastery of nature by the knowing subject as of a piece with the domination of nature and of human beings in capitalist society.

He is equally critical of Kant's **moral philosophy**. The categorical imperative, with its rigid universal law, its subordination of inclination to duty, and its formalism, strikes Adorno as another face of the same coercion — reason legislating against the sensuous, particular self, the suppression of impulse and happiness in the name of an abstract universal. There is, he suggests, a cold streak in the Kantian exaltation of duty over feeling.

Yet Adorno's critique is dialectical, not dismissive. He *values* what is unresolved in Kant — the honesty of the thing-in-itself, the acknowledgment that the object exceeds our concepts. In leaving the thing-in-itself unknowable, Kant preserved, against his own idealism, a trace of the **non-identical** — the recognition that reality is not simply the subject's construction. This is precisely what Hegel, absorbing everything into the self-developing concept, threw away. So Adorno reads Kant's block on knowledge of things-in-themselves not only as a limit to be lamented but as a saving reticence, a refusal to let the subject swallow the world whole.

So Adorno's critique exposes the domination latent in the constituting subject and the coercion in the moral law, reading both as philosophical forms of reification — while rescuing, against Kant himself and against Hegel, the Kantian acknowledgment of a reality that exceeds the concept. Kant is criticized as a philosopher of mastery and salvaged as a reluctant witness to the non-identical.
