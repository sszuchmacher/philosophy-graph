The later Putnam turned increasingly to the later **Wittgenstein**, and Wittgensteinian themes came to shape his mature rejection of the metaphysical realism and scientism he had once embraced. What began as an occasional reference deepened into one of the guiding influences of his final decades.

Several Wittgensteinian ideas became central to Putnam. First, the critique of the idea of a **"God's-eye view"** — a description of reality as it is in itself, independent of any human perspective, concepts, or practices. Putnam's "internal realism," and later his "natural realism," deploy a Wittgensteinian suspicion of the notion that we could step outside all our conceptual schemes and language-games to compare them with an unconceptualized reality. Truth and reference make sense only within our practices, not from a transcendent standpoint.

Second, the rejection of the **fact/value dichotomy**. Drawing on Wittgenstein (and the pragmatists), Putnam argued at length that the sharp separation of "facts" from "values" — a dogma of logical positivism — is untenable: our descriptive and evaluative concepts are entangled ("thick" ethical concepts like *cruel* are at once factual and evaluative), and the practice of science itself embodies values (coherence, simplicity, reasonableness). This collapse of the fact/value split undoes the positivist picture Putnam had been raised in.

Third, a broadly **therapeutic and anti-metaphysical** conception of much philosophy — the sense that many traditional problems (about the external world, the relation of mind to reality) arise from confused pictures that need to be dissolved rather than solved, and that philosophy should return us to the ordinary rather than build metaphysical systems.

So the later Putnam continues Wittgenstein by turning Wittgensteinian resources against his own earlier self. Having once been a scientific realist and functionalist in the confident post-positivist mode, he used Wittgenstein — together with William James and John Dewey — to criticize the very idea of metaphysical realism, the fact/value dichotomy, and the scientistic reduction of the mind, arriving at a chastened, pluralistic, common-sense realism. Wittgenstein helped the most protean of analytic philosophers find his way out of the metaphysical pictures that had held his own tradition captive.
