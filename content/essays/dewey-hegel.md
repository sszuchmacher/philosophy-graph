Before he was a pragmatist, Dewey was a Hegelian, and he never entirely left Hegel behind. As a young man he found in Hegel a liberation — a way past the dualisms that fractured experience: mind against world, self against society, thought against action. He would later say Hegel left a "permanent deposit" in his thinking. His mature pragmatism is, in part, Hegel *naturalized* — stripped of its Absolute and reborn in the idiom of Darwin and experimental science.

What Dewey keeps from Hegel is the drive to **overcome dualism** and to think in terms of **organic wholes** and **development**. Hegel had refused the sharp separations of Kantian and empiricist philosophy — subject and object, reason and sense, individual and community — insisting that these are moments within a larger developing unity. Dewey inherits this holism: experience is a continuous transaction between organism and environment, not a spectator-mind confronting an external world; the self is constituted through its social relations; thought and action, means and ends, are continuous.

He also keeps a version of Hegel's **developmentalism** — the idea that mind, knowledge, and institutions grow through a dynamic process, that reality is history and change rather than fixed structure. And his emphasis on the social, communal character of mind owes much to Hegel's insight that reason is realized in shared institutions and culture, not in the isolated individual.

But Dewey **reinterprets** Hegel by cutting away his metaphysics. Gone are the Absolute Spirit, the cosmic teleology, the necessary dialectic culminating in self-knowledge. In their place Dewey puts **nature**, biology, and open-ended experimental inquiry. Development is no longer the logical unfolding of an Absolute toward a preordained end but the contingent, unfinished adaptation of organisms and societies solving their problems. The Hegelian dialectic becomes the method of experimental intelligence; the reconciliation of oppositions becomes the practical resolution of concrete difficulties; Spirit realizing itself in history becomes democratic community reconstructing itself through cooperative inquiry.

So Dewey carries Hegel's holism and developmentalism into a thoroughly naturalistic, anti-metaphysical philosophy. He keeps Hegel's cure for dualism and his sense of reality as process, while replacing the Absolute with nature and logic with experiment — Hegel without the metaphysics, in the service of science and democracy.
