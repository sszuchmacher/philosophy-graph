In the 1560s a Latin edition of Sextus Empiricus reintroduced Europe to the full arsenal of ancient Pyrrhonism, and no one absorbed it more deeply than Montaigne. He had skeptical maxims carved into the beams of his library ceiling, and his longest essay, the "Apology for Raymond Sebond," is a sustained deployment of the Sextan modes against the pretensions of human reason.

From Sextus, Montaigne takes the whole machinery of doubt: the endless disagreement of philosophers, the variability of the senses, the way custom and perspective shape every judgment, so that for any claim an opposing claim stands equally supported. Reason, he concludes, is a weak and wandering instrument; the animals may be wiser than we in their way, and our vaunted knowledge is mostly presumption.

> What do I know?

That motto — *Que sais-je?* — is Pyrrhonian suspension of judgment turned into a personal signature, even struck on a medal. It is doubt not as system but as disposition, a habit of never quite closing the question.

Yet Montaigne transforms what he inherits. Sextus offered skepticism as a *method* aimed at tranquility; Montaigne makes it the atmosphere of a wholly new form — the **essay**, the *essai* or "attempt," a tentative, exploratory, self-observing kind of writing that never pretends to conclude. Ancient skepticism becomes modern self-portraiture: he turns the doubt inward and studies the one changing, uncertain thing he can observe from within — himself.

The continuity is the doubt; the invention is the voice. Sextus doubted to reach *ataraxia*; Montaigne doubts to know a human being, and in doing so founds a genre and a whole modern manner of skeptical, humane reflection.
