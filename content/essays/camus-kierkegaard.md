In *The Myth of Sisyphus*, Camus treats Kierkegaard as one of his most important interlocutors — and one of his sharpest targets. Kierkegaard is, for Camus, a supreme diagnostician of the **absurd** who then commits what Camus calls "philosophical suicide" by leaping out of it into faith.

Camus honors the diagnosis. Kierkegaard had felt, as few others had, the collision between human reason's demand for meaning and clarity and a reality that refuses to supply it — the anguish, the paradox, the way existence resists rational comprehension. This confrontation with absurdity, the anxiety before a world reason cannot master, Camus takes as genuine and profound. Kierkegaard is one of the thinkers who truly *sees* the absurd.

But then, Camus charges, Kierkegaard **flees** it. Faced with the unbearable tension between the human need for meaning and the world's silence, Kierkegaard resolves it by a **leap of faith** — an embrace of the very paradox that crushed him, a surrender to God that transforms the absurd from an offense into an object of worship. He "makes of the absurd... the criterion of the other world," turning what should be lived as a permanent contradiction into a springboard to the eternal. For Camus this is an evasion: to escape the absurd by leaping into faith is to abolish one of its two terms — the lucid human demand — by sacrificing reason to a consoling beyond.

Camus insists instead on **staying** in the absurd. The whole point, for him, is to hold the contradiction open — to keep both terms, the human demand for meaning *and* the world's silence, in permanent tension, without resolving it by either suicide (abolishing the human) or faith (abolishing the honest confrontation). The absurd must be *lived*, not escaped; one must remain lucid, in revolt, refusing all false consolations, including the religious one.

So Camus's critique is a fork in the road that both men reach from the same place. Kierkegaard and Camus stand together before the absurd; but where Kierkegaard leaps into faith, Camus refuses the leap and demands that we live without appeal, keeping our lucidity and our revolt. What Kierkegaard calls the movement of faith, Camus calls philosophical suicide.
