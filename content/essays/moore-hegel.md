G. E. Moore, alongside Russell, led the **revolt against idealism** that founded analytic philosophy — and where Russell's rebellion turned on logic and relations, Moore's turned on a defense of **common sense** and a demand for clear, ordinary meaning.

British philosophy in the 1890s was ruled by Hegelian **absolute idealism**: the doctrine that reality is a single spiritual Absolute, that the material world and separate physical objects are mere appearance, and that ordinary beliefs must be corrected by a higher metaphysical truth. Moore found this simply *incredible* — and made his incredulity a philosophical method.

His great weapon is the appeal to what we all really know. In "A Defence of Common Sense" and "Proof of an External World," Moore insists that he knows, with far greater certainty than he knows any premise of any idealist argument, a great many ordinary truths: that this is a hand, that the earth existed before his birth, that there are material objects. Holding up his hands — "here is one hand, and here is another" — he claims to prove the external world exists. The point is not the gesture's logical force but its **weight of certainty**: any argument concluding that material things are unreal must rest on premises *less* certain than the plain fact that here are two hands, so the argument should be rejected rather than the fact.

Moore's critique is also **analytic** in a founding sense. In "The Refutation of Idealism" he takes the idealist slogan *esse est percipi* and dissects it, distinguishing the act of awareness from its object and arguing that idealists had confused the two — that clear analysis of what the doctrine actually *means* dissolves its plausibility. This attention to the precise meaning of philosophical claims, and the refusal to let grand systems override ordinary knowledge, became hallmarks of the whole tradition.

So Moore's relation to Hegelian idealism is a founding **critique**. Against the idealist claim that reality is spiritual and the everyday world illusory, he sets the greater certainty of common-sense knowledge and the discipline of analyzing what philosophical statements really mean. Where the idealists demanded that we distrust the obvious in the name of the Absolute, Moore trusts the obvious and distrusts the Absolute — and analytic philosophy is born from that reversal of priorities.
