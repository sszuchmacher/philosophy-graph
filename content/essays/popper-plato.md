The first volume of Popper's *The Open Society and Its Enemies* is devoted to **Plato**, whom Popper — writing during the Second World War — identified as the original and most formidable philosophical enemy of the open society: the great thinker who first, and most seductively, articulated the ideal of a **closed, totalitarian** state.

Popper's target is above all Plato's *Republic*. Behind its beauty and profundity, Popper argues, lies a blueprint for a rigid, hierarchical, authoritarian society: a caste system with the mass of people fixed in their places; rule by a guardian elite of philosopher-kings who alone know the Good and are entitled to total power; strict censorship of art and ideas; eugenic breeding; the subordination of the individual entirely to the collective; and the famous "noble lie" — a myth propagated to keep the classes in their place. This, for Popper, is the archetype of the **closed society**: static, tribal, hostile to change, criticism, and individual freedom, ruled by those who claim privileged knowledge of the truth.

Popper links this to Plato's **historicism** and his metaphysics. Plato's theory of Forms, with its changeless, perfect archetypes and its devaluation of the changing sensible world, expresses (Popper argues) a deep **hostility to change** — a longing to arrest the flux of history and freeze society in a perfect, unchanging order, halting the "corruption" that time brings. Plato feared the open, changing, democratic Athens of his day and sought to stop change dead. And his rule of the wise who *know* the Good licenses the authoritarian conviction that those with special knowledge should hold unquestioned power.

Against Plato, Popper defends the **open society**: one that frees the critical powers of individuals, that is subject to democratic control and peaceful change, that abandons the search for infallible rulers in favor of institutions that let us remove bad rulers without violence, and that embraces fallibility and piecemeal reform rather than the imposition of a perfect blueprint. His counter-question to Plato's "who should rule?" (answer: the wise) is "how can we arrange institutions so that bad rulers do least harm and can be removed?"

Popper's reading is, again, **fiercely contested** — many Plato scholars regard it as anachronistic and one-sided, reading twentieth-century totalitarianism back into a very different ancient text. But its force as a manifesto is undeniable.

So Popper's relation to Plato is a foundational critique that casts the greatest of philosophers as the first great enemy of freedom. He reads the *Republic* as a totalitarian blueprint, the theory of Forms as an expression of the fear of change, and the rule of philosopher-kings as the archetype of authoritarian rule by those claiming special knowledge. Against Plato's closed, unchanging, expert-ruled utopia Popper sets the open society — fallibilist, democratic, self-correcting — and begins his history of freedom's enemies with the founder of Western philosophy himself.
