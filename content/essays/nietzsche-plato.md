Plato's founding gesture was to divide reality in two. On one side, this world: changing, deceptive, subject to time and death. On the other, the true world: that of the eternal Forms, accessible only to reason, where what is real and what is good reside. Philosophy, morality, and later Christianity inherited that map: the sensible life is worth less than a perfect beyond.

Nietzsche devotes his work to dismantling that division. Not by proposing a third world, but by showing that the "true world" never existed.

> Christianity is Platonism for the people.

The sentence concentrates his thesis: what Christianity calls God, heaven, immortal soul is the popularized version of the Platonic Forms. Always the same movement: invent a perfect beyond in order to devalue the here. And always, according to Nietzsche, the same consequence: resentment against life, contempt for the body, hatred of what changes and perishes.

That is why the relation is one of *inversion*. Nietzsche does not refute Plato's arguments one by one; he inverts the entire hierarchy. What Plato considered highest — the eternal, the unchanging, the supersensible — becomes a sign of weakness: the inability to bear a world without guarantees. And what Plato despised — becoming, the body, appearance — becomes the only thing that is real and the only thing worth affirming.

In *Twilight of the Idols*, Nietzsche narrates in a few lines "how the true world finally became a fable": the history of the West as the slow fading of that other world, until nothing remains behind the appearances. And then, he says, neither does the apparent world: if we abolish the true world, we also abolish its opposite. Only *this* world remains, without a second story above it. Learning to live there, without metaphysical consolation, is the task Nietzsche leaves to us.
