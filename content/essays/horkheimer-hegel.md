Critical theory was Marxist, but its Marxism was of the **Hegelian** kind — the tradition, revived by Lukács in the 1920s, that read Marx back through Hegel and stressed the dialectical, humanist core over the economic determinism of the Second International. Horkheimer inherited and institutionalized this Hegelian Marxism.

From Hegel, Horkheimer takes above all the **dialectical method** — thinking in terms of totalities, contradictions, and development, refusing to treat social facts as isolated, fixed things. Society must be grasped as a dynamic whole shot through with contradictions, in which every phenomenon is mediated by its relation to the larger system and to its own history. This is the opposite of the "traditional theory" that treats its objects as given, static data; critical theory's power to see the existing order as *historical* and *changeable* comes from Hegel's insight that reality is process and that the apparently solid is a moment in a developing whole.

Horkheimer also inherits Hegel's insistence on the **critical power of reason** — reason as more than instrumental calculation, as a faculty that can measure the existing world against its own unrealized potential and find it wanting. Indeed his late diagnosis of the "eclipse of reason" is Hegelian in its lament: it mourns the shrinking of a full, substantive (Hegelian) reason, capable of reflecting on ends and grasping the whole, into a merely instrumental reason concerned only with efficient means. The very standard by which Horkheimer condemns instrumental reason is the richer, dialectical reason inherited from Hegel.

But Horkheimer, following the Hegelian-Marxist line, keeps this dialectic **materialist and critical** rather than idealist and reconciling. He rejects Hegel's culminating Absolute — the claim that reason is already actual, that the real is rational, that history has reached its fulfillment. For Horkheimer the dialectic must remain **negative** and unfinished, refusing to bless the existing order as rational; the reconciliation Hegel proclaimed has not happened, and to say it has is ideology.

So Horkheimer continues Hegel by making the dialectical method, the critical concept of reason, and the thinking of society as a contradictory totality the very tools of critical theory — while stripping away Hegel's affirmative conclusion. He keeps Hegel's engine and discards Hegel's destination, turning the dialectic from a philosophy that reconciles us to the actual into one that exposes the actual as false and unfree.
