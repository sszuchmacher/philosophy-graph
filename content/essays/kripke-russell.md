Kripke's *Naming and Necessity* overturned a theory of names that had reigned since Russell, and much of its power comes from its precise demolition of the **descriptivist** account Russell had made orthodox.

Russell (developing Frege) had held that ordinary proper names are really disguised **descriptions**. "Aristotle," on this view, does not refer directly to a man but abbreviates some description like "the teacher of Alexander" or "the author of the *Metaphysics*." The name refers to whatever uniquely fits the description; the description gives the name's meaning and fixes its reference. Russell's theory of descriptions had shown how to analyze such phrases, dissolving puzzles about non-existent objects ("the present King of France").

Kripke rejects the descriptivist picture with a battery of arguments. His central claim is that names are **rigid designators**: a name refers to the *same object in every possible world*, whereas the associated description does not. Consider "Aristotle might not have taught Alexander." This is plainly true — Aristotle could have chosen a different life. But on Russell's theory, "Aristotle" *means* "the teacher of Alexander," so the sentence would say, absurdly, "the teacher of Alexander might not have taught Alexander." The name and the description come apart: the name sticks to the man across counterfactual situations, the description does not.

Kripke adds that the descriptions speakers associate with a name are often **wrong or empty** — most people can supply no uniquely identifying description of Feynman or Gödel — yet they still succeed in referring. So reference cannot be secured by descriptions in the speaker's head.

In place of descriptivism Kripke proposes the **causal-historical theory of reference**: a name is fixed to its bearer by an initial "baptism" and then passed from speaker to speaker in a chain of communication; I refer to Aristotle because my use is causally linked, through that chain, to the original naming — not because I have a correct description.

So Kripke's relation to Russell is a foundational **critique** that redrew the philosophy of language. He dismantles the reigning Russellian descriptivism about names, replaces it with rigid designation and causal reference, and in the process reopens metaphysics — his rigid designators underwrite the categories of necessary a posteriori and contingent a priori truth, and the essentialism, that Russell's generation thought logic had banished.
