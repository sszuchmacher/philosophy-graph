Quine was Carnap's devoted student and friend — he called Carnap his greatest teacher and the dominant influence of his early career — and his most famous work is a respectful patricide: the dismantling, from within, of the logical empiricism Carnap had built.

Carnap's philosophy rested on a foundational distinction: between **analytic** truths (true by virtue of meaning alone — "all bachelors are unmarried," the truths of logic and mathematics) and **synthetic** truths (true by virtue of fact, answerable to experience). On this distinction the whole edifice depended: mathematics and logic could be counted true-yet-empty (analytic), metaphysics dismissed as neither, and empirical science neatly separated into its factual and its conventional-linguistic components.

In "**Two Dogmas of Empiricism**" (1951), Quine attacks the distinction itself. Every attempt to define "analytic" precisely, he argues, moves in a small circle — explaining it in terms of "synonymy," "meaning," or "necessity," each as obscure and undefined as analyticity itself. There is no non-circular criterion. The supposedly sharp line between truths of meaning and truths of fact is a dogma, an article of faith empiricism has not earned.

In its place Quine offers **holism**. No individual statement faces experience alone to be confirmed or refuted; rather, our beliefs form a single interconnected "web" that meets experience only as a whole. When experience conflicts with the web, we have wide latitude about what to revise — even the laws of logic could in principle be given up to save the rest, and even a statement about immediate experience could be held come what may. There are no truths immune to revision (none purely analytic) and none tied to experience in isolation.

The consequences are large. Without the analytic-synthetic distinction, the tidy logical-empiricist picture collapses: mathematics is not a special class of empty analytic truths but part of the same web as physics; the line between science and metaphysics blurs; and epistemology must become **naturalized**, a chapter of empirical science rather than its a priori foundation.

So Quine continues Carnap's empiricism by purging it of what he saw as its last unempirical dogmas — and in doing so transforms it utterly. He keeps Carnap's logical rigor and scientific temper; but by demolishing the analytic-synthetic distinction he brings down the architecture of logical positivism and replaces its layered, foundational picture of knowledge with a single seamless web answerable, as a whole, to experience. It is a pivotal moment in analytic philosophy: the student ending the movement of the teacher he most admired.
