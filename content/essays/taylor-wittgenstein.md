Charles Taylor draws on the later **Wittgenstein** as a key ally in his campaign against the disengaged, representational picture of the mind and against naturalistic reductions of meaning. Wittgenstein's account of meaning, rules, and forms of life supplies Taylor with tools for his own view of human beings as embedded in shared practices and background understandings.

From Wittgenstein, Taylor takes the doctrine that **meaning is use** — that words and concepts get their sense not from private mental representations or from labeling a pre-given reality, but from their role in public practices, in the "language games" woven into a shared way of life. This undercuts the Cartesian and empiricist picture of meaning as inner representation, which Taylor spends his career attacking, and it fits his "expressivist" view that language constitutes rather than merely designates its meanings.

He draws especially on Wittgenstein's treatment of **rule-following**. Wittgenstein had argued that following a rule is not a matter of grasping an explicit formula and applying it by a further interpretation (which would launch a regress), but a practice, a customary way of going on, grounded in shared training and agreement in a form of life. Taylor uses this to argue that our understanding always rests on a **background** of largely inarticulate practices and know-how that cannot be fully spelled out in explicit rules. Beneath our explicit beliefs lies a tacit, embodied, communal understanding — an idea Taylor shares with Heidegger and finds powerfully in Wittgenstein.

And Wittgenstein's concept of a **form of life** — the shared, communal background of practices within which language and meaning are possible — reinforces Taylor's communitarian, anti-atomist conviction that the human agent is constituted within a community and its practices, not prior to them.

So Taylor continues Wittgenstein by taking meaning-as-use, the practical account of rule-following, and the notion of a form of life, and enlisting them in his own philosophy of the engaged, socially embedded agent. He reads Wittgenstein alongside Heidegger as a great critic of the modern epistemological picture — of the mind as an inner theater of representations — and as a witness to the tacit, communal background of understanding that grounds all thought and language. Wittgenstein gave Taylor powerful reasons for holding that meaning is public and practice-based and that explicit knowledge floats on a sea of inarticulate shared understanding — cornerstones of Taylor's critique of naturalism and his defense of situated human agency.
