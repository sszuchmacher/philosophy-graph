Empedocles had explained the world with four eternal **roots** — earth, water, air, fire — mixed and separated by Love and Strife. It was the most successful physics before Aristotle, and Aristotle keeps a great deal of it: the four elements survive, transformed, at the core of his own cosmology.

But he judges the scheme radically incomplete, and the judgment flows from his signature doctrine: to explain anything is to give its **four causes** — its matter, its form, the agent that produced it, and the end for which it exists. Measured against that fourfold demand, Empedocles has supplied only *one* kind of cause well. The four roots are **material** causes, the stuff things are made of; Love and Strife gesture at a *moving* cause. But of **form** and **end** — what makes a thing the kind of thing it is, and what it is *for* — Empedocles says almost nothing.

And form is everything. Flesh and bone are not merely earth and fire in a heap; they are those materials arranged in a definite ratio and structure, organized toward a function. A corpse has the same elements as a living animal and is not an animal at all. The **form**, the organizing principle, is exactly what Empedocles's mixtures cannot capture.

Worse, by letting Love and Strife alternate blindly, he lets the cosmos be governed partly by *chance*, with organisms surviving only because ill-formed combinations happened to perish — an idea Aristotle rejects as unable to explain the regular, purposive order of the living.

The critique is constructive. Aristotle does not discard the elements; he embeds them as the *matter* within a richer world of form, agency, and purpose.
