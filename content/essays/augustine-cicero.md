Augustine's lifelong love of philosophy had a precise and unlikely origin: a now-lost book by Cicero. At nineteen, a student of rhetoric in Carthage, he read Cicero's *Hortensius*, an "exhortation to philosophy," and it set his soul ablaze. He describes the moment in the *Confessions* as a turning point — the book taught him no doctrine but ignited a *desire*, a longing to seek not eloquence or success but wisdom itself.

> This book changed my affections, and turned my prayers toward Thee.

What Cicero gave him was the conviction, drawn from the Greek tradition, that happiness lies in the pursuit of wisdom and truth rather than in the goods the world prizes — the very goods young Augustine, ambitious and pleasure-loving, had been chasing. The *Hortensius* redirected his wanting. From that reading he dates the beginning of a search that would wind through Manichaeism and Neoplatonism before arriving, decades later, at Christianity.

There is a poignant qualification Augustine himself adds. The one thing that cooled his enthusiasm for the *Hortensius*, he says, was that the name of Christ was not in it — a name his mother's faith had planted in him as a child. So Cicero lit the fire but could not name its true object; the exhortation to wisdom pointed beyond what its author knew.

The episode captures Augustine's whole debt to pagan philosophy: it could awaken the longing for God, and even describe the ascent toward Him, but it could not, by itself, complete the journey. A Roman orator's lost protreptic began the conversion of the greatest theologian of the Latin West.
