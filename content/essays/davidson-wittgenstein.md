Davidson is usually classed with Quine and the formal, truth-theoretic tradition, but his mature philosophy converges, from that unlikely direction, on central themes of the later **Wittgenstein** — above all the impossibility of a private language and the constitutive role of community in meaning and thought.

The deepest shared conclusion concerns the **social and public character of thought and meaning**. Wittgenstein's private-language argument had shown that there could be no language intelligible to only one person, that meaning requires public criteria and shared practice, and that the idea of a solitary mind with its own private meanings is incoherent. Davidson reaches a kindred result through his account of interpretation. Meaning and belief, he argues, are essentially interpretable — they exist only insofar as they could be understood by another — and in his later work he makes this explicit in the "**triangulation**" argument: the concepts of objective truth, of a mind with determinate thoughts, and of a shared world arise only in the interaction of *two* creatures responding to a common environment. A single mind alone could not have the concept of objectivity, of getting things right or wrong. Thought requires the Other.

Both thinkers, then, reject the **Cartesian picture** of a self-contained mind that has its meanings and beliefs privately, prior to any community. For both, mind and meaning are irreducibly interpersonal.

The convergence is all the more striking for the difference in **method** — and this is why it is continuity rather than discipleship. Wittgenstein reached these conclusions by describing ordinary uses and dissolving confusions, in an anti-theoretical, therapeutic spirit. Davidson reaches them by building a formal theory of meaning and interpretation in the Quinean-Tarskian mode. One assembles reminders; the other constructs arguments. Yet they arrive at the same place: no private language, no solitary meaning, thought and truth as achievements of communicating creatures in a shared world.

So Davidson "continues" Wittgenstein less by direct influence than by vindicating Wittgensteinian insights from within the rival tradition. He shows that the formal, systematic philosophy of language descended from Frege, Russell, and Quine, followed rigorously, lands on the later Wittgenstein's conclusions — the publicity of meaning, the incoherence of the private, the dependence of thought on community and world.
