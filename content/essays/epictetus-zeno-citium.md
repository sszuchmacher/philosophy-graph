Three centuries separate Epictetus from Zeno, and the school's doctrines are largely unchanged: virtue is the only good, the passions are false judgments, the cosmos is governed by a providential logos. What changes is the *register*. In Epictetus, the system built by the early Stoa becomes a practical discipline for enduring a hard life.

Epictetus knew hardship. Born a slave and reportedly lamed by a cruel master, he taught a Stoicism stripped of the founders' elaborate logic and physics and focused almost entirely on ethics — on how to live. His single great theme is the early Stoic distinction between what is "up to us" and what is not, sharpened into a rule of life: **some things are in our power** — our judgments, desires, and choices — **and some are not** — our bodies, possessions, reputation, the actions of others.

Misery comes from wanting what we cannot control. Freedom — real freedom, the freedom a slave may have and an emperor may lack — comes from confining our desires to what is genuinely ours: the inner realm of the will.

> Men are disturbed not by things, but by their opinions about things.

This is pure Zeno — the passions as errors of judgment — but pointed at the sufferer. The early Stoics asked what the sage *is*; Epictetus asks what the struggling person should *do* this morning. He turns a metaphysical system into a set of exercises, a philosophy you can practice in chains. That practical turn is why his little *Handbook* has consoled soldiers, prisoners, and the grieving for two thousand years while the Stoa's treatises on logic gather dust.
