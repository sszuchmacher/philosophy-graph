Derrida's deconstruction is unimaginable without Heidegger — Derrida called Heidegger's thought the indispensable and "in a certain way, uncircumventable" event that made his own work possible. Deconstruction is, in large part, the radicalization of Heidegger's project of dismantling the Western metaphysical tradition.

From Heidegger, Derrida takes the fundamental gesture: the **critical dismantling of the history of metaphysics**. Heidegger had argued that Western philosophy from Plato onward forgot the question of Being and fell into thinking Being as *presence* — as the stable, present-at-hand — and he called for a "destruction" (*Destruktion*) of this tradition to recover what it had covered over. Derrida's very word "deconstruction" translates and transforms Heidegger's *Destruktion*. And Derrida takes over Heidegger's central diagnosis: that the tradition is governed by a **metaphysics of presence** — the privileging of what is immediately present to consciousness, of origin, of full self-identical being.

But Derrida **radicalizes** Heidegger, and turns Heidegger's own tools against him. His decisive move is to argue that Heidegger did not go far enough — that Heidegger *himself* remained caught in the metaphysics of presence he sought to overcome. Heidegger still sought a more original access to Being, a truth as unconcealment, a proper "meaning of Being," a nostalgia for a lost origin and a hope for an "other beginning." All of this, Derrida argues, still privileges presence, proximity, and origin — still dreams of reaching something proper, primordial, and present beneath the tradition's forgetting.

Derrida's **différance** is designed to close off this escape. There is no origin, no pure presence, no proper meaning of Being to be recovered — only the endless play of differences and deferral, traces referring to traces, with no self-present ground anywhere, at any "beginning" or "end." Where Heidegger sought to think Being more originally, Derrida shows that there is no originality to be reached, that presence is always already divided by the trace.

So Derrida continues Heidegger by inheriting the deconstruction of the metaphysics of presence and the critical reading of the whole Western tradition — while radicalizing it past Heidegger's own limits. He takes Heidegger's destruction of metaphysics and applies it, relentlessly, to Heidegger himself, exposing the residual nostalgia for presence and origin in the very thinker who taught him to hunt for it. Deconstruction is Heidegger's project pushed to a point where even Heidegger's "Being" and "truth" become metaphysical residues to be unsettled — the destruction of metaphysics carried out without any hope of a purer ground beyond it.
