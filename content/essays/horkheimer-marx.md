As director of the Frankfurt Institute for Social Research, Max Horkheimer gave the movement its name and its charter — "**critical theory**" — and defined it explicitly by reference to Marx. His programmatic essay "Traditional and Critical Theory" (1937) sets out what the Frankfurt School would be, and it is a self-conscious extension of Marx's project into a new age.

The Marxian core is a distinction between two kinds of theory. **Traditional theory** — the model of the natural sciences and most academic thought — takes society as a given object to be described and explained, treating the theorist as a detached observer standing outside what he studies. **Critical theory**, by contrast, follows Marx in understanding that the social world is not a natural given but is *produced* by human activity, that the theorist is *part* of the society he analyzes, and that the aim of theory is not merely to understand the world but to help **transform** it — to expose the historically produced, changeable, and unjust character of arrangements that present themselves as natural and eternal. This is Marx's eleventh thesis on Feuerbach institutionalized as a research program.

Horkheimer also inherits Marx's **materialism**, his method of ideology-critique — the exposure of how ruling ideas mask relations of domination — and his focus on capitalism as a system of exploitation and reification.

But Horkheimer already begins the Frankfurt School's characteristic **broadening and darkening** of Marx. He supplements Marxian economics with psychoanalysis and cultural analysis, seeking to understand why the proletariat had *not* become revolutionary — why the masses submitted to fascism and consumer capitalism rather than overthrowing them. And over time, especially in *Dialectic of Enlightenment* and *Eclipse of Reason*, his Marxism grows pessimistic: he comes to see not just capitalism but the whole trajectory of Enlightenment **instrumental reason** as the problem — a domination of nature and humanity that Marx's own faith in the productive forces had shared.

So Horkheimer continues Marx by founding critical theory as an emancipatory, world-transforming inquiry that keeps Marx's materialism, ideology-critique, and practical aim — while opening it to psychology and culture and, increasingly, questioning the confidence in reason and progress that classical Marxism had taken for granted. He preserves Marx's revolutionary intent even as the revolution recedes, turning historical materialism into a critique of a domination deeper than Marx had diagnosed.
