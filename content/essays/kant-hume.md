Hume had pushed empiricism to an unsettling conclusion. If everything we know comes from experience, then we have no right to speak of **causes**. We see one billiard ball strike another and the second one move; we see that succession repeated many times. But we never *perceive* the necessary connection between them. Causation is not in the world: it is a habit of the mind that expects the future to resemble the past.

The blow was serious. If causation has no rational foundation, neither does science, which is built entirely on relations of cause and effect.

> I freely admit that it was the recollection of David Hume which, many years ago, first interrupted my dogmatic slumber.

Kant acknowledges the debt without hedging. But his answer consists not in denying Hume, but in *turning the question around*. Hume was right: causation is not found in experience. The mistake is to expect to find it there. For Kant, causation is one of the **a priori categories**: a structure the understanding contributes so that experience is possible at all.

It is not that the mind discovers causes in the world; it is that the mind can only have experience of a world *already organized* in terms of cause and effect. Causation does not come from experience: it is the condition that there be experience.

This is why the relation is one of *critical continuation*. Kant does not refute Hume from outside; he accepts the empiricist starting point and keeps it, but adds the level Hume was missing. He saves science from skepticism — at an enormous price: we know only phenomena, the world as it appears under our categories, never things as they are in themselves. That "thing in itself" will be precisely what his successors refuse to forgive him.
