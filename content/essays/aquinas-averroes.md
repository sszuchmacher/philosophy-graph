Aquinas revered Averroes as "the Commentator" and learned Aristotle largely through him — but on one doctrine he judged the great commentator not merely mistaken but dangerous, and wrote a whole treatise, *On the Unity of the Intellect against the Averroists*, to demolish it.

The doctrine is the **unity of the intellect**. Interpreting Aristotle's obscure remarks on the "agent intellect," Averroes had concluded that the highest intellect is *one and the same for all human beings* — a single, separate, immaterial mind in which individuals temporarily participate when they think. On this view, what is immortal is not your mind or mine but the one shared Intellect; the individual person, with the death of the body, perishes entirely.

For Aquinas this is intolerable on two fronts. Philosophically it is false: it cannot explain the plain fact that *this* individual, Socrates, thinks — that thinking is the act of a particular human being, not of a mind hovering outside him. "This particular man understands," Aquinas insists; if the intellect were not truly his own form, he would not think at all.

Theologically it is fatal. If there is no individual intellect, there is no individual immortality, no personal soul to be judged, rewarded, or saved. The entire Christian hope of resurrection and eternal life for *persons* collapses.

Aquinas's answer is that the intellectual soul is the **form of each individual body** — genuinely spiritual and immortal, yet genuinely *this* person's own. Each of us has our own intellect, which survives death as an individual soul awaiting the body's resurrection.

The critique shows Aquinas's method exactly: he takes Aristotle from Averroes with gratitude, then breaks decisively with the Commentator wherever his reading would cost the soul its individuality — and Christianity its promise.
