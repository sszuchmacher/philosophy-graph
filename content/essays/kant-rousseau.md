Kant kept a single decoration in his austere study: a portrait of **Rousseau**. He said Rousseau had "set him right," and the debt runs to the heart of his moral philosophy — an unexpected influence of the philosopher of feeling on the philosopher of reason.

Kant reports that he had once despised the common person and prized only intellectual achievement, until reading Rousseau taught him to honor the moral worth of ordinary human beings. From Rousseau he learned that what gives a person dignity is not talent or knowledge but the good **will** — a capacity every rational being possesses equally. This egalitarian conviction becomes bedrock in Kant: the moral law is accessible to every person, learned philosopher and simple laborer alike, and moral worth is distributed equally across humanity.

The deeper inheritance is Rousseau's idea of **freedom as self-legislation**. Rousseau had solved the problem of political freedom with the **general will**: one is free not when one does as one likes but when one obeys a law one has given oneself, a law aimed at the common good rather than private inclination. To obey the general will is to obey oneself, and so to remain free.

Kant transposes this from politics into ethics and makes it the core of the **categorical imperative** and of **autonomy**. To be moral is to act on a law you give yourself as a rational being — a maxim you could will to be universal — rather than on the pull of desire and inclination. Freedom is precisely this self-legislation: not doing what you want, but binding yourself to a universal law of your own reason. Rousseau's citizen, free in obeying the general will, becomes Kant's moral agent, free in obeying the moral law he legislates for all rational beings.

So the icy architecture of Kantian ethics has a warm Rousseauian source. The equal dignity of persons, the identification of freedom with obedience to a self-given universal law, the primacy of the good will over cleverness — all bear Rousseau's stamp. Kant gave these intuitions rigorous rational form, but he credited the man whose portrait watched over his desk with having taught him what morality was really about.
