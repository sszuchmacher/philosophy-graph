Judith Butler's theory of gender performativity draws crucially on **Derrida** — specifically on Derrida's account of **iterability** and his reworking of speech-act theory, which gives Butler the mechanism by which gender is both produced and made vulnerable to subversion.

From Derrida, Butler takes the concept of **iterability** — the idea that a sign, a mark, or a performative utterance is what it is only because it can be *repeated*, cited, and reproduced across different contexts. Derrida had argued (against Austin and Searle) that the possibility of repetition, of being "grafted" into new contexts, is essential to any sign or performative, and that this repeatability means no utterance is ever fully controlled by its origin or context; each citation both repeats and alters. Repetition is never pure reproduction; it always carries the possibility of difference and drift.

Butler applies this to gender. Gender, she argues, is **performative** in a sense sharpened by Derridean iterability: it exists only through the repeated citation of gender norms — the reiteration of stylized acts, gestures, and conventions. Gender is not a single original act but a *citational practice*, the reciting of norms that precede and exceed any individual, which through their compulsory repetition produce the effect of a natural, stable gender. This is why gender feels given: it is the sedimented result of countless citations.

Crucially, Derridean iterability supplies Butler's account of **agency and subversion**. Because gender is produced through repetition, and because repetition (as Derrida showed) can never be perfectly identical — each citation can go astray, be repeated in a new context, be parodied or resignified — the very mechanism that produces gender norms also opens the possibility of disrupting them. Drag, parody, and subversive reiteration exploit the gap in every citation. The norm depends on being repeated, and repetition is never fully faithful; there lies the room for resistance.

She also draws on Derrida's broader deconstruction of stable identity and presence — the idea that identities are effects of differential, citational processes rather than self-present essences — which underwrites her insistence that there is no natural gender identity behind its performance.

So Butler continues Derrida by taking iterability and the citational nature of the performative and building them into her theory of gender. Gender is produced by the compulsory citation of norms; but because citation, in Derrida's sense, always admits difference and can be repeated otherwise, that same citational structure makes gender open to subversion and resignification. Derrida gave Butler the logic of repetition-with-difference; Butler used it to explain both how gender is imposed through reiteration and how, in the slippage of every repetition, it can be turned against itself.
