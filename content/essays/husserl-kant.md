Husserl's mature "transcendental" phenomenology brought him close to Kant, and he came to see himself as renewing the Kantian project — though from a very different starting point and with very different tools.

The shared aim is **transcendental**: to investigate not the objects of experience directly but the *conditions under which* objects can be given to consciousness at all — the structures of subjectivity that make knowledge and a world possible. Both Kant and Husserl turn from the world to the knowing subject and ask how experience is constituted. Both reject naive empiricism, insisting that the mind actively shapes what appears. Husserl's talk of the "transcendental ego" and of the "constitution" of objects in consciousness deliberately echoes Kant's Copernican turn.

But Husserl reinterprets the project through his own method and corrects what he saw as Kant's failings. Kant had reached his conditions of experience — the forms of intuition, the categories — by a somewhat speculative "transcendental deduction," arguing indirectly from the possibility of experience to its necessary presuppositions. Husserl replaces this with **direct description**: through the phenomenological reduction, we can *intuit* and describe the structures of consciousness and the ways objects are constituted, rather than merely inferring them. Phenomenology grounds the transcendental in evidence, in what is actually given to a purified consciousness.

He also rejects Kant's **thing-in-itself**. Kant had left an unknowable reality behind appearances, a limit consciousness could never reach. For Husserl this is incoherent: there is nothing behind the phenomena that consciousness could not, in principle, come to intend; the object just *is* what is constituted in and for consciousness. And Kant's fixed table of twelve categories, deduced once and for all, gives way to an open-ended descriptive study of the endlessly various structures of intentional experience.

So Husserl reinterprets Kant's transcendental philosophy as a descriptive rather than a deductive science, grounded in intuition rather than inference, and freed of the unknowable thing-in-itself. He keeps the Kantian question — how are objects constituted for a subject? — while claiming to answer it rigorously, from the evidence of consciousness itself, where Kant had only reasoned toward it from a distance.
