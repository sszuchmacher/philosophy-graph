Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (1990) begins from Beauvoir and radicalizes her most famous claim past the point Beauvoir herself would have gone — dissolving even the distinction on which Beauvoir's feminism rested.

Beauvoir had written that "one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman" — the founding thesis that **gender is not biological destiny but a social construction**, something one is made into rather than born as. This let Beauvoir distinguish, in effect, between biological **sex** (the given body) and **gender** (the cultural situation, "woman," constructed on top of it). Feminism could then argue that the subordinate role of "woman" is imposed, not natural, and could be changed.

Butler seizes Beauvoir's insight and pushes it to a more radical conclusion. If becoming a woman is a cultural process, Butler asks, why assume there is a natural, pre-cultural **sex** underneath, a biological given that gender merely interprets? She argues that **sex itself is already gendered** — that the very idea of a pre-discursive, purely natural "sex" is itself a product of cultural and discursive practices, produced retroactively to look like a natural foundation. The sex/gender distinction collapses: there is no natural sex beneath the cultural gender, because "sex" was always already a cultural, normative category.

From this comes Butler's signature doctrine of **gender performativity**. If there is no natural sex or inner gender essence that gender expresses, then gender is not the expression of a prior identity but is *constituted* by its own performance — by the repeated, stylized acts and gestures that, through their very repetition, produce the *illusion* of a stable inner gender behind them. Gender is something one *does*, repeatedly, not something one *is*; the "doer" is an effect of the deed, not its source. And because it is produced by repetition, it can be repeated differently — subverted, parodied, resignified.

So Butler radicalizes Beauvoir by taking "one becomes a woman" and removing its remaining foundation. Beauvoir had denaturalized *gender* while leaving *sex* as a biological given; Butler denaturalizes sex as well, argues that gender is performatively constituted rather than the expression of any essence, and thereby opens gender to endless resignification. Beauvoir's constructed "woman" becomes, in Butler, a performance with no natural original — the founding feminist insight pushed to a point that unsettles the very categories of sex, gender, and identity on which feminism had relied.
