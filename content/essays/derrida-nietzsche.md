Derrida found in Nietzsche one of the great liberating precursors of deconstruction — the thinker who, more radically than anyone, had unsettled the founding assumptions of Western philosophy about truth, language, and the self. Where Heidegger read Nietzsche as the last metaphysician, Derrida reads him as a genuine escape route, and enlists him against Heidegger.

The central inheritance concerns **truth and language**. Nietzsche had argued that "truths" are "a mobile army of metaphors" — that what we call truth is a web of rhetorical figures whose figurative origin has been forgotten, and that there are no facts, only interpretations. He denied that language transparently mirrors reality, exposing the metaphorical, constructed, perspectival character of all supposed truths. Derrida takes this as a decisive precedent: it dissolves the dream of a language that gives us pure presence and unmediated truth, and it opens the field of **interpretation without end** that deconstruction inhabits — the play of meaning with no final, authoritative reading.

Derrida also inherits Nietzsche's **suspicion of binary hierarchies** and his genealogical unmasking of the values the tradition treats as natural (truth over lie, reason over rhetoric, presence over absence). And crucially, he takes over Nietzsche's **affirmative** style — the "joyful," playful, self-undermining writing that refuses systematic mastery, using irony, aphorism, and multiple voices rather than building a doctrine. Deconstruction's playfulness, its refusal to settle into a thesis, is Nietzschean.

Most pointedly, Derrida uses Nietzsche *against Heidegger*. Heidegger had tried to save Nietzsche for the history of Being, reading him as the culmination of metaphysics who still sought a truth of Being. Derrida resists this recuperation: he reads Nietzsche as the one who genuinely broke with the nostalgia for presence, origin, and proper meaning — who affirmed the play of the world and the absence of any final ground without mourning it. Nietzsche's affirmation of a world without a stable center, without an origin to be recovered, is exactly the stance Derrida opposes to Heidegger's residual longing for Being.

So Derrida continues Nietzsche as a founding ancestor of deconstruction: the philosopher who exposed truth as metaphor, denied facts in favor of interpretations, unmasked the tradition's hierarchies, and wrote in an affirmative, anti-systematic play that refuses closure. He takes Nietzsche's dissolution of truth and presence and his joyful embrace of endless interpretation, and makes them central to deconstruction — while wielding Nietzsche as the thinker who escaped the metaphysics of presence that even Heidegger could not fully leave behind.
