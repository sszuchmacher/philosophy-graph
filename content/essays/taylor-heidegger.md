Alongside Hegel, **Heidegger** is a major source of Charles Taylor's philosophy — especially Taylor's critique of the disengaged modern subject and his account of human beings as **engaged agents** embedded in a meaningful world and a background of shared understanding.

From Heidegger, Taylor takes the analysis of human existence as **being-in-the-world**: the insight that we are not detached minds representing an external world, but agents always already engaged, practically absorbed, in a world of significance. Heidegger had shown (with his analysis of the ready-to-hand, of tools and practical coping) that our primary relation to things is not theoretical contemplation but skilled, involved dealing, and that this engaged understanding is prior to and grounds any detached, representational knowledge. Taylor makes this the centerpiece of his critique of the **epistemological tradition** — the Cartesian-Lockean picture of the mind as a container of representations mirroring an outer world. Against it, following Heidegger, he sets the engaged agent whose grasp of the world is embodied, practical, and prior to representation.

Taylor also takes from Heidegger the notion of a **background** of understanding — the largely inarticulate, holistic web of significance against which anything can show up as meaningful. Our explicit thoughts always rest on this unformulated background of practices and understandings that we cannot fully make explicit. This becomes central to Taylor's account of the self and of meaning: significance is not constructed by an isolated subject but disclosed against a shared, mostly tacit background we inhabit.

And Taylor draws on Heidegger's account of **language and expression** — language not as a mere tool for labeling a pre-given world but as disclosive, as that which opens up and constitutes meaning and world. This feeds Taylor's "expressivist" view of language and his critique of instrumental, designative theories of meaning.

Taylor puts all this to work in a broadly **humanist and moral** direction that is his own — using Heidegger's engaged agent and disclosive language to combat naturalism, atomism, and the reductive picture of the human being as a disengaged processor of information, and to defend the reality of strong evaluation and moral meaning. He is less concerned than Heidegger with Being as such, and more with human agency, ethics, and modern identity.

So Taylor continues Heidegger by taking his analysis of being-in-the-world, the engaged agent, the tacit background, and disclosive language, and making them the basis of his own critique of the disengaged Cartesian self and his defense of embodied, situated, meaning-laden human agency. Heidegger gave Taylor the engaged, world-involved agent against the epistemological subject; Taylor deployed it in the service of a humanist philosophy of the self, meaning, and the moral sources of modern identity.
