Imre Lakatos was Popper's student and follower, and his "methodology of scientific research programmes" is an attempt to **rescue Popper's rationalism** from the objections of Kuhn — to preserve what is right in falsificationism while accommodating what Kuhn showed about the actual history of science.

Lakatos starts from Popperian ground: science is a rational enterprise, demarcated from pseudo-science, in which theories are appraised by their relation to evidence, and dogmatic immunity to criticism is the mark of bad method. He shares Popper's hostility to relativism and his conviction that there are objective standards for judging science better or worse. Against Kuhn's suggestion that paradigm change is a quasi-irrational "conversion," Lakatos wants to defend the **rationality** of scientific development.

But Lakatos accepts that Popper's **naive falsificationism** — the idea that a single contrary observation refutes a theory and a good scientist abandons it at once — is untenable, exactly as Kuhn's history showed. Real scientists do *not* drop a theory at the first anomaly; every important theory is "born refuted," swimming in a sea of counter-examples, and it would be irrational to abandon it before a better one exists. So Lakatos revises Popper.

His solution is to shift the unit of appraisal from the single theory to the **research programme** — a historically extended sequence of theories sharing a common "**hard core**" of central assumptions, which is protected from refutation by a "**protective belt**" of auxiliary hypotheses that scientists adjust in the face of anomalies. Falsification does not strike the core; the belt absorbs the blows. This concedes Kuhn's point that frameworks resist refutation — but Lakatos denies that this makes science irrational, because there is still a rational criterion for appraising programmes over time: a programme is **progressive** if its adjustments predict *novel* facts that are then corroborated, and **degenerating** if it merely makes *ad hoc* excuses to cover anomalies without predicting anything new. We rationally prefer progressive programmes and abandon degenerating ones — though this can be judged only over time, not by a single test.

So Lakatos continues Popper by preserving his central commitment — that science is rational, that there are objective standards distinguishing good science from bad, that falsifiability and the confrontation with evidence matter — while reforming falsificationism to survive Kuhn's historical critique. He replaces the single falsifiable theory with the research programme, the instant refutation with the long-run distinction between progressive and degenerating programmes, and so builds a bridge between Popper's logic and Kuhn's history. Popper gave him the rationalist framework and the demand for novel predictions; Lakatos rebuilt it so that it could accommodate the tenacity of theories and the extended, programme-based character of real scientific change — Popperian rationalism made historically realistic.
