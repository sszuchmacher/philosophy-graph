Wilfrid Sellars was the great **Kantian** of American analytic philosophy, and his difficult, powerful work is in large part an attempt to rethink Kant's insights in the idiom of logic, language, and science. He described his aim as moving analytic philosophy "from its Humean to its Kantian phase."

The deepest Kantian theme in Sellars is the rejection of the **Myth of the Given**. Classical empiricism, from Hume down to the logical positivists, held that knowledge rests on a foundation of pure, unconceptualized sensory data — the immediately "given," simply received and then justifying everything built on it. Sellars's famous critique argues that nothing can play this role: to be *aware of* something as a fact that can justify a belief is already to bring it under **concepts**, to place it in what he calls the "space of reasons." There is no bare given standing outside the conceptual order and grounding it from below. This is a thoroughly Kantian point — Kant had insisted that "intuitions without concepts are blind," that all experience is already shaped by the understanding.

Sellars takes over Kant's central insight that **knowledge is essentially conceptual and normative** — that experience is not a passive reception but is structured by concepts, and that to know is to occupy a place in a rule-governed practice of giving and asking for reasons. His distinction between the "space of reasons" (the normative domain of justification) and the "space of causes" (the natural domain of description) is a modern rendering of Kant's distinction between the order of thought and the order of nature.

But Sellars **reinterprets** Kant naturalistically. Kant grounded the categories in a transcendental subject and left an unknowable thing-in-itself; Sellars wants to reconcile the Kantian conceptual framework with a thoroughgoing **scientific naturalism** — his project of fusing the "manifest image" (the world of persons, reasons, and ordinary experience) with the "scientific image" (the world as physics describes it). The categories become features of our conceptual-linguistic framework, understood through the theory of language rather than a transcendental deduction, and the thing-in-itself gives way to the scientific account of what there really is.

So Sellars reinterprets Kant for the analytic age. He revives Kant's demolition of the given, his conceptualism, and his normativity of knowledge, recasting them in terms of language and the space of reasons — while replacing Kant's transcendental idealism with a naturalism that seeks to locate the space of reasons within the world described by science.
