Hume stands at the end of the line that Locke began. Locke had launched British empiricism with the claim that all our ideas come from experience, the mind a blank slate written on by sensation and reflection. Hume takes that founding principle and wields it with a consistency and rigor Locke never approached — turning a broadly commonsense epistemology into a radical instrument of doubt.

The inheritance is direct. Hume's basic division of the mind's contents into **impressions** (the vivid originals of sensation and feeling) and **ideas** (their faint copies) is Locke's empiricist framework tightened into a sharp criterion: every genuine idea must be traceable to a prior impression, or it is empty. Where Locke had gestured at experience as the source of all ideas, Hume makes the tracing a *test* he applies mercilessly.

And the results are far more unsettling than anything in Locke. Locke had been a moderate, keeping material substance, the self, causal power, and a rational religion even where his own empiricism strained against them. Hume applies the empiricist test to each and finds them wanting: the idea of **necessary connection** in causes cannot be traced to any impression, so causation reduces to habitual expectation; the idea of a substantial **self** answers to no impression, so the self dissolves into a bundle of perceptions; the very concept of **substance** is exposed as obscure.

So the relationship is continuity as intensification. Hume does not overturn Locke; he holds Locke to his own principle more strictly than Locke held himself. The empiricism that in Locke supported a confident, if cautious, account of knowledge becomes in Hume a demonstration of how little reason and experience can actually establish — waking Kant, as he famously said, from his dogmatic slumber. The founder's method, followed without flinching, arrives at skepticism.
