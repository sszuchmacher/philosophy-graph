Montaigne's *Essays* are steeped in Seneca — quoted, paraphrased, argued with on nearly every page. Alongside Plutarch, Seneca was his most constant companion, and the affinity runs deeper than borrowed sentences. Both wrote a philosophy that is personal, practical, and aimed at how to live and how to die.

The debt is partly one of *form*. Seneca's chosen genre was the moral *letter* — the informal, first-person essay-in-disguise, addressed to a friend, ranging freely over a single theme: on the shortness of life, on tranquility, on facing death. Montaigne inherits this intimate, digressive, self-disclosing manner and perfects it as the **essay**. Where Seneca writes "letters to Lucilius," Montaigne writes attempts to himself; the voice — candid, unsystematic, wise about ordinary life — is recognizably the same lineage.

The debt is also thematic. From Seneca, Montaigne takes the great Stoic preoccupation with **death** — "to philosophize is to learn how to die," he titles one early essay in a thoroughly Senecan spirit — along with the ideals of self-sufficiency, retirement from public tumult, and daily self-examination.

Yet Montaigne is no orthodox Stoic, and his later essays gently drift from Seneca's severity. Where Seneca braces against the passions and rehearses for death with tension, the older Montaigne learns to accept human weakness, to live *with* the body and its variability, and finally to let death come naturally rather than by constant grim preparation. He keeps Seneca's intimacy and moral seriousness while shedding the strain toward the impassive sage.

The result is Seneca made more human and more skeptical — the ancient art of living, loosened from doctrine and turned into the freest self-examination in European letters.
