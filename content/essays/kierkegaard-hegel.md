Hegel had built the most ambitious philosophical **system** in history — a total account in which all of reality, thought, history, and religion is comprehended as the rational self-development of the Absolute, every particular a necessary moment in the unfolding whole. To understand anything truly was to see its place in the System.

Kierkegaard made it his life's mission to explode this pretension, and his weapon was the **existing individual**. The System, he charges, has no place for the one thing that matters most: *me*, this particular person, here, now, who must choose, act, suffer, and die. Hegel's thought soars into universal abstractions and mediated totalities and forgets that all real existence is lived by singular individuals in irreducible situations. "A logical system is possible," Kierkegaard concedes; but "an existential system is impossible" — existence cannot be captured in thought, because to exist is precisely to be in the process of becoming, engaged, undecided, not a finished object of contemplation.

The deepest target is the way the System dissolves **decision**. Hegel's dialectic *mediates* opposites, reconciling them in a higher synthesis; nothing is finally either/or, everything is both/and, sublated into the whole. But real existence, Kierkegaard insists, confronts us with genuine **either/or** — choices between incompatible ways of life, aesthetic or ethical, despair or faith — that no mediation can dissolve and that each person must make in fear and trembling.

And faith, above all, resists the System. Hegel had made Christianity a stage of Spirit, rationally comprehended, its paradoxes resolved into philosophy. Kierkegaard insists that faith is precisely a **paradox** that thought cannot digest — the eternal entering time, the demand that the individual stand alone before God — grasped not by understanding but by a passionate leap. To "comprehend" faith in a system is to abolish it.

So Kierkegaard opposes to Hegel's objective, mediated, universal System the **subjective**, decisive, singular existence of the individual before God. "Truth is subjectivity": what matters is not the objective content contemplated but the passionate, personal appropriation of it in a life. Against the philosopher who wanted to comprehend everything, Kierkegaard sets the human being who must *exist* — and in doing so founds existentialism.
