Modern philosophy defined itself by rejecting Aristotle's substantial forms — those inner principles that made a thing the kind of thing it is and directed it toward its end. Descartes, Hobbes, and the mechanists had banished forms as occult verbal fictions, leaving only matter in motion. Leibniz, alone among the great moderns, deliberately **rehabilitates** them — though transformed almost past recognition.

His reason is that the mechanical philosophy, for all its power, cannot supply what a *substance* requires: genuine unity and genuine activity. A heap of extended matter, infinitely divisible, contains no true individuals and no source of the force that physics needs. To have real beings at all, Leibniz argues, we must reintroduce something like Aristotle's forms — principles of unity and activity within things.

These become his **monads**: simple, indivisible, soul-like substances, each an active center of perception and appetite. Leibniz explicitly links them to the Aristotelian and Scholastic notion of *entelechy* — a form that is a principle of activity and self-completion. Each monad unfolds its own nature from within, developing according to its internal law toward its own ends — a distant, modernized echo of Aristotelian teleology.

But this is Aristotle updated for the age of Descartes and Newton, not restored. The forms are no longer embedded in a physics of natural places and qualitative motions; they are metaphysical points beneath a fully mechanical description of the phenomenal world. Leibniz keeps mechanism for the science of bodies while grounding it in a deeper realm of form-like substances. And he adds what Aristotle lacked: each monad mirrors the entire universe, and their harmony is pre-established by God.

The reinterpretation is a bridge across the modern divide. Leibniz salvages Aristotle's deepest insight — that reality is composed of active, unified individuals with inner natures — and carries it, disguised as the monad, into a world otherwise given over to the mechanical philosophy that thought it had left Aristotle behind for good.
