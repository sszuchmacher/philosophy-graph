Plato held that the things we see — this horse, that circle drawn in the sand — are imperfect copies of perfect Forms existing in a separate realm, eternal and unchanging. The particular horse ages and dies; the Form of "horse" does not. To know, for Plato, is to ascend from the copies to the originals.

Aristotle was his student for twenty years. And his critique is born precisely from taking the theory seriously. His central objection is simple and devastating: **separating the Forms explains nothing; it only doubles the problem.**

> To say that the Forms are paradigms and that other things participate in them is to use empty words and poetic metaphors.

If, to explain why this horse is a horse, I need a Form of horse in another world, then I will also have to explain what binds the Form to the concrete horse: a third thing will be needed to relate them, then a fourth, and so on forever (the famous "third man" argument). Separation, far from solving, multiplies entities.

Aristotle's alternative is to bring the Form *inside* things. There is no separate world: the form of a living being is its way of being organized, its way of functioning and tending toward its end. The form of the oak does not float in an intelligible sky; it is at work in the acorn unfolding. Matter and form are two inseparable aspects of a single concrete reality.

It is a *critique*, but of the most fertile kind: it does not destroy the Platonic problem — what makes something be what it is? — but proposes to answer it without paying the price of a second world. Out of it will come biology, physics, and much of Western metaphysics.
