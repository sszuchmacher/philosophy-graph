Kant and Mill stand for the two great rival systems of modern ethics, and Mill's *Utilitarianism* takes direct aim at the Kantian approach. The disagreement is over the very *source* of moral obligation.

Kant had grounded morality in **reason** alone, independent of consequences. The rightness of an act depends not on its results but on the maxim behind it: act only on a principle you could will to be a universal law. Duty is categorical, binding regardless of outcome, and to consult consequences or happiness is, for Kant, to corrupt the purity of the moral will.

Mill holds the opposite: the rightness of actions depends **entirely** on their consequences for happiness — the greatest happiness of the greatest number is the whole of morality. And he presses a pointed charge against Kant. When Kant actually tries to apply the categorical imperative — to show that some immoral maxim cannot be universalized — what he really shows, Mill argues, is not a logical contradiction but that *the consequences of everyone acting that way would be undesirable*. Kant's own examples smuggle in utilitarian reasoning under the guise of pure logic.

> When he begins to deduce any actual duties, he fails, almost grotesquely, to show that there would be any contradiction in the adoption of the most outrageously immoral rules of conduct.

The critique aims to expose the categorical imperative as either empty (a mere demand for consistency that forbids nothing) or covertly consequentialist (forbidding acts because their universalization would have bad effects). Either way, Kant has not, Mill thinks, provided an independent rational foundation that escapes the appeal to human welfare.

Yet Mill honors what Kant was after — the impartiality and universality of morality, the equal claim of every person's happiness. Utilitarianism, he insists, contains its own principle of impartiality: "everybody to count for one, nobody for more than one."

So the relationship is a foundational rivalry. Kant grounds ethics in rational duty and treats consequences as irrelevant to moral worth; Mill grounds it in consequences for happiness and argues that Kant's own reasoning secretly depends on them. The debate — duty versus utility, the right versus the good — remains the central axis of moral philosophy to this day.
