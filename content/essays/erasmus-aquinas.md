Erasmus, prince of the Northern humanists, aimed his wit at the whole edifice of medieval scholasticism, of which Aquinas was the supreme representative. The quarrel is between two ideals of learning and of Christianity: the schoolmen's system of technical theology, and the humanists' return to the sources.

Scholasticism, in the centuries after Aquinas, had elaborated theology into an immense apparatus of definitions, distinctions, and disputed questions — reasoning, in Aristotelian form, about the finest points of metaphysics and doctrine. Erasmus found much of this arid, hair-splitting, and spiritually empty. In *The Praise of Folly* he mercilessly mocks the theologians who tie themselves in knots over subtleties of the Trinity or the mechanics of grace, mistaking logical ingenuity for piety and burying the simple gospel under a mountain of jargon.

Against the *method of the schools* Erasmus sets the humanist program: *ad fontes*, back to the sources. Rather than syllogistic system-building on Aristotelian foundations, he calls for the direct study of Scripture and the early Church Fathers in their original languages — and he did the work, producing a landmark critical edition of the Greek New Testament. True theology, for him, is not a science modeled on Aristotle but the reading and living of Christ's teaching.

This connects to his ideal of the **philosophia Christi** — the "philosophy of Christ" — a simple, ethical, inward Christianity of love and imitation, opposed to the ceremonial religion and doctrinal hair-splitting he associated with the scholastic establishment.

The critique is not really of Aquinas the man — Erasmus could respect his intellect — but of the *scholastic culture* he had come to symbolize: theology as technical system, Aristotle as its master, disputation as its method. Where Aquinas had made Christian truth a rigorous science built with Aristotelian tools, Erasmus wanted to dissolve the apparatus and recover the plain, transforming word of the Gospel. It is the Renaissance turning against the Middle Ages — eloquence and the sources against logic and the schools — and it helped prepare the ground for the Reformation that Erasmus himself would try, in vain, to moderate.
