Sartre's *Being and Nothingness* wears its debt in its very title, echoing Heidegger's *Being and Time*, and Sartre drew the framework of his existentialism — being-in-the-world, anxiety, authenticity, being-toward-death — largely from Heidegger. But he reads Heidegger through a Cartesian, humanist lens that Heidegger would emphatically reject, and the "misreading" is productive: it creates "existentialism" as the world came to know it.

From Heidegger, Sartre takes the analysis of human existence as thrown into a world, disclosing itself through moods, fleeing itself in inauthenticity, and defined not by a fixed essence but by its possibilities. Heidegger's insistence that Dasein has no ready-made nature but *is* its existence becomes Sartre's ringing formula: **existence precedes essence**.

But Sartre transforms this in ways that horrified Heidegger. First, he recenters everything on **consciousness and freedom**. Heidegger's Dasein was meant to displace the modern subject and reopen the question of Being; Sartre reads him as offering a philosophy of the free human subject, and makes radical, absolute freedom — consciousness as the "nothingness" that escapes all determination — the heart of the system. This is, for Heidegger, a relapse into the Cartesian subjectivism he was trying to overcome.

Second, Sartre makes existentialism a **humanism** and an ethics of choice and responsibility, drawing moral and political consequences — commitment, bad faith, responsibility for all mankind — that Heidegger's ontology deliberately avoided. Heidegger wrote his *Letter on Humanism* precisely to disown Sartre's reading, insisting that his concern was Being, not man, and that Sartre had inverted his meaning by keeping the human subject at the center.

So Sartre's relation to Heidegger is a creative **reinterpretation** that is almost a misappropriation. He takes Heidegger's ontological analyses of existence and reads them as a philosophy of human freedom, consciousness, and moral responsibility — turning fundamental ontology into existentialist humanism. Heidegger protested that his heir had misunderstood him; but it was Sartre's version — vivid, ethical, centered on freedom and choice — that made "existentialism" a movement and a household word.
