Charles Taylor's first major work was a massive, sympathetic study of **Hegel** (1975) that helped revive serious Anglophone interest in him — and Hegel is the deepest philosophical source of Taylor's own thought about the self, community, and modernity, even though Taylor cannot accept Hegel's metaphysics.

From Hegel, Taylor takes several load-bearing ideas. First, the critique of the atomistic, disengaged **modern self** — the punctual, self-defining subject of Descartes, Locke, and utilitarianism — and its replacement with a self that is **socially and historically constituted**. Following Hegel, Taylor argues that we become selves only within a community, a language, and a web of relations; the self is not prior to its social bonds but formed through them. This "expressivist" and communitarian conception of the self, drawn from Hegel (and Herder), underlies Taylor's critique of liberal atomism.

Second, Taylor takes Hegel's concept of **recognition**. Hegel had shown that self-consciousness and identity depend on being recognized by others. Taylor develops this into his influential analysis of the modern "**politics of recognition**" — the demand that our identities (cultural, ethnic, gendered) be publicly acknowledged — and into his account of how modern identity is dialogically formed through recognition and misrecognition.

Third, he inherits Hegel's method of understanding the present through its **history** — the idea that to grasp modern identity, secularity, and their discontents, one must trace the long historical development that produced them. Taylor's great genealogies, *Sources of the Self* and *A Secular Age*, are Hegelian in this sense: narratives of how the modern self and the modern condition came to be.

But Taylor firmly rejects Hegel's **metaphysics** — the Absolute Spirit, the cosmic teleology, the necessary dialectic by which Geist comes to know itself. He wants Hegel's insights into the situated, socially constituted, historically developing, recognition-dependent self *without* the grand ontological system Hegel built to ground them. He "deflates" Hegel, keeping the philosophical anthropology and the historical method while setting aside the Absolute.

So Taylor reinterprets Hegel by extracting his account of the socially and historically constituted self, the centrality of recognition, and the understanding of the present through its genesis, and rebuilding them without Hegel's metaphysics of Spirit. Hegel gave Taylor the situated self against liberal atomism, the politics of recognition, and the genealogical understanding of modernity; Taylor made these the basis of a communitarian philosophy and a history of the modern identity — Hegel's insights freed from the Absolute that Hegel thought guaranteed them.
