Arendt's very first book was her doctoral dissertation on **Augustine** — *Love and Saint Augustine* — and though she went on to become a political thinker far from theology, one Augustinian concept became the secret heart of her whole philosophy: **natality**, the fact of birth as the source of human beginning.

Arendt drew from Augustine the idea that the human being is essentially a **beginner** — a being defined by the capacity to begin something new. She quotes Augustine's phrase from *The City of God*: "*Initium ut esset homo creatus est*" — "that there be a beginning, man was created." The creation of the human being introduced into the world the very capacity for **beginning**, for initiating what has not been before. Every human being, by being born, is a new start, someone who has never existed before and who can begin something unprecedented.

Arendt transforms this theological-cosmological insight into the foundation of her political philosophy of **action**. To act, for Arendt, is precisely to *begin* — to set something new in motion, to bring the unexpected into the human world. And the ontological root of this capacity is **natality**: because human beings are born, because new beginners constantly enter the world, the world is never closed or fully determined; there is always the possibility of a new beginning, of action that interrupts automatic processes and starts something fresh. Natality is for Arendt the "miracle" that saves the world from ruin.

This is a deliberate reversal of the dominant emphasis in the existential tradition. Heidegger had made **being-toward-death** the fundamental horizon of human existence; Arendt, drawing on Augustine, makes **natality** — birth, beginning — the central category, the ground of freedom, action, and hope. Where the philosophy of her time meditated on finitude and mortality, she meditates on the fact that human beings are constantly born and can constantly begin.

She also secularizes Augustine thoroughly. What was in Augustine a doctrine about creation and the human relation to God becomes, in Arendt, an entirely worldly ontology of human beginning and political action. The theological content is left behind; the structural insight — that to be human is to be a beginning — is retained.

So Arendt reinterprets Augustine by extracting from his theology of creation the concept of the human being as beginner, and rebuilding it as **natality** — the philosophical ground of her account of action, freedom, and the ever-renewed possibility of the new. Augustine gave her the insight that with each birth a new beginning enters the world; Arendt made that the answer to despair and the foundation of politics.
