Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (1981) launched the modern revival of **virtue ethics** by calling for a return to **Aristotle** against the wreckage of modern moral philosophy. MacIntyre's diagnosis is that Enlightenment ethics — utilitarianism, Kantianism, and their heirs — has failed, leaving us with a moral language of fragments and interminable, unresolvable disagreement; and his prescription is the recovery of the Aristotelian tradition it displaced.

From Aristotle, MacIntyre takes the core structure of virtue ethics. First, the idea that ethics is about the **virtues** — settled dispositions of character — and the **good life** for a human being (*eudaimonia*, flourishing), rather than about isolated rules or the calculation of consequences. Second, and crucially, the **teleological** framework: for Aristotle, ethics makes sense only against an account of the *telos*, the proper end of human life, toward which the virtues direct us. Human beings have a characteristic function and a good; the virtues are the qualities that enable us to achieve it. MacIntyre argues that it was precisely the Enlightenment's **abandonment of teleology** — its attempt to justify morality without any conception of the human good or purpose — that doomed it to incoherence. Moral rules, torn from the teleological context that gave them point, became free-floating and unjustifiable.

MacIntyre reconstructs Aristotle's ethics for modern conditions with his own conceptual apparatus. He grounds the virtues in the notion of a **practice** — a coherent, socially established cooperative activity with internal goods and standards of excellence (medicine, farming, chess, science) — the virtues being those qualities that enable us to achieve the goods internal to practices. He adds the **narrative unity of a human life** (a life is intelligible as a story with a telos) and the **tradition** within which practices and lives are situated. These update Aristotle without the biology of his metaphysics.

So MacIntyre continues Aristotle as the philosopher whose ethics of virtue, flourishing, and teleology offers the only coherent alternative to the failed morality of modernity. He inherits the primacy of the virtues and character, the conception of the human good as the end of ethics, and above all the teleological framework whose loss he blames for modern moral chaos — rebuilding them through the concepts of practice, narrative, and tradition. Against a modernity that tried to have morality without a human *telos*, MacIntyre returns to Aristotle to argue that the virtues make sense only within a shared account of what human life is for.
