Quine and the later Wittgenstein arrived, from opposite temperaments, at a strikingly similar destination — a deep suspicion of **meanings** as objects — and then parted over what to do about it. The relation is a convergence shadowed by critique.

The common ground is the rejection of meanings as determinate mental or abstract entities. Wittgenstein's later philosophy famously replaced meaning-as-a-thing (an image, a definition, a sense attached to a word) with **meaning as use** in a public "language-game" and "form of life." Meaning is not an object but a pattern of activity. Quine, from his behaviorist and empiricist side, likewise denies that there are determinate meanings: his doctrine of the **indeterminacy of translation** argues that all the objective facts about a speaker (their dispositions to assent and dissent under sensory stimulation) do not fix a unique translation of their words — there is no fact of the matter about meaning beyond behavior. Both refuse to treat meaning as a hidden entity behind linguistic behavior, and both make the public and behavioral primary.

But Quine's stance is a **critique** as much as an echo, and the divergence is sharp. First, on **method**: Quine pursues these conclusions within a rigorously scientific, naturalistic framework — meaning tied to stimulus and response, epistemology to be absorbed into psychology and physics — whereas Wittgenstein rejects the very idea that philosophy should be scientific or explanatory. Wittgenstein's "meaning is use" is a therapeutic reminder meant to dissolve confusions, not a proto-scientific theory; his appeal to "forms of life" is anti-theoretical. Quine wanted a *theory* of language as part of natural science; Wittgenstein thought the demand for such a theory was itself the disease.

Second, Quine had little patience for Wittgenstein's method of ordinary-language description and his quietism — the idea that philosophy "leaves everything as it is" and only assembles reminders. Quine's project is revisionary: regiment language in the notation of logic, and let our best science, not ordinary usage, tell us what there is.

So the relationship is a critical near-convergence. Quine shares the later Wittgenstein's demolition of meaning-as-object and the priority of public linguistic behavior, but rejects Wittgenstein's anti-scientific, therapeutic conception of philosophy in favor of a hard naturalism. Two of the century's great critics of "meaning" agree that there are no meanings to be found — and disagree completely about whether philosophy should become science or dissolve into the description of ordinary practice.
