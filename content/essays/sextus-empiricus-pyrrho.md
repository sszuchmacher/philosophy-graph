Pyrrho of Elis wrote nothing and left only a legend: a sage of such perfect detachment that nothing disturbed him, having grasped that we can know nothing of how things really are. His skepticism was less a set of arguments than a way of life, transmitted through anecdotes and his pupil Timon's verses.

Five centuries later, Sextus Empiricus turned that elusive attitude into a rigorous method. In his *Outlines of Pyrrhonism* and related works, the vague Pyrrhonian mood becomes a fully articulated system — the most complete account of ancient skepticism to survive.

Sextus supplies the machinery. He catalogues the **modes**, or *tropes*: standardized argumentative patterns showing that for any claim about how things are in themselves, an equally strong opposing claim can be produced, since appearances vary with the perceiver, the circumstance, the culture. The proper response is *epochē* — **suspension of judgment**, neither affirming nor denying.

And he preserves Pyrrho's practical goal. The skeptic suspends judgment not as an intellectual game but as a therapy: having given up the anxious struggle to determine the truth of things, tranquility (*ataraxia*) follows "like a shadow." One still *lives* — following appearances, custom, and natural feeling — but without dogmatic commitment about how things really are.

The relationship is preservation through transformation. Pyrrho was a saint of doubt; Sextus is its systematizer and archivist, turning a temperament into a discipline that could be taught and, crucially, written down. Without him, Pyrrho would be only a name — and the skeptical challenge that would later jolt Montaigne, Descartes, and Hume out of complacency might have been lost.
