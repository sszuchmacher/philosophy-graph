Giordano Bruno drew on the Neoplatonic tradition of Plotinus to reach a conclusion that would have horrified any ancient Platonist — and that helped send him to the stake. He took the Plotinian vision of a single divine source overflowing into all things and pushed it to a scandalous cosmological extreme: an **infinite universe** filled with innumerable worlds.

From Plotinus, Bruno inherited the core intuition that all reality emanates from and participates in a single divine unity, and that the cosmos expresses the boundless creative power of its source. But where Plotinus's overflow descended through graded hypostases toward an ever-fainter material world at the bottom, Bruno collapses the hierarchy into an immanent whole. If God is infinite, he reasons, the divine power cannot be stingy: an infinite cause must produce an infinite effect. The cosmos is therefore boundless, with no center and no edge, strewn with countless suns and inhabited worlds, all animated by the one divine life.

This is Neoplatonic emanation radicalized into **pantheism**. God is not beyond the world as Plotinus's ineffable One is beyond being; God is the immanent soul and substance of an infinite nature — *Deus sive natura* before Spinoza gave it that name.

Bruno fuses this with the new Copernican astronomy, turning a technical rearrangement of the planets into a mystical metaphysics of infinity. The heliocentric sun becomes merely one star among endless others.

The continuity with Plotinus is the vision of a single divine source pouring itself into all things; the rupture is Bruno's refusal of any limit or transcendence. Emanation becomes infinite immanence — and the closed, finite cosmos of antiquity and the Church bursts open into an infinity for which Bruno was burned in 1600.
