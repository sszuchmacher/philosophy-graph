Marx was formed as a Hegelian and never ceased to think dialectically — but he declared that in Hegel the dialectic "is standing on its head," and that "it must be turned right side up again." His whole relationship to Hegel is this famous **inversion**.

For Hegel, the motor of history is **Spirit** — ideas, consciousness, the self-development of the Concept. Material and social reality is the outward expression of an inner, spiritual, rational movement; history is the progress of Spirit toward self-conscious freedom, and the real is the working-out of the Idea. Consciousness determines being.

Marx inverts the whole relation. It is not consciousness that determines life, but **material life that determines consciousness**. The true motor of history is not Spirit but the **forces and relations of production** — how human beings produce their means of subsistence, and the class relations that follow. Ideas, law, politics, religion, and philosophy are the "superstructure," arising from and reflecting the economic "base." Hegel's procession of Spirit becomes the succession of modes of production and the class struggles they generate.

Yet the inversion **keeps the method**. Marx retains Hegel's dialectic — development through contradiction, each stage generating the tensions that overturn it and give rise to the next. Feudalism gives way to capitalism; capitalism generates the proletariat that will overturn it; history advances by internal contradiction toward a resolution, exactly the shape of Hegelian development. What Marx does is fill this dialectical form with **material content**. The contradictions that drive history are not contradictions in thought but real conflicts between classes and between the forces and relations of production.

This is why Marx said he found the dialectic "mystified" in Hegel and had to extract its "rational kernel" from the "mystical shell." The rational kernel is the dialectical logic of development-through-contradiction; the mystical shell is Hegel's idealism, which made that development the self-movement of Spirit.

So Marx is Hegel inverted, not abandoned. He takes the most powerful account of historical development ever devised, strips away its idealism, and reinstalls it on a material base — producing **historical materialism**, a theory of history driven not by the Idea coming to know itself but by human beings producing their lives and struggling over the conditions of production.
