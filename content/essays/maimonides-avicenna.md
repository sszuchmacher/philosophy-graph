Maimonides built his philosophy on foundations the Islamic philosophers had laid, and Avicenna above all. Writing in Arabic, steeped in the *falsafa* tradition, he took over its metaphysical framework — including the proof of God as the **Necessary Existent** who alone exists of Himself while all else is merely possible and dependent. Avicenna's contingent/necessary analysis becomes a pillar of Maimonides's natural theology.

But the deepest shared thread is **negative theology** — the doctrine of what we *cannot* say of God. Both hold that God is absolutely simple and utterly transcends human concepts, so that no positive attribute we predicate of creatures can apply to Him in the same sense. To say God is "wise" or "living" cannot mean He has the qualities we call wisdom or life, for that would compromise His unity and liken Him to creatures.

Maimonides pushes this austerity further than almost anyone. Positive attributes, he argues, must be understood either as descriptions of God's *actions* or as *negations* — "God is living" means "God is not dead," "God is one" means "there is no multiplicity in Him." We approach God by stripping away, denying imperfection after imperfection, never by adding a positive concept. The most fitting worship, he says, is silence.

> Silence is praise to Thee.

The continuity with Avicenna is clear — the simple, necessary God beyond composition, known more by what He is not than by what He is. But Maimonides radicalizes it into a discipline of the mind: a systematic humbling of language before a God who exceeds it. This *via negativa*, carried from Avicenna through Maimonides, would shape Aquinas's own careful account of how human words can and cannot name God.
