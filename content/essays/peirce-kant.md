Peirce, the founder of pragmatism, was steeped in Kant — he said he had "almost by heart" the *Critique of Pure Reason* in his youth — and his philosophy can be read as a transformation of Kantian themes in a new, experimental key. Even the name "pragmatism" he traced to Kant's use of *pragmatisch* for what pertains to purposive action.

The Kantian inheritance runs deep. From Kant, Peirce took the anti-empiricist insight that experience is not a passive reception of raw sense-data but is structured by the mind through concepts and categories — that cognition is active and rule-governed. Peirce's own famous triad of categories (Firstness, Secondness, Thirdness) is a self-conscious rethinking of Kant's project of deriving the fundamental categories of thought, though Peirce grounds them in logic and the theory of signs rather than in the structure of judgment.

But Peirce reinterprets Kant by **pragmatizing** and **fallibilizing** him. Kant had sought certain, a priori foundations — synthetic a priori truths fixed once and for all by the structure of the mind. Peirce dissolves this quest for certainty. Concepts get their meaning not from a fixed transcendental structure but from their **practical effects**: the whole meaning of a conception lies in the conceivable experimental consequences it would have — the pragmatic maxim. And knowledge is not built on indubitable foundations but is **fallible**, self-correcting, converging on truth only in the long run of inquiry by a community of investigators.

Above all, Peirce rejects Kant's **thing-in-itself** — the unknowable reality behind appearances. An absolutely unknowable, he argues, is a meaningless notion: whatever is real is knowable in the long run, and reality just *is* what the endless progress of inquiry would ultimately converge upon. There is no forever-hidden noumenon.

So Peirce takes Kant's active, category-structured mind and his concern with the conditions of knowledge, and remakes them for a scientific age. The fixed transcendental categories become logical-semiotic categories; a priori certainty becomes fallible inquiry; the unknowable thing-in-itself becomes the knowable limit of investigation. Kant's critical philosophy is reborn as pragmatism — a theory of meaning and truth for a community of inquirers rather than a solitary transcendental ego.
