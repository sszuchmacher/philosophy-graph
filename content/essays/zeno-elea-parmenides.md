Parmenides had proved, by pure reasoning, that Being is one and unchanging and that motion and plurality are illusions. It was a conclusion so at odds with common sense that his opponents thought it enough simply to laugh — of course things move; watch me cross the room.

Zeno, his devoted pupil, came to the master's defense with a new weapon: not a direct proof of the One but a demolition of its rivals. If you find Parmenides absurd, he says, wait until you examine what *you* believe. Assume, as everyone does, that there are many things and that motion is real — and watch the contradictions multiply.

Take his most famous paradox. To cross the room, Achilles must first reach the halfway point; but to reach that he must reach a quarter, and before that an eighth, without end. To traverse any distance he must complete infinitely many steps — which is impossible. So motion cannot even begin.

> If things are many, they must be both like and unlike — and that is impossible.

Zeno's method is **reductio ad absurdum**, and he is arguably its inventor: rather than defend your own thesis, take your opponent's and drive it to contradiction. The paradoxes are not idle tricks. They expose how deeply our ordinary notions of space, time, and infinity resist analysis — puzzles that would wait until the calculus and modern set theory for their dissolution. Behind the games stands a loyal purpose: to leave Parmenidean Being the only refuge still standing.
