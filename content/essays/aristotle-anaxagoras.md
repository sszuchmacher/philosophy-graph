Of all his predecessors, Anaxagoras gave Aristotle the most tantalizing idea and the sharpest disappointment. Anaxagoras had declared that **Nous** — Mind — is the cause that orders the cosmos, the one thing unmixed with everything else, setting the primal jumble into rational motion. Here, at last, seemed to be a thinker who grasped that intelligence, not chance or blind necessity, governs the world.

Aristotle records the letdown, echoing a complaint Plato had voiced before him. Having proclaimed Mind the principle of order, Anaxagoras hardly *uses* it. When it comes to explaining particular events he falls back on mechanical causes — vortices, mixtures, the push and pull of stuffs — invoking Mind only as a last resort, like a *deus ex machina*, when no physical cause is at hand. He named the right principle and then failed to let it do any work.

> He uses Mind as a device for producing the world-order, and drags it in whenever he is at a loss, but otherwise makes anything rather than Mind the cause.

The reproach reveals what Aristotle himself demands: a Mind that orders *for the sake of the good*, a genuine **final cause** running through nature, not a first shove followed by mechanism. Anaxagoras had glimpsed teleology and flinched from it.

Yet the praise is real — Aristotle calls him the one sober man among babblers for naming Mind at all. The relationship is exemplary of Aristotle's whole method with his forebears: gratefully harvest the true insight — that the cosmos is intelligible and ordered — while exposing exactly where the predecessor stopped short of carrying it through.
