Paul Feyerabend was Kuhn's friend and interlocutor — they discussed *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* in draft — and Feyerabend took Kuhn's historical insights and pushed them to an anarchic extreme that Kuhn himself found alarming. Where Kuhn described how science actually works, Feyerabend drew radical, subversive conclusions about method and authority.

From Kuhn, Feyerabend took several key ideas. First, the lesson from the **history of science**: that science does not in fact conform to the tidy methodological rules the philosophers prescribe, and that any adequate philosophy of science must attend to what scientists have really done. Second, the concept of **incommensurability** — that rival theories can be so different in their concepts and standards that there is no neutral common measure between them. Feyerabend developed his own version of incommensurability and made it central to his attack on the idea of steady, cumulative progress and on the notion that theories can simply be compared and the better one chosen by shared criteria.

But Feyerabend radicalizes Kuhn in two provocative directions. First, on **method**: Kuhn had shown that science does not follow the falsificationist rules; Feyerabend concludes, far more sweepingly, that there are *no* fixed methodological rules that science does or should follow — his notorious "**anything goes**." Studying the actual history (Galileo above all), he argues that every proposed rule of scientific method has, at some point, been productively *violated*, and that progress often required breaking the rules. There is no method, and imposing one would only strangle science.

Second, and more sharply against Kuhn, Feyerabend **rejects Kuhn's valorization of normal science**. Kuhn had described — and, Feyerabend thought, seemed to *approve* — the long periods of dogmatic puzzle-solving within a single paradigm. Feyerabend recoils: normal science, with its conformism and its suppression of alternatives, is for him intellectually stultifying, even totalitarian. He champions instead a permanent **proliferation** of competing theories and a **theoretical pluralism** — the deliberate multiplication of rival views — as the true engine of critical progress. Where Kuhn saw normal science as necessary and productive, Feyerabend saw it as a threat to the freedom and creativity that science needs.

So Feyerabend's relation to Kuhn is a radicalization that turns Kuhn's descriptions into anarchist prescriptions. He takes Kuhn's history-based critique of method and his incommensurability and pushes them to "anything goes" and a celebration of theoretical proliferation — while rejecting Kuhn's apparent endorsement of normal science as conformist and repressive. Kuhn described science as alternating between dogmatic normal periods and revolutions; Feyerabend concluded that there are no binding rules at all, that pluralism and rule-breaking drive knowledge, and that the disciplined "normal science" Kuhn described is something to be resisted rather than admired.
