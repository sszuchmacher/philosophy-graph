Moore's *Principia Ethica* opens modern analytic ethics with an attack on the whole utilitarian tradition of Bentham and Mill — an attack summed up in a phrase that became famous: the **naturalistic fallacy**.

Bentham had defined the good in **natural** terms: the good is pleasure and the absence of pain, and the right action is the one that maximizes pleasure. Goodness, on this view, just *is* a natural property — pleasantness — and ethics becomes the empirical science of maximizing it. Mill continued this, defining the desirable in terms of what is desired.

Moore argues that any such definition commits a fallacy. Whatever natural property you identify goodness with — pleasure, or being desired, or conducing to survival — you can always intelligibly ask: "but is *that* good?" If "good" simply *meant* "pleasant," then "is pleasure good?" would be a trivial, closed question, like "is pleasure pleasant?" — but it is not; it is an **open question**, a real question anyone can sensibly ask. This shows that "good" does not mean "pleasant," nor any other natural property. Goodness cannot be defined in natural terms.

His positive conclusion is that **good is simple and indefinable** — a unique, non-natural property, grasped by a kind of moral intuition, not analyzable into anything else, as "yellow" cannot be defined but only pointed to. To try to define it in terms of natural facts is to commit the naturalistic fallacy and to confuse the question "what things are good?" with the question "what does good *mean*?"

The critique reshaped ethics. By severing "good" from any natural definition, Moore drove a wedge between fact and value (reinforcing Hume's is/ought gap), undercut the utilitarian attempt to make ethics an empirical science of pleasure, and set the agenda for a century of meta-ethics — the study not of what is good but of what moral words *mean* and whether moral claims can be true at all.

So Moore's relation to Bentham is a foundational critique that turns ethics inward. He accepts neither Bentham's hedonism nor his naturalism, charging that the very project of defining goodness as a natural property rests on a logical mistake. Where Bentham made the good a measurable natural quantity, Moore makes it a simple, indefinable, non-natural object of intuition — transforming ethics from a science of maximizing pleasure into an analysis of the meaning of moral concepts.
