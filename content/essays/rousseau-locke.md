Locke had given private property its great modern justification. In the state of nature, he argued, when a man mixes his **labor** with the unowned things of the earth — tilling a field, gathering an acorn — he makes them his own; property is a natural right, prior to government, and the very purpose of government is to protect it. Property is legitimate, natural, and good.

Rousseau turns this into the story of humanity's fall. In the *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality*, he identifies the invention of property not as a natural right but as the **original catastrophe** — the source of inequality, greed, and the corruption of the human race.

> The first man who, having enclosed a piece of ground, bethought himself of saying "This is mine," and found people simple enough to believe him, was the real founder of civil society.

From that first enclosure, Rousseau writes, flowed "crimes, wars, murders... horrors and misfortunes." What Locke celebrated as the natural extension of the self through labor, Rousseau reads as a usurpation that, once accepted, generated ranks of rich and poor, master and servant, and the whole machinery of domination. And he adds a bitter twist: the state, which Locke said exists to *protect* property, is really a fraud instituted by the rich to secure their unjust holdings and give inequality the sanction of law.

The critique cuts to the moral foundation of liberal politics. Locke's labor theory made property the ground of rights and the guarantor of freedom; Rousseau makes it the ground of servitude and the death of natural equality. Where Locke's individual becomes free and secure by acquiring, Rousseau's becomes enslaved by it — bound to endless comparison, envy, and dependence.

This is one of the deepest forks in modern political thought. From Locke descend the liberal traditions that treat property as a bulwark of liberty; from Rousseau's indictment descend the egalitarian and socialist traditions that treat it as the origin of injustice — a line that runs directly toward Marx.
