Arendt's political philosophy is a modern recovery of **Aristotle** — the Aristotle for whom the human being is a political animal and political life the highest sphere of human freedom and excellence. In an age that had reduced politics to administration and violence, Arendt returned to the Greek *polis*, largely through Aristotle, to recover what politics could be.

From Aristotle, Arendt takes the foundational conviction that action in the public realm, among equals, is the distinctively human and free activity — that the human being is realized not in private life or mere survival but in the shared political world. Aristotle's *zōon politikon*, the political animal, and his account of the *polis* as the community in which humans achieve their highest good, stand behind her celebration of the public realm as the space of freedom and disclosure. She also draws on Aristotle's *zōon logon echon*, the being who has speech — for Arendt, action and **speech** together are the medium of politics, the way distinct individuals appear to and act with one another.

Her most important borrowing is Aristotle's distinction between kinds of human activity. Aristotle had distinguished *praxis* (action, done for its own sake) from *poiēsis* (making, aimed at an external product), ranking the life of action and contemplation above mere labor for necessity. Arendt develops this into her tripartite scheme in *The Human Condition*: **labor** (meeting biological necessity), **work** (fabricating the durable artificial world), and **action** (the free deed and word among plural others) — with action, the heir of Aristotelian *praxis*, as the properly political and free activity.

But this is reinterpretation, not repetition. Arendt reads Aristotle through the catastrophes of the twentieth century and against the modern eclipse of action. She sharpens and modernizes the Greek distinctions, gives natality and plurality a centrality Aristotle did not, and mourns the modern world's inversion of the ancient hierarchy — its exaltation of labor and production and its loss of the space for genuine political action. She also drops Aristotle's teleological metaphysics and his exclusions, keeping the phenomenology of action while leaving behind the biology and cosmology.

So Arendt reinterprets Aristotle by recovering his vision of political action as the highest human freedom and his distinctions among human activities, and rebuilding them into a modern phenomenology of the *vita activa*. Aristotle gave her the political animal, the primacy of action and speech, and the distinction of *praxis* from *poiēsis*; Arendt made these the basis of a diagnosis of modernity's loss of politics and a call to recover the public realm of free action among equals.
