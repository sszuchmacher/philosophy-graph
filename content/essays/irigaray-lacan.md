Luce Irigaray trained as a psychoanalyst within the Lacanian school — and was expelled from it after her *Speculum of the Other Woman* (1974) turned Lacanian theory into an indictment of the very tradition Lacan represented. Her relation to Lacan is a critique from within psychoanalysis, using its tools against its masculine bias.

Lacan had made the **phallus** the central signifier — the master signifier around which the Symbolic order, language, and desire are organized. In his account, entry into language and subjectivity is structured by the phallus; woman is defined in relation to it, in terms of *lack* (she "is" the phallus for the other, or lacks it), and Lacan notoriously wrote that "The woman does not exist" — that Woman has no place as a full subject in the Symbolic, only as a fantasy or a lack.

Irigaray turns this from a description into an **accusation**. Lacanian psychoanalysis, she argues, does not neutrally describe the structure of subjectivity; it reproduces and enshrines the **phallocentrism** (she coins "phallogocentrism," fusing it with Derrida's logocentrism) of the entire Western tradition. By making the phallus the one signifier and defining woman only as lack or mirror, Lacan represents woman solely from a masculine standpoint, as the negative or complement of the male subject. Woman is never a subject in her own right, only the "other" of the same — the mirror (the *speculum*) in which man sees himself reflected. Female sexuality and subjectivity are defined entirely in male terms.

Against this she calls for a femininity and a female desire articulated on their **own** terms, not as lack relative to the phallus. Her famous image — "this sex which is not one" — plays on the idea that female sexuality is not singular (not organized around a single organ or signifier like the phallus) but plural, multiple, diffuse. Female desire and subjectivity exceed the phallic economy; they are irreducibly other, and cannot be captured by a system built on the one masculine signifier.

So Irigaray's relation to Lacan is a critique that wields psychoanalysis against itself. She accepts that the Symbolic order and Western thought are organized around the phallus — but reads this not as a truth to be accepted but as the mark of a pervasive masculine bias to be exposed and overcome. Where Lacan defined woman as lack in relation to the phallic signifier, Irigaray insists on a female subjectivity and desire that are plural, self-defined, and irreducible to the masculine economy. Lacan gave her the analysis of the phallocentric Symbolic; Irigaray turned it into a feminist indictment and a demand for a language and desire of one's own.
