Few philosophical hatreds were fiercer than Schopenhauer's for Hegel. Convinced his own work was ignored while Hegel's held the German universities in thrall, Schopenhauer scheduled his Berlin lectures at the same hour as Hegel's — and lectured to an empty room. He never forgave it, and denounced Hegel as a "charlatan" who wrote "sheer nonsense" in impenetrable jargon to dazzle the gullible.

Beneath the venom lies a real philosophical opposition, centered on the role of **reason** in reality. Hegel had made reason the very substance of the world: "what is rational is actual," history is the rational self-development of Spirit toward freedom, and even the horrors of history serve the **cunning of reason** (*List der Vernunft*), which uses human passions and catastrophes as instruments of its rational progress. Reality is fundamentally rational, and unfolds toward a good and intelligible end.

Schopenhauer denies every part of this. At the heart of the world is not reason but blind, irrational **Will** — purposeless striving with no goal, no plan, no progress. There is no cunning of reason guiding history to freedom; there is only endless craving, conflict, and suffering, signifying nothing. Where Hegel saw the march of Spirit toward self-conscious freedom, Schopenhauer sees a meaningless churn of desire that would be better never to have begun. History is not the biography of Reason but a record of misery without redemption in this world.

The clash is thus between the most optimistic and the most pessimistic of the post-Kantians. Hegel's cosmos is rational, developmental, and ultimately affirmative; Schopenhauer's is irrational, essentially suffering, and calls for *denial* of the will rather than triumphant fulfillment.

The critique proved prophetic. As faith in Hegelian system and rational progress waned later in the century, Schopenhauer's dark counter-vision — reality as blind will, existence as suffering, reason as a mere tool of deeper irrational forces — surged in influence, feeding into Nietzsche, Freud, and the whole modern suspicion that beneath our rationality lies something unreasoning and unruly.
