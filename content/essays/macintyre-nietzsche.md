MacIntyre's *After Virtue* frames its whole argument as a stark choice: "**Nietzsche or Aristotle?**" Nietzsche is, for MacIntyre, the indispensable antagonist — the thinker who saw most clearly the bankruptcy of modern morality, and whose challenge can be answered only by the Aristotelian tradition Nietzsche himself rejected.

MacIntyre grants Nietzsche a devastating **partial victory**. The Enlightenment tried to provide a rational justification for morality — a secular grounding for inherited moral rules — after abandoning the teleological framework (the human *telos*, the good life) that had once given those rules their point. This project, MacIntyre argues, *failed*: the various attempts (Kantian, utilitarian) could not agree and could not succeed, leaving modern moral discourse a chaos of incommensurable fragments. And Nietzsche, MacIntyre says, was the philosopher who **saw this failure most honestly**. Nietzsche recognized that the moral "objectivity" the Enlightenment claimed was a sham — that behind supposedly rational, universal moral rules lie only the will to power and the disguised preferences and resentments of particular people. His genealogical unmasking of morality is, MacIntyre concedes, the *correct diagnosis* of what Enlightenment morality had become: mere assertion dressed up as reason, "emotivism" with a rational alibi.

But MacIntyre argues that Nietzsche's victory is only over the **modern** morality that had already abandoned its Aristotelian foundations — and that Nietzsche wrongly took this to be a refutation of *all* morality. Here is MacIntyre's decisive counter: Nietzsche's critique succeeds against the rootless moral fragments of modernity, but it does *not* touch a morality grounded in the Aristotelian tradition of the virtues, practices, and the human good. If that tradition can be rationally vindicated — if there really is a human *telos* and virtues that lead to it — then morality is not merely disguised will to power, and Nietzsche's unmasking, however apt for the Enlightenment's ruins, misses its mark.

So MacIntyre's relation to Nietzsche is a critique that turns him into the great either/or of moral philosophy. He accepts Nietzsche's diagnosis — that modern morality, having cut itself off from any conception of the human good, is rationally bankrupt and reducible to the assertion of will — as a correct verdict on the Enlightenment project. But he rejects Nietzsche's conclusion that morality as such is thereby exposed as a fraud, arguing that the failure is the failure of *modern* morality specifically, and that the Aristotelian tradition Nietzsche never seriously confronted offers a coherent alternative. The choice, for MacIntyre, is finally "Nietzsche or Aristotle": either the honest nihilism that follows from modernity's collapse, or the recovery of the tradition of the virtues.
