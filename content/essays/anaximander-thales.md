Thales had asked the decisive question — what is the single source from which all things come? — and answered: **water**. Everything, he thought, is ultimately a transformation of the moist. It was a stunning move: for the first time the world was explained not by the doings of gods but by a natural principle.

Anaximander, his younger associate, kept the question and rejected the answer. His objection was elegant. If the source of all things were itself one of the things — water, say — it would be at war with its opposites. Water is cold and wet; how could it give rise to fire, hot and dry, without being consumed in the process? Any definite element, made the origin, would tip the balance and swallow the rest.

So the source must be **indefinite** — the *apeiron*, the boundless. Neither wet nor dry, hot nor cold, it holds all opposites without being any of them, and out of it they eternally separate and to it they return.

> ...and into that from which existing things come to be, they also pass away, according to necessity.

With that sentence — the oldest surviving fragment of Western philosophy — Anaximander does something new. He does not merely swap one stuff for another; he grasps that a first principle must be *more abstract* than anything it explains. The demand for an origin has become the demand for something that transcends the ordinary furniture of the world.
