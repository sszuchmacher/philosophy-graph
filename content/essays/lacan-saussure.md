Jacques Lacan's celebrated slogan — that "the unconscious is structured like a language" — is, at bottom, the importing of **Saussure** into psychoanalysis. Lacan's "return to Freud" was carried out with the tools of structural linguistics, and Saussure's theory of the sign is the key to his rereading of Freud.

From Saussure, Lacan takes the concept of the **sign** as the union of a **signifier** (the sound-image, the material mark) and a **signified** (the concept). But he transforms it decisively. Saussure had pictured signifier and signified as two sides of a sheet of paper, joined in a stable unit. Lacan breaks them apart and asserts the **primacy of the signifier**: the signifier dominates, and meaning (the signified) is not fixed but slides beneath an endless chain of signifiers, never pinned down. Meaning is produced by the movement and relations among signifiers, not by their attachment to stable concepts or things.

This lets Lacan reread Freud's mechanisms of the **unconscious** as linguistic operations. Freud's *condensation* and *displacement* — the ways the dream-work disguises desire — Lacan identifies with the classic tropes of **metaphor** (one signifier substituted for another) and **metonymy** (meaning sliding along a chain of associated signifiers). The unconscious, then, is not a cauldron of biological instincts but a **structure of signifiers**, operating according to the laws of language; it is "structured like a language," organized by the same differential logic Saussure found in speech.

And Lacan makes the **Symbolic order** — the whole system of language, law, and social convention, a Saussurean system of differences — one of his three fundamental registers, into which the human subject is inserted and by which it is constituted. The subject is not the master of language but is "spoken" by it, subjected to the signifier; we are born into a pre-existing symbolic system that shapes desire and selfhood.

So Lacan continues Saussure by making structural linguistics the very grammar of the unconscious and the subject. He takes Saussure's signifier/signified and system of differences, tilts them toward the sovereignty of the sliding signifier, and uses them to rewrite Freud: the unconscious as language, its mechanisms as metaphor and metonymy, the subject as constituted by its subjection to the Symbolic order. Structuralism, which Lévi-Strauss carried into anthropology, Lacan carried into the depths of the psyche — making Saussure's linguistics the unexpected key to the Freudian unconscious.
