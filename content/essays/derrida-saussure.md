Derrida's deconstruction takes structuralism's own founding text — Saussure's linguistics — and turns its central insight against structuralism itself, radicalizing Saussure to a conclusion he never drew and would have resisted.

From Saussure, Derrida takes the crucial thesis that meaning is **differential**: signs have value not through any positive content or natural link to things but only through their **differences** from other signs. "In language there are only differences without positive terms" — Saussure's own words. Derrida seizes on this and pushes it relentlessly. If meaning is nothing but difference, then no sign is ever fully present or self-identical; each is constituted by its relation to what it is not, by the traces of other signs within it. Meaning is never simply *there*; it is the effect of an endless play of differences. This is the germ of Derrida's **différance** — difference plus deferral: meaning is not only differential but perpetually postponed, never arriving at a final, present signified.

But Derrida then uses this radicalized Saussureanism to expose a **contradiction in Saussure himself**. For all his differential principle, Saussure remained committed to the metaphysics of presence at two points. First, he privileged **speech over writing**, treating the spoken word as the authentic, primary form of language, close to the presence of the speaker's thought, and writing as a mere derivative, external representation. Derrida calls this **phonocentrism** and argues it is unjustified: the very features Saussure uses to define the sign — difference, the trace, repeatability, the arbitrary and non-present relation of signifier and signified — are the features of *writing* in Derrida's enlarged sense. Writing — the trace, spacing, difference — is therefore not secondary to speech but names the general condition of all signification, speech included. He inverts the hierarchy: "arche-writing" comes first.

Second, Saussure still imagined signifier and signified as joined in a relatively stable unit. Derrida's differential radicalization dissolves that stability: the signified is never present, only endlessly deferred along chains of signifiers.

So Derrida radicalizes Saussure by taking the principle of pure difference more seriously than Saussure did, and turning it against Saussure's own residual metaphysics — his phonocentrism and his stable sign. He keeps and intensifies the differential theory of meaning while overturning the privilege of speech and the stability of the signified. Structuralism had used Saussure to find fixed underlying structures; Derrida uses Saussure's deepest premise to show that there are no fixed structures at all — only the ceaseless play of difference and the trace, with presence and the stable sign exposed as illusions the tradition, and Saussure with it, could not give up.
