Empedocles had reduced the world to four roots endlessly mixed and unmixed. It was tidy — perhaps too tidy. Anaxagoras pressed a hard question: how can hair come from what is not hair, or flesh from what is not flesh? If you eat bread and it becomes blood, bone, and sinew, where were the blood and bone before?

His answer radicalizes Empedocles by abolishing the very idea of a few pure elements. **There is a portion of everything in everything.** In each thing, however small, are seeds of all the rest — bread already contains blood and bone, which is why it can nourish them. Things differ not because they are made of different elements but because *different ingredients predominate* in them.

Matter, on this view, is infinitely divisible, and every division still contains everything. There is no bottom, no simple stuff.

But if all is mixed with all, what sorts the mixture into a cosmos? Here Anaxagoras introduces the idea that would echo for centuries: **Nous**, Mind — alone unmixed with anything — which sets the primordial blend spinning and thereby orders it.

> All things were together; then Mind came and set them in order.

It was Nous that thrilled the young Socrates and Aristotle, and then disappointed them, for Anaxagoras used Mind only to start the vortex and explained the rest mechanically. Still, the move is momentous: the first time intelligence, rather than another stuff, is named as the ordering principle of the world.
