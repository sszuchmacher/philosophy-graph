Heidegger's radical philosophy of Being grew, paradoxically, out of the most intense engagement with Aristotle of any twentieth-century thinker. For years he lectured on Aristotle line by line, and he said it was Aristotle — specifically the question "what is being?" — that first set him on his lifelong path. *Being and Time* is unimaginable without this ancient root.

From Aristotle, Heidegger takes above all the **question of Being** itself. Aristotle had defined first philosophy as the science of "being *qua* being" and observed that "being is said in many ways." Heidegger seizes on this: the question of the meaning of Being is *the* question, and Aristotle was one of the last to feel its full force before the tradition let it fall into oblivion. His whole project is announced as a **retrieval** of a question the Greeks opened and the West forgot.

He also mines specific Aristotelian analyses and reads them against the later tradition. From Aristotle's practical philosophy — especially *phronēsis*, practical wisdom — Heidegger draws his account of how Dasein primarily understands the world through *engaged, practical dealing* rather than detached theoretical staring. His famous analysis of equipment — the hammer known "ready-to-hand" in use, not "present-at-hand" as an object of contemplation — is deeply Aristotelian, privileging practical involvement over theoretical cognition. Aristotle's *alētheia*, truth as unconcealment, becomes central to Heidegger's own conception of truth as the disclosure of beings.

But this is **reinterpretation**, even violence, in the name of retrieval. Heidegger reads Aristotle *against the grain* of the metaphysical tradition Aristotle himself founded. That later tradition, he argues, took Aristotle's insights and froze them into a metaphysics of substances and present-at-hand objects — "being" understood as fixed, enduring presence. Heidegger goes back behind that ossification to recover what he takes to be Aristotle's more originary experience of Being as a happening, a coming-to-presence bound up with time.

So Heidegger's engagement is a destruction-and-retrieval: he dismantles the standard, textbook Aristotle — father of substance-metaphysics and formal logic — in order to recover a hidden, more radical Aristotle who still felt the living question of Being. The most future-oriented philosophy of the century is built by digging back through the tradition to its Greek source and reading Aristotle more originally than the Aristotelians ever had.
