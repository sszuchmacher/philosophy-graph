Derrida's earliest work was on Husserl — his first books were an introduction to Husserl's *Origin of Geometry* and the study *Speech and Phenomena* — and it was through a meticulous **critique of Husserl** that deconstruction was born. Phenomenology was the rigorous philosophy Derrida had to work through and undo from within.

Derrida honors Husserl's rigor and shares the phenomenological attention to how meaning is constituted. But he identifies at the heart of Husserl's project the very thing he calls the **metaphysics of presence** — and shows, by close reading, that Husserl's own analyses undermine it.

His central target is Husserl's privileging of the **living present** and of **self-presence**. Husserl had sought an absolute foundation in the immediate self-presence of consciousness to itself — the pure, punctual "now" in which meaning is directly present to the mind, and the inner voice in which the speaking subject hears itself think in a moment of undivided presence ("hearing-oneself-speak"). This self-present interior experience, prior to any external sign, was to be the bedrock of meaning.

Derrida argues, from Husserl's own descriptions, that this pure presence is impossible. Husserl's analysis of **time-consciousness** shows that the "now" is never simple: every present moment is already shot through with "retention" (the just-past) and "protention" (the about-to-come), so the present is constituted by what it is *not*, divided from itself by traces of past and future. Self-presence is thus always already contaminated by difference and deferral — by what Derrida names **différance**. Likewise, Husserl's attempt to separate a pure inner "expression" from the external, sensible "sign" collapses: meaning always depends on signs, on repeatability, on the trace — on a structure Husserl wanted to keep outside the pure interior of consciousness.

The consequence is that Husserl's foundation dissolves. There is no absolutely self-present consciousness grounding meaning; the sign, difference, absence, and repetition are there from the start, at the supposed origin. What Husserl treated as secondary and external — writing, the sign, mediation — turns out to be the condition of the very interiority he privileged.

So Derrida's relation to Husserl is a founding **critique** from within. He takes phenomenology's most rigorous attempt to ground meaning in the self-presence of consciousness and shows, using Husserl's own analyses of time and signs, that this self-presence is always already broken by difference and the trace. Out of this immanent undoing of Husserl's foundationalism, deconstruction and the concept of différance emerge — the demonstration that presence is never pure, that the origin is always already divided, and that the whole metaphysics of presence Husserl exemplified cannot deliver the pure ground it seeks.
