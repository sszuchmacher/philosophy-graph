Bergson's philosophy defines itself against Kant on the question of what the intellect can and cannot know. Kant had drawn strict limits to knowledge: we can know only **appearances**, structured by our forms of intuition (space and time) and the categories; the thing-in-itself, reality as it is in itself, lies forever beyond our reach. Metaphysics, the attempt to know reality directly, is impossible.

Bergson accepts that the **intellect** is indeed limited in something like Kant's way — but he denies Kant's conclusion, because he denies that the intellect is our only faculty of knowledge. The key to his critique is his analysis of **time**. Kant had treated time as a *form of intuition*, a subjective framework the mind imposes, and had modeled it on space — as a homogeneous medium of juxtaposed instants. Bergson argues this is a falsification. Real time, **duration**, is not spatial or homogeneous; it is a qualitative, interpenetrating, flowing continuity that the intellect — whose business is to carve the world into fixed, spatial units for action — systematically distorts.

The intellect, then, gives us not reality itself but reality *spatialized* — chopped into static, external, measurable pieces suited to practical manipulation. Kant was right that this conceptual knowledge cannot reach the absolute. But he was wrong to think it is our only access. Alongside the intellect, Bergson claims, we possess **intuition** — a sympathetic, immediate coincidence with the inner flow of reality, first available in our direct acquaintance with our own duration, our own conscious life lived from within.

Through intuition we *can* grasp the absolute that Kant declared unknowable — not by concepts, which spatialize and externalize, but by entering into the very movement of things. Metaphysics, impossible for Kant, becomes possible again: the effort of intuition to place itself within reality's creative flow rather than viewing it from outside through the distorting grid of the intellect.

So Bergson turns Kant's limitation of knowledge into a limitation of the *intellect* only, and reopens the door Kant had shut. He keeps the Kantian point that conceptual, scientific knowledge does not reach things in themselves, while insisting that a different faculty — intuition, attuned to duration — restores our contact with the absolute.
