Alongside Saussure, the other great presence in Lacan's thought is **Hegel** — the Hegel of Alexandre Kojève's electrifying Paris lectures on the *Phenomenology*, which Lacan attended and which shaped his entire generation. From Hegel, by way of Kojève, Lacan drew his account of **desire** and **recognition**.

The central borrowing is the Hegelian thesis that human **desire is desire for recognition** — and, more radically in Kojève's reading, that "desire is desire of the Other's desire." What the human being fundamentally wants is not an object but to be desired, valued, recognized by another self-consciousness. Lacan makes this the cornerstone of his theory: human desire is never simply for a thing; it is always mediated by the Other, structured by what we imagine the Other wants, a desire to be the object of the Other's desire. We desire through and for the Other.

Lacan also draws on Hegel's dialectic of self-consciousness — the **master-slave** struggle for recognition — and on the constitutive role of the **Other** in the formation of the self. His famous **mirror stage**, in which the infant forms an identity by identifying with an external image (its reflection, and the gaze of others), is deeply Hegelian: the self is not given but constituted through an alienating relation to something outside it. The ego is founded on a méconnaissance, a misrecognition, in a structure that echoes Hegel's account of self-consciousness coming to itself only through another.

And Lacan inherits the Hegelian emphasis on **negativity** and lack — the idea that the human subject is defined by a fundamental absence, a restless want that can never be filled, which he renders as the constitutive "lack" at the heart of desire.

But Lacan, like the structuralists generally, ultimately **de-centers** the Hegelian subject. Where Hegel's dialectic of recognition drives toward reconciliation, a fulfilled self-consciousness, and finally Absolute Spirit knowing itself, Lacan denies any such resolution. The subject is permanently split, alienated in the Symbolic and the image, its desire structurally unsatisfiable; there is no synthesis, no coming-home of the self to itself. The Hegelian journey toward reconciled self-knowledge becomes, in Lacan, an endless circling around a lack that can never be overcome.

So Lacan continues Hegel by making desire-as-recognition and the constitution of the self through the Other central to psychoanalysis — inheriting, through Kojève, the dialectic of recognition, the role of negativity and lack, and the alienated genesis of the self. But he strips away Hegel's reconciling telos, leaving a subject Hegelian in its structure of desire yet condemned, unlike Hegel's, never to arrive.
