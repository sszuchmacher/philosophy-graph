Heraclitus had made change the truth of things. You cannot step twice into the same river; reality is a ceaseless flowing, a war of opposites held in tension by a hidden *logos*. To be is to become.

Parmenides answered with the most audacious argument in early philosophy — and reached the opposite conclusion. Begin, he said, from a single principle: **what is, is; what is not, is not, and cannot even be thought.** Nothing comes from nothing. But every change means something coming to be from what it was not — and that is impossible. Therefore change is impossible.

If the reasoning holds, the Heraclitean world of flux is not the truth but an illusion. What truly *is* must be ungenerated, indestructible, one, and unmoving — for to move or alter would be to pass into what is not.

> For never shall this be proved, that things that are not are.

The clash could not be sharper. For Heraclitus, whoever thinks reality stands still has missed everything; for Parmenides, whoever trusts the flowing world of the senses has been deceived by mere opinion, and only reason, following the argument wherever it leads, touches Being.

Greek philosophy would spend the next two centuries trying to escape the fork. The pluralists, the atomists, and finally Plato and Aristotle all take their bearings from this quarrel between the one who said everything moves and the one who proved that nothing can.
