Aristotle's universe is saturated with purpose. Every natural thing has an end, a *telos*, toward which it strives; nature does nothing in vain; and the whole cosmos is drawn, like a lover toward the beloved, toward the unmoved mover. To understand anything is to grasp what it is *for*.

Epicurus rejects the entire framework. In a world of **atoms and void** there are no ends, no purposes, no cosmic striving — only particles falling, swerving, and colliding according to their weights and shapes. Where Aristotle sees an eye made *for* seeing, Epicurus sees a structure that happened to form and happened to be useful; function follows from the arrangement, it does not guide it.

The stakes are practical, not merely cosmological. Aristotelian teleology tends to underwrite a providential order — a nature that aims at the good, a place for everything. That is precisely the comforting picture Epicurus wants to dismantle, because it feeds the fear that the universe is watching and judging. A purposeless cosmos is, for him, a *liberating* one.

He rejects Aristotle's fifth element and eternal heavens too: the stars are not divine, imperishable beings but natural bodies, and the cosmos itself is one of infinitely many that come to be and pass away. Nothing is sacred; nothing is aimed.

The disagreement runs to the root of what explanation *is*. For Aristotle, to explain is to give the final cause; for Epicurus, invoking purposes explains nothing and only smuggles the gods back into physics. Two millennia later it is Epicurus's purpose-free nature, not Aristotle's teleology, that modern science will make its own.
