Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) offered a picture of science so different from **Popper's** that their confrontation became the defining debate in twentieth-century philosophy of science — a clash between the *logic* of science and its *history*.

Popper had made **falsification** the essence of science: scientists propose bold conjectures and try to refute them, and a genuine scientist abandons a theory when it is contradicted by evidence. Science advances by the relentless, critical elimination of error; the good scientist is a permanent revolutionary, always trying to overthrow the reigning theory.

Kuhn, looking at the actual **history** of science, argues that this is not how science mostly works — and that if scientists behaved as Popper prescribed, science could not function. Most science, Kuhn observes, is "**normal science**": the patient, puzzle-solving work conducted *within* an accepted framework — a **paradigm** — which scientists do *not* try to refute but take for granted as the basis of their research. During normal science, anomalies (apparent falsifications) are not treated as refutations that overthrow the paradigm; they are set aside, explained away, or filed as unsolved puzzles, because a paradigm is abandoned only when a *better* one is available. Scientists cling to their framework in the face of contrary evidence — and, Kuhn argues, they are *right* to, since constant wholesale doubt would make sustained research impossible.

Only in periods of **crisis**, when anomalies accumulate intolerably, does a **scientific revolution** occur — a wholesale shift from one paradigm to another. And this shift, Kuhn controversially argues, is not the purely rational, evidence-driven affair Popper's model implies. Rival paradigms are to some degree **incommensurable** — they carry different concepts, standards, and even perceptions, so there is no neutral, shared body of evidence by which one is simply proven superior. The change has the character of a "conversion," involving persuasion and generational change more than a clean refutation.

This threatened Popper's rationalist picture at its core. Where Popper saw continuous critical testing and rational theory-choice governed by falsification, Kuhn saw long stretches of dogmatic normal science punctuated by non-fully-rational revolutions between incommensurable worldviews. To Popper and his followers, Kuhn's account made science look irrational; Popper insisted that Kuhn's "normal scientist" — uncritically working within a framework — was precisely the *bad*, uncritical scientist.

So Kuhn's relation to Popper is a historically grounded critique that reshaped the philosophy of science. Against Popper's picture of science as continuous falsification and permanent revolution, Kuhn set the actual pattern of normal science within paradigms, the resistance of frameworks to refutation, and the incommensurable, not-fully-rational character of revolutionary change. Popper described how he thought science *ought* rationally to proceed; Kuhn described how, historically, it *has* proceeded — and their disagreement became the central axis around which later philosophy of science would organize itself.
