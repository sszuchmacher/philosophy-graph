Anaximander had pushed the first principle beyond all the elements into the boundless *apeiron*. It was a bold abstraction — perhaps too bold. His successor Anaximenes felt the loss and pulled back to something you can point to: **air**.

The worry was concreteness. The apeiron explained everything and therefore, in a sense, nothing: having no properties of its own, how could it produce a world of definite things? Anaximenes wanted a source that was both a real substance and genuinely capable of becoming everything else.

Air fit. It is nearly invisible, formless enough to seem a plausible ground of all things, yet unmistakably *something*. And crucially, Anaximenes supplied a mechanism the apeiron had lacked. Air becomes other things through **condensation and rarefaction**: rarefied, it grows warm and turns to fire; condensed, it cools into wind, then cloud, water, earth, and stone.

This is the quiet revolution in the fragment. Change is no longer a mystery but a matter of *degree* — the same stuff, more or less compressed. Quality is reduced to quantity.

In stepping back from Anaximander's abstraction, Anaximenes actually stepped forward. He kept the Milesian project — one natural principle behind all things — but added the idea that would prove more fertile than the apeiron itself: that the visible variety of the world is a single underlying substance, thickened and thinned.
