When Descartes searches for an indubitable foothold for knowledge, he finds it in the subject: *I think, therefore I am*. Everything else — the world, bodies, other people — comes to depend on the certainty of that thinking self. The world becomes, strictly speaking, what the subject *re-presents to itself*.

Heidegger does not argue that Descartes's reasoning is invalid. He does something more radical: he reads it as a **symptom**. The Cartesian gesture of placing the subject at the center is not an individual mistake but the moment when an entire epoch begins to understand the being of things as *objectivity*: to be is to be an object for a subject.

> ...to render ourselves the masters and possessors of nature.

That line from the *Discourse on the Method* announces, for Heidegger, much more than a scientific program. It announces **modern technology**, where everything that exists — rivers, forests, persons — appears as *standing reserve*, a calculable and exploitable resource. Nature ceases to be what shows itself of itself and becomes raw material waiting to be put into production.

This is why the relation is one of *diagnosis* rather than mere critique. Heidegger does not want to refute Descartes with better arguments; he wants to show that Cartesianism is the philosophical name of something that runs through us all whenever we treat the world as a stockpile of resources. Descartes did not cause technological modernity: he formulated it with a clarity that makes it recognizable.

The question Heidegger returns to us is uncomfortable: if the subject secure in itself is the source of this way of inhabiting the world, what would have to change for being to be thought otherwise?
