For Aristotle, politics was a branch of ethics. The city exists for the sake of the good life; man is by nature a political animal, and the state's purpose is to cultivate virtue and enable human flourishing. To ask how to govern was inseparable from asking what makes a life excellent.

Machiavelli severs the tie. In *The Prince* he brackets the question of the good life entirely and asks a different one: how is power actually gained, held, and lost? He announces the break almost programmatically — he will treat "the effectual truth of the matter" rather than the imagined, the way men *do* behave rather than how they ought. Many have dreamed of ideal republics; he will study politics as it is.

> A man who wishes to make a profession of goodness in everything must necessarily come to grief among so many who are not good.

The consequences are shocking to the Aristotelian tradition. The ruler must learn "how not to be good," to deploy cruelty and deceit when necessity demands, because a prince who insists on virtue among the vicious will be destroyed. Political success has its own logic, distinct from — sometimes opposed to — moral virtue. *Virtù*, for Machiavelli, is not Aristotle's moral excellence but the skill and boldness to master fortune and hold the state.

This is the birth of political realism, and of the modern separation of politics from ethics. Aristotle asked what the state is *for*; Machiavelli asks only how it *works*. He does not deny that goodness is admirable — he denies that it reliably guides ruling. Politics becomes an autonomous art, and the study of power breaks free from the study of the good.
