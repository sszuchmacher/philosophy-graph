Anselm stands at the head of scholasticism, but his roots are entirely Augustinian. He inherited from Augustine the governing conviction of medieval theology — **faith seeking understanding** (*fides quaerens intellectum*) — the idea that one begins by believing and then uses reason to understand what one already holds. "I believe so that I may understand," Anselm writes; "for unless I believe, I shall not understand."

The content is Augustinian too: the Neoplatonic God of pure being, the priority of grace, the soul's ascent toward its source, the conviction that all truth is grounded in God. Anselm's famous proofs are cast as meditations addressed to God, in the confessional, prayerful key Augustine had made the mode of Christian thought.

What Anselm adds is a new **rigor**. Where Augustine reasoned in a rhetorical, associative, biblical idiom, Anselm reasons like a logician. He wants arguments that stand on necessity alone — *sola ratione*, "by reason alone," without appeal to scripture — arguments a non-believer could in principle be made to feel the force of. His *Monologion* and *Proslogion* set out to prove God's existence and attributes as tightly linked chains of inference.

The **ontological argument** is the emblem of this shift. From the mere concept of God as "that than which nothing greater can be conceived," Anselm tries to derive God's necessary existence — a proof so purely conceptual it still provokes philosophers.

So the relationship is continuity sharpened into method. Anselm keeps Augustine's faith, his God, his goal; but he forges the tools — definition, deduction, the demand for necessary reasons — that will make theology a *science* and open the scholastic age.
