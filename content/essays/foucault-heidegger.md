Foucault made a striking confession near the end of his life: "Heidegger has always been the essential philosopher for me." It is surprising from a thinker so identified with Nietzsche and with concrete historical research, but the debt is real, if quieter than his Nietzscheanism.

What Foucault took from Heidegger is, above all, a way of thinking about the **historical conditions of knowledge and experience**. Heidegger had argued that at each epoch there is a fundamental "understanding of Being" — a basic way beings are disclosed and made intelligible — which is not chosen by individuals but forms the horizon within which everything appears, and which shifts historically. Foucault's concept of the **episteme** (in his "archaeological" period) is a close cousin: each historical era has an underlying, largely unconscious system of ordering — rules that determine what can count as knowledge, what can be said and thought — that structures all the discourses of the age and changes in deep, discontinuous ruptures. This is Heidegger's history of Being transposed from ontology into the concrete history of the human sciences.

From Heidegger too comes the critique of the **modern subject** and of the humanism that centers everything on "man." Heidegger sought to displace the sovereign Cartesian-Kantian subject; Foucault's "death of man" and his treatment of the subject as historically constituted rather than foundational extend that anti-humanist gesture.

And Foucault shares Heidegger's concern with **technology and the way modern rationality orders and enframes the world** — reducing things and people to objects of calculation and control. Heidegger's meditation on how modern technology turns everything into "standing reserve" resonates in Foucault's analyses of how disciplinary and administrative rationality orders bodies and populations.

But Foucault historicizes and de-mystifies Heidegger. Where Heidegger's history of Being remains grand, ontological, and gestures toward a poetic "other beginning," Foucault turns to the archive — the concrete documents of medicine, punishment, and sexuality — and analyzes epistemic shifts through detailed empirical genealogy. He replaces Heidegger's pathos of Being with the study of discourses and power, grounding the changing horizons of intelligibility in institutions and practices rather than in a quasi-sacred history.

So Foucault continues Heidegger by inheriting the idea that knowledge and experience rest on deep, historically shifting conditions of intelligibility, the critique of the sovereign subject, and the concern with modern technological ordering — while pulling these down from ontology into the empirical history of the human sciences and power. Heidegger gave him the sense that the very conditions of thought have a history; Foucault wrote that history in the archives.
