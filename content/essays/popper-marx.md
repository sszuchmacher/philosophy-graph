Popper's *The Open Society and Its Enemies* and *The Poverty of Historicism* made him one of the twentieth century's most influential critics of **Marx**, whom he attacked on two connected fronts: the scientific status of Marxism and the political dangers of its philosophy of history.

The first charge is that Marxism, for all its scientific pretensions, is **not scientific** — because it is not **falsifiable**. Popper's criterion of demarcation holds that a theory is scientific only if it forbids certain observations, making risky predictions that could refute it. Marxism, he argues, fails this test. Marx did make testable predictions (the increasing misery of the proletariat, revolution in the most advanced capitalist countries), and these were falsified by events. But rather than accept refutation, Marx's followers **rescued** the theory with auxiliary hypotheses — reinterpreting it to explain away every apparent counter-example, so that no possible course of history could count against it. A theory that can accommodate *any* outcome explains nothing and risks nothing; it becomes an unfalsifiable, self-sealing system — for Popper, a mark of pseudo-science.

The second, deeper charge is against Marx's **historicism** — Popper's term for the belief that history is governed by discoverable **laws** that determine its course and enable large-scale prophecy of the future (here, the inevitable collapse of capitalism and the coming of communism). Popper argues that historicism is intellectually bankrupt: there are no such laws of historical development, the course of history depends heavily on the growth of knowledge (which we cannot predict in advance), and grand historical prophecy is impossible. Trends are not laws, and to mistake them for iron necessities is a fundamental error.

Worse, historicism is **politically dangerous**. The belief that one has discovered the inevitable direction of history licenses **totalitarian** politics: those who claim to know history's necessary destination feel entitled to impose it, to sacrifice the present generation to the promised future, and to sweep aside dissent and individual rights as obstacles to the inevitable. Marx's historicism thus feeds, for Popper, the closed, totalitarian society — the enemy of the "open society" with its piecemeal reform and its respect for individual freedom and criticism.

Popper was not wholly hostile to Marx the man — he credited him with genuine moral passion and insight into the social dimension of ideas, and treated him far more respectfully than later ideologues. But the philosophy he judged a double failure.

So Popper's relation to Marx is a two-pronged critique. On the philosophy of science, he charges that Marxism evades falsification and so is not genuine science but an unfalsifiable pseudo-science. On the philosophy of history and politics, he charges that Marx's historicism — the prophecy of history's inevitable laws — is both false and a doctrinal source of totalitarianism. Against Marx's confident science of history's destination, Popper sets fallibilism, piecemeal reform, and the open society whose future is not written in advance.
