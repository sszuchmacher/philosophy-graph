The Stoics were self-consciously Socratic. Zeno came to philosophy, the story goes, by reading Xenophon's memoirs of Socrates in an Athenian bookshop and asking where such men were to be found. The whole school took its bearings from the figure of Socrates — his indifference to fortune, his conviction that virtue is knowledge, his serenity before death.

Several Socratic paradoxes become Stoic pillars. Socrates had suggested that **virtue is the only real good** and that no genuine harm can come to a good person — that it is worse to do wrong than to suffer it. The Stoics harden this into system: virtue is the *sole* good, vice the sole evil, and everything else — health, wealth, reputation, even life itself — is "indifferent." The sage, possessing virtue, is perfectly happy even on the rack.

Socrates had also held that no one does wrong willingly, that wickedness is a kind of ignorance. The Stoics agree: the passions are errors of judgment, false beliefs about what is good and bad, and virtue is the knowledge that corrects them. Get your judgments right, and right action and tranquility follow.

Above all they inherited the *image* — Socrates in prison, calmly reasoning about the soul, declining to fear what the city could do to his body. That composure, grounded in the conviction that the good man cannot really be harmed, is the Stoic ideal made flesh.

Zeno systematized what Socrates had lived. Where Socrates questioned and left conclusions open, the Stoa built a complete philosophy of logic, physics, and ethics — but its ethical core remains the Socratic wager that virtue alone suffices for happiness, now defended as doctrine.
