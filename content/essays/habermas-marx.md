Habermas began as a Marxist and never abandoned the emancipatory, critical intent of Marx — but his life's work is a thoroughgoing **reconstruction** of Marxism, revising its foundations so drastically that some deny he remains a Marxist at all. He keeps Marx's goal — a rational, free, just society, and the critique of domination — while rebuilding the theory that was to reach it.

His central revision concerns Marx's **productivist** framework. Marx had made **labor** — the transformation of nature through work — the basic category, grounding everything, including emancipation, in the development of the forces of production. Habermas argues this is one-sided. Human social life rests on *two* irreducible dimensions: **labor** (instrumental action, the mastery of nature, governed by technical rationality) and **interaction** (communicative action, the coordination of persons through language, norms, and mutual understanding). Marx, by reducing everything to labor and production, collapsed interaction into labor and so lost sight of a whole domain — communication, norms, legitimacy — that has its own logic and its own kind of rationality and pathology.

This lets Habermas rediagnose modern ills. The core disorder of advanced capitalism is not only economic exploitation but the "**colonization of the lifeworld**": the encroachment of instrumental, systemic imperatives — money and bureaucratic power — into the communicative sphere of everyday life, culture, and democratic will-formation, which ought to be coordinated by mutual understanding rather than by the market and the administrative state. Emancipation is not just the overthrow of capitalist production but the defense of undistorted communication and democratic deliberation.

Habermas also abandons other Marxian commitments: the labor theory of value, the privileged revolutionary role of the proletariat, the confidence in a single systemic collapse. In their place he puts a reformist, deliberative-democratic politics and a normative theory grounded in communication rather than in the movement of history.

So Habermas reinterprets Marx by adding what Marx's productivism left out — the dimension of communicative interaction — and rebuilding critical theory around it. He preserves Marx's aim of a society without domination and his method of ideology-critique, while replacing the primacy of labor and the economy with the primacy of communication and the lifeworld. Marxism, in his hands, becomes a theory of communicative rationality and deliberative democracy: the critique of capitalism reconceived as the defense of the lifeworld against systems, and emancipation reconceived as the achievement of inclusive, undistorted, rational discourse.
