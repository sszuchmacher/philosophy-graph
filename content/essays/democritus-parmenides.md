Parmenides had argued that Being is one, full, and unchanging, because *what is not* cannot exist — and so there can be no empty space to separate one thing from another, and no room for anything to move.

The atomists, Leucippus and Democritus, made the boldest reply in ancient thought: they simply *granted non-being existence*. **The void is real** — what is not is, no less than what is. With that single scandalous move, the Parmenidean prison opens.

For now there can be many beings after all. Each **atom** is a tiny Parmenidean One: solid, indivisible, ungenerated, unchanging, with no internal parts and no empty space within. Democritus keeps everything Parmenides demanded of Being — and then multiplies it into infinitely many such beings, scattered through the void.

Change is saved without violating the logic. Atoms never come to be or perish; they only move and combine. The whole visible world — colors, tastes, warmth, life — is nothing but atoms of various shapes hooking together and drifting apart.

> By convention sweet, by convention bitter; in reality, atoms and void.

It is a stunning reinterpretation: not a refutation of Parmenides but a pluralizing of him. Take the eternal, indivisible Being the Eleatics proved must exist, break it into countless pieces, add the empty space they forbade — and you have the first thoroughgoing materialism, a universe explained entirely by particles in motion.
