Between Mary Wollstonecraft's *Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792) and Simone de Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* (1949) lies a century and a half — and the great tradition of feminist thought that runs between them. Beauvoir stands at the culmination of the line Wollstonecraft opened, taking up her founding claims and giving them a depth the Enlightenment could not.

The shared foundation is the insistence that woman's apparent inferiority is **made, not born** — the product of upbringing, education, and social conditioning rather than of nature. Wollstonecraft had argued that the frivolity, weakness, and vanity attributed to women were manufactured by an education that trained them to please rather than to reason; give women the same rational education as men, she urged, and the supposed natural differences would vanish. Beauvoir's most famous sentence — "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman" — is the twentieth-century radicalization of exactly this insight: femininity is not a biological destiny but a construction imposed by civilization.

Both also anchor the demand for women's liberation in a **universal** ideal — the human capacity for freedom and self-determination — and insist that women be admitted to it as full subjects rather than as relative, dependent beings defined by their usefulness to men.

But Beauvoir goes far beyond Wollstonecraft. Wollstonecraft, a child of the Enlightenment, framed the issue largely as one of *reason, education, and rights*: give women rational education and civil equality, and justice is served. Beauvoir, armed with existentialism, phenomenology, Hegel, Marx, and psychoanalysis, digs beneath rights to the whole existential and social **construction** of woman as Other — the myths, the economic structures, the lived experience of the body, the complicity, the psychology of oppression.

So Beauvoir continues Wollstonecraft as the heir who inherits the founding thesis — that women are made, not born, and are owed the full status of free human beings — and carries it from an Enlightenment argument about education and rights into a sweeping existential and social account of women's oppression. She completes, with far heavier philosophical artillery, the revolution Wollstonecraft began.
