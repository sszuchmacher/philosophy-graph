Bentham found the seed of utilitarianism in Hume, and said so — reading Hume's *Treatise*, he recalled, made the scales fall from his eyes. But he took Hume's descriptive psychology of morals and turned it into something Hume never intended: a precise, calculating **standard** for legislation and reform.

From Hume, Bentham took the empiricist, this-worldly grounding of morality in **feeling** rather than reason or divine command. Hume had argued that moral distinctions arise from sentiment — that we call actions virtuous because they produce agreeable feelings, and that **utility**, an action's tendency to promote happiness and well-being, is a central source of our approval. Virtue pleases because it is useful. This naturalistic account, locating morality in human pleasure and pain rather than in eternal truths or God's will, is Bentham's starting point.

But Hume was a subtle observer, not a systematizer. He described *how* our moral sentiments actually work, noting that utility is one important source of approval among others, and he proposed no single decision-procedure for right action. Bentham hardens Hume's insight into a **principle** and a **method**: the *principle of utility* — that the right action or law is the one producing the greatest happiness of the greatest number — and the *felicific calculus*, an attempt to actually measure and sum pleasures and pains by their intensity, duration, certainty, and so on.

So the relationship is continuity as a transformation from *is* to *ought*. Hume analyzed the moral sentiments as a psychologist; Bentham converts the analysis into a reforming standard, a tool for redesigning law, punishment, and institutions to maximize aggregate happiness. What in Hume was an account of why we feel approval becomes in Bentham a demand that we *calculate* it and legislate accordingly.

The result is a whole new practical philosophy. Hume's gentle skepticism about reason and his grounding of morals in feeling become, in Bentham's hands, a confident, quantifying reformism — sentiment turned into arithmetic, and moral philosophy turned into a program for the rational redesign of society by the measure of pleasure and pain.
