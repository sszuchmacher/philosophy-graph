Foucault called himself, simply, a Nietzschean, and of all his philosophical debts Nietzsche's is the deepest. "I am simply a Nietzschean," he said late in life, and his major methods and themes are extensions of Nietzsche's.

The central inheritance is **genealogy**. Nietzsche, in *On the Genealogy of Morals*, had pioneered a way of investigating values not by asking whether they are true but by tracing their **historical emergence** from relations of power, struggle, and domination — showing that "good" and "evil" have a low, contingent origin in the will to power and *ressentiment*, not an eternal or rational foundation. Foucault takes over this method and generalizes it. His "genealogies" of the prison, of madness, of sexuality trace how our seemingly natural categories, institutions, and "truths" emerged from contingent historical struggles and relations of power — exposing as historical and constructed what presents itself as natural, necessary, and universal.

From Nietzsche too comes the intimate link between **knowledge and power**. Nietzsche had unmasked the "will to truth" as a form of the will to power, arguing that knowledge is not disinterested but bound up with domination and perspective. Foucault's signature concept, "**power/knowledge**," is the systematic development of this: forms of knowledge (the human sciences, medicine, criminology) are inseparable from systems of power; they produce the very objects they claim to describe, and power operates through the production of "truth."

Foucault also inherits Nietzsche's **anti-humanism** and suspicion of the sovereign subject. Nietzsche had dissolved the unified, self-transparent self into a play of drives and forces; Foucault announces the "death of man," treating the "subject" not as a given foundation but as something *produced* by historical practices and discourses — the effect, not the origin, of the systems he studies.

And he shares Nietzsche's rejection of a smooth, progressive, teleological **history**. Against the story of reason and liberty steadily advancing, Foucault, following Nietzsche's genealogy, presents history as discontinuous, a series of ruptures and reversals driven by struggle, with no underlying rational direction.

So Foucault continues Nietzsche as his most important twentieth-century heir. He takes genealogy, the power/knowledge nexus, the critique of the subject, and the anti-teleological view of history, and turns them into concrete historical investigations of the institutions and discourses that make us who we are. Nietzsche supplied the method and the suspicion; Foucault applied them, with archival patience, to the prisons, clinics, and confessionals in which modern power has quietly shaped the modern soul.
