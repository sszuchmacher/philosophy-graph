MacIntyre began *After Virtue* as an Aristotelian, but his thought moved, over the following years, toward **Aquinas** — and he came to describe his own position as **Thomist**. The journey from Aristotle to Aquinas completes his project: Aquinas, for MacIntyre, offers the fullest and most defensible version of the tradition of the virtues.

MacIntyre's turn to Aquinas grows from his conviction that rationality and morality are **tradition-constituted** — that there is no neutral, tradition-independent standpoint from which to judge, and that we reason always from within a particular tradition of inquiry. Among the great traditions, he came to argue, **Thomism** is the one best able to sustain rational inquiry into the human good, precisely because Aquinas himself had achieved the supreme act of traditioned reasoning: the synthesis of Aristotle with Augustinian Christianity, absorbing and surpassing both. Aquinas showed how a tradition can encounter a rival (Aristotle, newly recovered and seemingly threatening) and, rather than reject or capitulate, integrate its truths into a richer whole — a model of how traditions rationally progress by overcoming their crises.

From Aquinas, MacIntyre takes a virtue ethics that keeps the Aristotelian structure — the virtues, the human *telos*, practical reason — while grounding it more deeply. Aquinas's **natural law** provides the account of the human good and its precepts that MacIntyre thinks Aristotle's ethics needed; his integration of the theological virtues and a transcendent end enriches the merely natural flourishing of Aristotle; and his metaphysics of the good gives the tradition a foundation Aristotle's biology could not durably supply. Aquinas answers questions — about the ultimate human end, about the grounding of the virtues — that Aristotle left open.

So MacIntyre continues into Aquinas as the culmination of his recovery of the virtue tradition. Having begun by pitting Aristotle against modern moral chaos, he comes to see Aquinas — the great synthesizer who fused Aristotle with Christianity through an exemplary act of traditioned reasoning — as offering the most rationally defensible form of the tradition of the virtues, and as the model of how rational inquiry proceeds within and between traditions. Aristotle gave MacIntyre the virtues and the *telos*; Aquinas gave him natural law, a transcendent human end, and the very account of tradition-constituted rationality that MacIntyre made his own.
