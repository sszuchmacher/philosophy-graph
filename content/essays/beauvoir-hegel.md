Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* turns one of Hegel's most famous ideas into the master key of feminist analysis: the dialectic of **Self and Other**. Hegel had argued that self-consciousness constitutes itself by opposition to another — that the "I" defines itself against what it is not, and that consciousnesses meet in a struggle in which one becomes the dominating Subject and the other the subordinated.

Beauvoir applies this structure to the relation between the sexes with startling results. Man, she argues, has posited himself as the **Subject**, the essential, the absolute — and has cast woman as the **Other**, the inessential, defined always in relation to him: "He is the Subject, he is the Absolute — she is the Other." The Hegelian category of otherness becomes the deep grammar of women's oppression. Woman is not simply different; she is made *the* Other against which man affirms himself as the norm and the self.

But Beauvoir reworks Hegel to expose what makes women's situation unique and uniquely intractable. In Hegel's master-slave dialectic, the two consciousnesses struggle, and the relation is *reciprocal* and unstable — the slave, through labor and the risk of death, can win recognition, and the dialectic moves forward. Beauvoir points out that women's otherness has *not* worked this way. Women have historically failed to posit themselves as a rival Subject, have not engaged as a collective in the struggle for recognition, and have often been complicit in their own othering — bound to men by ties (of family, sex, love, economic dependence) that no slave shares with a master. Their otherness has been fixed, absolute, seemingly natural, lacking the reciprocity and forward movement of the Hegelian struggle.

So Beauvoir reinterprets Hegel by taking his abstract dialectic of Self and Other and grounding it in the concrete, asymmetrical, historically frozen relation of the sexes. She keeps the profound insight — that selfhood is constituted through the positing of an Other — while showing how, in the case of women, the dialectic has been arrested, producing not a struggle toward recognition but a durable structure of subordination. The task she sets is to unfreeze it: for women to claim the status of Subject that men have reserved for themselves.
