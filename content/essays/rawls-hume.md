Rawls drew on Hume at a foundational point: his account of the **circumstances of justice** — the conditions under which questions of justice arise at all — is taken directly from Hume, and it frames the whole problem *A Theory of Justice* sets out to solve.

Hume had asked *why* human beings need justice, and answered by describing the circumstances that make it both necessary and possible. Justice, he argued, is an artificial virtue that arises under specific conditions: **moderate scarcity** (there is neither such abundance that everyone can have whatever they want, nor such desperate want that cooperation is impossible) and **limited generosity** (human beings are neither perfectly altruistic, in which case rules of justice would be unnecessary, nor purely selfish, in which case they would be impossible). In a world of angels or of infinite plenty, justice would have no point; in a world of pure egoism or utter destitution, it could not exist. Justice is for beings like us, in circumstances like ours.

Rawls takes over this Humean analysis wholesale as the **circumstances of justice** that define the problem his theory addresses. The parties in the original position operate under exactly these conditions — moderate scarcity, mutually disinterested individuals with their own aims — and it is because human life presents these circumstances that principles for the fair distribution of the benefits and burdens of social cooperation are needed. Justice, for Rawls as for Hume, is the virtue of social cooperation among people who are neither saints nor devils.

Rawls also shares Hume's understanding of justice as bound up with the terms of **cooperation for mutual advantage** and his refusal to ground it in a prior metaphysical order.

But Rawls departs from Hume on the derivation and status of the principles. Hume grounded justice in **utility** and **convention** — the rules of justice arise gradually because they serve the general interest, and we approve them by sympathy. Rawls rejects the utilitarian grounding (his whole theory is built against utilitarianism) and replaces Hume's evolutionary, convention-based account with a **constructivist** one: principles chosen through a fair procedure, the original position. Where Hume explains how justice *arises* and why we approve it, Rawls asks which principles are *justified*, and answers by rational agreement rather than by utility and custom.

So Rawls continues Hume by adopting his analysis of the circumstances of justice — moderate scarcity and limited generosity — as the framing conditions of his own theory, and by conceiving justice as the virtue of cooperation among mutually disinterested persons. He inherits Hume's realistic picture of when and why justice matters, while replacing Hume's utilitarian and conventionalist foundations with a constructivist procedure. Hume told him the conditions that make justice necessary; Rawls built the fair procedure meant to say what justice, under those conditions, requires.
