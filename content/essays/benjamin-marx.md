Walter Benjamin was a Marxist, but a Marxist like no other — one who fused historical materialism with Jewish messianism, Romantic literary criticism, and a mystical sense of history, producing a reinterpretation of Marx so idiosyncratic that orthodox Marxists distrusted it and readers have been fascinated ever since.

From Marx, Benjamin took the foundational commitments: that culture and consciousness are bound up with the **material and economic conditions** of production; that history is a field of class struggle; and that capitalism must be grasped in its concrete, sensuous, everyday forms. His vast, unfinished *Arcades Project* is a materialist study of nineteenth-century Paris — its shopping arcades, commodities, fashions, and crowds — read as the dreamworld of industrial capitalism. His famous essay on the work of art in the age of mechanical reproduction analyzes how the technology of production (photography, film) transforms art, stripping it of its "aura" and changing its political function — a classic relating of the cultural superstructure to the material base.

But Benjamin **reinterprets** Marx by infusing him with **theology** and messianism, and by rejecting the doctrine of inevitable progress. Orthodox Marxism had inherited a confidence that history advances lawfully toward socialism. In his late "Theses on the Philosophy of History," Benjamin savagely attacks this. The idea of history as smooth progress, he argues, is an illusion shared by both bourgeois and Social-Democratic thought; the "angel of history" is blown backward into the future, seeing only a mounting pile of wreckage. Redemption will not come as the automatic result of economic development but as a **messianic** interruption — a revolutionary "now-time" (*Jetztzeit*) that blasts open the continuum of history and redeems the sufferings of the past.

He also reads history "against the grain," insisting that "there is no document of civilization which is not at the same time a document of barbarism," and that the historical materialist must side with the defeated and the forgotten rather than identify with the victors whose triumphs constitute the official record.

So Benjamin reinterprets Marx by welding historical materialism to messianic theology, replacing the faith in progress with a vision of history as catastrophe awaiting redemptive rupture, and turning the analysis of culture into a rescue of the oppressed past. Historical materialism, he wrote, can win every time if it enlists the services of theology — "a wizened dwarf hidden inside the chess-playing automaton." It is Marx read through the Kabbalah and Baudelaire, and it gave critical theory some of its most haunting ideas.
