Gadamer's philosophical hermeneutics was built in part *against* Dilthey, even as it inherited the hermeneutic tradition Dilthey had done so much to shape. The quarrel is whether hermeneutics is a **method** for securing objective knowledge — as Dilthey hoped — or something deeper and more unavoidable than method.

Dilthey had sought to give the human sciences a rigorous **foundation** that would make their results as valid, in their own way, as those of the natural sciences. Understanding (*Verstehen*), for Dilthey, was to be a method: by empathetically reliving the lived experience expressed in a text, and by disciplined interpretation, the human scientist could achieve **objective knowledge** of the human past. Dilthey remained, in this sense, within the orbit of nineteenth-century concerns with objectivity — he wanted hermeneutics to overcome the interpreter's own standpoint and reach the thing as it really was.

Gadamer, following Heidegger, rejects this whole framing — hence the pointed title *Truth and Method*, which sets "truth" *against* method. Understanding, he argues, is not a **method** we can apply to guarantee objectivity; it is the fundamental way human beings exist historically, and it can never escape the interpreter's own situation. Dilthey's dream of an empathetic reliving that transcends one's own horizon is an illusion. We always understand from within our own **historically effected consciousness**, shaped by tradition and prejudice, and there is no standpoint outside history from which to achieve the detached objectivity Dilthey craved.

Gadamer's central charge is that Dilthey, for all his emphasis on life and history, was still captive to the **objectivism of the natural sciences** — still measuring the human sciences by the ideal of method and objective validity borrowed from science. He failed to see that understanding is not the reconstruction of an object as it was "in itself," but a **fusion of horizons** in which past and present, text and interpreter, meet and the interpreter is changed. Meaning is not simply *there* in the text to be recovered; it emerges in the dialogue between the text and the questions the interpreter brings from his own situation.

So Gadamer continues the tradition Dilthey founded while criticizing its guiding ambition. He keeps Dilthey's central insight — that the human world is a realm of meaning to be understood rather than causally explained — but rejects Dilthey's methodologism and objectivism. Understanding is not a method for escaping our historical situation to grasp the past objectively; it is an event that happens within tradition, in which our own prejudices are engaged and transformed. Where Dilthey wanted hermeneutics to be a science, Gadamer shows it to be the very condition of being human in history — deeper than any method.
