Democritus had built the boldest system of the ancient world: nothing exists but **atoms and the void**, indivisible particles moving through empty space, their collisions and combinations producing everything else. It was a complete materialism, and Aristotle took it with the seriousness it deserved — praising Democritus for actually seeking causes in nature while rejecting almost all his conclusions.

The deepest quarrel is over the **void**. For the atomists, empty space is what makes motion possible: without it, a full universe could not budge. Aristotle argues the reverse — that a true void is impossible and self-contradictory. In empty space there would be no up or down, no reason for a thing to move here rather than there, and, since resistance determines speed, a body in a void would move either infinitely fast or not at all. Nature, he concludes, abhors a vacuum.

He rejects atoms too. Magnitudes are, for Aristotle, *continuous* — infinitely divisible in principle — not built from indivisible minima. And the atomists' deepest sin is explanatory: they account for everything by the blind push of particle on particle, with no **final cause**, no purpose. Yet living things above all cry out for teleology; an eye is *for* seeing, and no rain of atoms explains that.

> Democritus neglected the final cause, referring all the operations of nature to necessity.

The disagreement is not a detail but a fork in the road of Western thought. Down one branch lies a purposeless universe of matter in motion, waiting to be revived in the seventeenth century; down the other, Aristotle's cosmos of forms and ends. For two thousand years the second branch won.
