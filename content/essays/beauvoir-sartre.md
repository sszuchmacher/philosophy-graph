Simone de Beauvoir and Jean-Paul Sartre were lifelong intellectual partners, and Beauvoir worked within the existentialist framework she and Sartre developed together — freedom, situation, bad faith, the Other. For decades she was read (and sometimes dismissed) as merely applying Sartre's philosophy. In fact she took its central concepts, corrected their deepest weakness, and turned them toward concrete human life with a rigor Sartre often lacked.

The shared foundation is the existentialist ontology of **freedom**: human beings have no fixed essence, they exist by choosing themselves, and they flee this freedom through *bad faith*, pretending to be fixed things when they are really free. From Sartre too comes the analysis of **the Other** — the way one consciousness objectifies another through "the Look." Beauvoir's masterwork, *The Second Sex*, is built on exactly this apparatus: **woman as the Other**, defined by man as the inessential object against his essential subject.

But Beauvoir corrects a real defect in Sartre's early philosophy — his tendency to treat freedom as *absolute*, as if consciousness were unconditionally free in any situation. Beauvoir insists, far more concretely, that freedom is always **situated**: shaped, constrained, sometimes crushed by the body, by history, by oppression and material conditions. A woman's "situation" is not freely chosen; it is imposed by an entire social order that trains her into immanence and dependence. This gives her existentialism a social and political seriousness — an attention to how oppression really limits freedom — that Sartre himself only reached later, partly under her influence.

She also grounds an **ethics** where Sartre had left a gap. *Being and Nothingness* ended with the promise of an ethics never delivered; Beauvoir's *Ethics of Ambiguity* provides it, arguing that to will one's own freedom authentically is to will the freedom of others, converting existentialist ontology into a genuine morality.

So Beauvoir "continues" Sartre only in the sense that a peer develops shared ideas further, and better, on crucial points. She takes existentialist freedom and the Other, embeds them in the concrete, situated, embodied, oppressed conditions of real lives — above all women's — and completes the ethics existentialism had promised. *The Second Sex* is what existentialism looks like when it takes situation, and injustice, fully seriously.
