Lyotard is famous as the theorist of the "postmodern condition" and "incredulity toward metanarratives," but the philosophical backbone of his mature work is a sustained, unorthodox reinterpretation of **Kant** — above all the Kant of the *Critique of Judgment* and the doctrine of the **sublime**.

Lyotard turns away from the Kant of determinate knowledge and universal law toward the Kant of **reflective judgment**. In the third *Critique*, Kant had distinguished *determinant* judgment, which subsumes a particular under a given universal rule, from *reflective* judgment, which faces a particular for which no rule is given and must seek the universal — as in aesthetic judgments of beauty, where no concept determines what pleases. Lyotard makes reflective judgment central: in the absence of universal rules — which is precisely the postmodern situation, once the grand metanarratives that supplied such rules have collapsed — we are left to judge, case by case, without criteria given in advance. Judgment "without concepts" becomes the model for thought and politics after the metanarratives.

Even more important is Kant's analysis of the **sublime**. For Kant, the sublime is the feeling that arises when we confront something that overwhelms the imagination's power to present it — the vast or the mighty — so that reason's idea of the infinite exceeds any possible image. It is a mix of pain (imagination fails) and pleasure (reason's vocation is revealed). Lyotard seizes on the sublime as the key experience of modernity and postmodernity: the presentation of the **unpresentable**, the gesturing toward what exceeds all representation. Avant-garde art, for Lyotard, is sublime in this sense — it bears witness to the existence of the unpresentable rather than offering beautiful, harmonious forms.

This connects to his politics of the **differend**. The sublime marks the limits of representation; the differend marks cases where no shared rule of judgment exists to resolve a conflict, where to apply a common measure would silence one party's wrong. Kant's reflective judgment without universal rules becomes Lyotard's model for judging justly amid heterogeneous, incommensurable claims.

So Lyotard reinterprets Kant by shifting the center of gravity from the first *Critique* to the third — from determinate knowledge and universal moral law to reflective judgment and the sublime. He takes Kant's judgment without concepts and his analysis of the unpresentable and makes them resources for thinking after the collapse of metanarratives: a postmodern Kantianism of judgment without criteria, the witnessing of the unpresentable, and justice among incommensurable claims. Where Kant used the sublime and reflective judgment as parts of a larger system aiming at harmony, Lyotard detaches them and turns them into the very figures of heterogeneity, dissensus, and the limits of representation.
