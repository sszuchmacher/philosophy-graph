Wilhelm Dilthey set out to give the **human sciences** (*Geisteswissenschaften*) — history, psychology, the study of culture, art, and society — their own proper foundation, distinct from the natural sciences. In doing so he had to settle accounts with Hegel, whose philosophy of history and of "objective spirit" loomed over any German attempt to think about culture and history.

From Hegel, Dilthey inherited a great deal, and never simply repudiated him. He took over the notion of **objective spirit** — the idea that the human world is a realm of *meaning*, embodied in institutions, laws, art, religion, and language, the collective creations in which mind objectifies itself and which can be understood as expressions of spirit. Dilthey's whole conception of the human sciences as the study of *meaningful* human productions — as opposed to the causal, law-governed objects of natural science — is deeply Hegelian in inspiration.

But Dilthey mounts a sharp **critique** of Hegel's **speculative, a priori** way of grasping this realm. Hegel had comprehended history and culture as the necessary self-development of a single Absolute Spirit, unfolding according to a rational, dialectical logic toward a preordained end — the whole of history deduced, in effect, from the concept. Dilthey rejects this as metaphysical overreach. There is no Absolute Spirit realizing a necessary logic; there is only the concrete, empirical variety of human historical **life** in its endless particularity. We cannot deduce history from a metaphysical principle; we can only *understand* it from within, through interpretation of the actual expressions human beings have left.

So Dilthey replaces Hegel's **construction** with **understanding** (*Verstehen*). Where Hegel comprehended objective spirit by fitting it into the necessary movement of the Absolute, Dilthey insists we grasp it through empathetic, hermeneutic interpretation — reliving the lived experience (*Erlebnis*) expressed in a text, an institution, a historical action — grounded in the concrete reality of human life rather than a speculative logic. And against Hegel's confidence that history has a single rational meaning culminating in the present, Dilthey stresses the **historicity and relativity** of all worldviews: every age has its own perspective, with no absolute standpoint above them.

So Dilthey continues Hegel's insight that the human world is a realm of objectified meaning to be understood, while criticizing and dismantling Hegel's metaphysical machinery. He keeps objective spirit and drops the Absolute; he keeps the focus on meaning and history and replaces speculative deduction with empirical, hermeneutic understanding grounded in lived experience — founding modern hermeneutics and the methodology of the human sciences: Hegel's realm of spirit set free from Hegel's system.
