Adorno was a Marxist, but of the Western, Hegelian, and deeply heterodox kind — one who kept Marx's critique of capitalism and reification at the center while abandoning almost everything about Marx's politics and philosophy of history.

The core Marxian inheritance is the concept of the **commodity** and **reification**. Following Marx (and Lukács), Adorno sees capitalist society as one in which the commodity form penetrates everything: human relations, consciousness, even art, are turned into things, subordinated to exchange and quantification. His entire analysis of the "**culture industry**" — the mass production of standardized film, music, and entertainment that pacifies and integrates people into the system — extends Marx's commodity analysis into the twentieth-century realm of culture: culture itself becomes a commodity, and the promise of art is betrayed as it is absorbed into the machinery of profit and manipulation. Adorno keeps Marx's ideology-critique, his materialism, and his revulsion at exploitation and domination.

But Adorno drops or inverts key Marxian doctrines. He abandons Marx's faith in the **proletariat** as the revolutionary agent — the working class, he thinks, has been integrated and pacified by the culture industry and consumer capitalism. He abandons the **philosophy of history** as progress toward revolution; after Auschwitz, the idea that history moves toward reason and freedom is untenable, and *Dialectic of Enlightenment* reads the whole trajectory of civilization as a deepening domination rather than an advance toward liberation. And he refuses to offer a political program, insisting instead on relentless **negative critique** — the refusal to affirm, the preservation of the memory of suffering, the demand that thought not reconcile itself to the existing order.

His hope, such as it is, migrates from politics to **art**. Where Marx looked to revolutionary praxis, Adorno looks to the difficult, autonomous modernist artwork — the dissonant music of Schoenberg, the negativity of Beckett — as the last refuge of the non-identical, a mode of resistance that preserves the promise of freedom precisely by refusing to fit into a false whole.

So Adorno continues Marx by radicalizing his critique of capitalism, commodification, and ideology while emptying it of its revolutionary optimism. He keeps the analysis of domination and extends it into culture with unrivaled subtlety, but replaces the proletariat, the philosophy of progress, and the promise of revolution with a stubborn, melancholy negativity that finds its only glimmers of freedom in autonomous art. It is Marx's critique of the world without Marx's confidence that the world can be redeemed.
