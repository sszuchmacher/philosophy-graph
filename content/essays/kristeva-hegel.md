Kristeva's theory of the subject and of signification draws on **Hegel** — especially the Hegelian concepts of **negativity** and the dialectical, processual subject — which she reinterprets and fuses with psychoanalysis and linguistics to conceive the subject as a dynamic "process."

From Hegel, Kristeva takes above all the concept of **negativity** — the restless, dynamic force of negation that, in Hegel, drives the dialectic, the movement by which the subject and reality develop through the negation and transformation of what is given. Kristeva prizes this as a way of thinking about what is *productive*, disruptive, and mobile in subjectivity and language, against static, structuralist conceptions. She reworks Hegelian negativity into what she calls **rejection** (*rejet*) — a bodily, drive-based force of negativity, rooted in the semiotic, that continually disrupts and renews signification and the subject.

This yields her signature notion of the **subject-in-process** (*sujet en procès* — a phrase that also puns on "subject on trial"). Against the idea of a fixed, unified subject, Kristeva conceives the subject as a dynamic process, never complete, constantly constituted and unsettled through the interplay of the semiotic and the Symbolic, drive and law, negativity and structure. This processual, self-negating, developing subject is deeply Hegelian in inspiration — the subject as movement and becoming rather than a static substance — even as Kristeva grounds it in the body and the drives rather than in Spirit.

But Kristeva **reinterprets** Hegel by materializing and de-teleologizing his negativity. She takes negativity out of Hegel's idealist, rational, teleological system — where it is the labor of the concept moving toward the Absolute's self-knowledge — and relocates it in the **body, the drives, and language**: negativity becomes a bodily, semiotic force operating in the material of signification, not the self-development of Spirit toward a rational end. There is no Absolute, no final synthesis, no reconciliation in which the process comes to rest; the subject-in-process is permanently open, continually disrupted.

So Kristeva reinterprets Hegel by borrowing his dynamic negativity and his processual, developing subject, and rebuilding them on psychoanalytic and materialist foundations. She keeps the Hegelian insight that the subject is a movement driven by negativity, not a static thing, while replacing Spirit's rational teleology with the bodily drives and the semiotic, and refusing any final synthesis. Hegel gave her negativity and the subject-as-process; Kristeva turned them into the *sujet en procès* — a subject perpetually made and unmade in the material play of drive and language, negation without the Absolute.
