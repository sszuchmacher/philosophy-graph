Locke and Hobbes both build the state on a social contract emerging from a state of nature — but Locke's state of nature, and the government he derives from it, are almost the mirror image of Hobbes's, and the difference founds the liberal tradition.

For Hobbes, the state of nature is a **war of all against all**, a condition of such terror that men will rationally surrender nearly all their rights to an absolute sovereign in exchange for security. There is no appeal against the Leviathan; unchecked power is the price of peace.

Locke rejects the premise and therefore the conclusion. His state of nature is not war but a condition governed by a **natural law**, knowable by reason, under which each person already has rights to **life, liberty, and property** — and a right to punish those who violate them. It is inconvenient and insecure, lacking an impartial judge, but it is not Hobbes's hell.

So men form government not to escape a war of all against all but to better protect rights they *already possess*. And this transforms the sovereign's authority. Because power is entrusted for the limited purpose of protecting natural rights, it is **conditional**. The government holds power in trust; if it turns to tyranny and attacks the rights it was instituted to defend, it forfeits its authority, and the people retain a **right of revolution** — to alter or abolish it.

Against Hobbes's absolute, irresistible sovereign, Locke sets **limited, accountable government**, resting on consent, bounded by natural rights, and answerable to the people. Where Hobbes's fearful subjects have no recourse, Locke's citizens may resist and replace a ruler who breaks the trust.

The critique is foundational. Hobbes had used the contract to justify absolutism; Locke uses the same device to justify constitutional, limited government — and hands the American and French revolutionaries their charter of natural rights and the right to overthrow tyranny.
