Nietzsche discovered Schopenhauer as a young man and was overwhelmed — he called him his great educator, and his early work is steeped in Schopenhauer's metaphysics of the **Will**. For a time Schopenhauer's vision of a blind striving underlying all things, and of existence as suffering, was Nietzsche's own. His mature philosophy is a long, grateful, and ferocious settling of accounts with this first master.

Nietzsche keeps the starting point. Schopenhauer was right, he thinks, that the fundamental reality is not reason but a deep, striving **will** — that beneath consciousness lies something that drives us. Nietzsche's **will to power** is unthinkable without Schopenhauer's will to life as its ancestor. And he keeps Schopenhauer's honesty about suffering: that life involves cruelty, loss, and pain, and that no rational providence redeems it.

But everything turns on what follows. Schopenhauer had drawn from the primacy of the Will a **pessimism** and a counsel of **denial**: since willing is endless craving and therefore suffering, the highest wisdom is to *deny* the will — to renounce desire, turn ascetic, quiet the striving, and reach a Buddhist-like extinction of want. Existence is something to be escaped.

This is exactly what Nietzsche comes to despise. To deny the will, he argues, is a form of **life-hatred** — a "will to nothingness," the same ascetic, world-renouncing impulse he finds at the root of Christianity and traces, in the *Genealogy*, to weakness and resentment dressed as wisdom. The pessimist who says *no* to life has, for Nietzsche, betrayed it.

Against Schopenhauer's denial he sets **affirmation**. Yes, life is striving, suffering, becoming — and the task is not to escape it but to say *Yes* to it, to affirm existence in its entirety, including its pain, even to *will its eternal return*. The will is to be embraced and enhanced, not extinguished; suffering is to be made into strength and beauty, not fled.

So Nietzsche inverts his teacher on the decisive point. He accepts Schopenhauer's diagnosis — reality as will, life as suffering — and rejects his prescription. Where Schopenhauer concludes "therefore deny the will," Nietzsche concludes "therefore affirm it." The pessimism of resignation becomes a tragic, joyful pessimism of strength, and Nietzsche's whole philosophy of affirmation is born from the reversal of the man who taught him to take the will seriously in the first place.
