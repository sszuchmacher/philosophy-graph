Seneca stands to the early Stoa roughly as Cicero stands to Plato: a Roman of immense literary gift who takes a Greek system and makes it speak to real, urgent life. The doctrines are Zeno's — virtue the only good, the passions to be mastered, nature to be followed — but Seneca cares far less for the technical apparatus than for how a person actually copes.

His chosen forms tell the story. Rather than treatises on logic and physics, Seneca writes *letters* — to his friend Lucilius — and short essays on concrete afflictions: on anger, on grief, on the shortness of life, on bearing exile or poverty or the fear of death. Stoicism becomes counsel, delivered in a warm, aphoristic, intimate voice. It is philosophy for the bedside and the sleepless night.

> As often as I wish to know how far virtue has advanced in me, I turn to myself.

Seneca is candid, even confessional, about his own failures to reach the ideal — a Stoicism aware that the sage is a distant goal and most of us are only *making progress*. This humane realism, and his stress on daily self-examination, soften the early school's forbidding image of the perfect, passionless wise man.

He is often charged with hypocrisy — a preacher of indifference to wealth who was among the richest men in Rome, tutor to Nero. But the tension is part of the appeal: here is Stoicism practiced not by a saint but by a powerful, compromised, worldly man trying to keep hold of his soul. Zeno gave the doctrine; Seneca gave it a human face and a Latin voice that Europe never stopped reading.
