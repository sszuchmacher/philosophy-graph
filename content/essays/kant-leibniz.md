Kant was raised in the rationalist tradition of Leibniz, as systematized by Christian Wolff, and his mature philosophy is in large part an accounting of what that tradition got wrong. He had, as he put it, been dwelling in "dogmatic slumber" — the confidence that pure reason, unaided by experience, could establish substantive truths about God, the soul, and the ultimate structure of reality.

Leibnizian metaphysics claimed exactly this power. From pure concepts and principles like sufficient reason, it deduced a whole world — monads, pre-established harmony, the identity of indiscernibles, the immortality of the soul, the existence of God — by reason alone, without appeal to experience. Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason* is a systematic demonstration that reason has no such reach.

His central objection is that Leibniz had confused the way we **think** with the way things **are**. The rationalists treated the concepts and principles of the understanding as if they revealed the intrinsic nature of reality; but concepts without sensory content, Kant argues, are **empty**. Categories like substance and cause yield knowledge only when applied to what is given in experience, through the forms of space and time. Detached from possible experience and used to reason about God, the soul, or the world as a whole, they generate not knowledge but **illusion** — the "antinomies" in which reason proves opposite conclusions with equal cogency, a sign that it has overstepped its bounds.

Kant makes the point sharply: Leibniz had "intellectualized appearances," treating sensibility as merely confused thinking, and so mistook logical relations among concepts for real relations among things. In truth sensibility and understanding are distinct sources, and knowledge requires both.

The critique is not a rejection of reason but a **delimitation** of it. Kant preserves the rationalist insistence that there are necessary, a priori truths — but confines them to the conditions of possible experience. Metaphysics of the old Leibnizian kind, soaring beyond all experience on pure concepts, is exposed as impossible. Reason must undergo self-criticism, learn its limits, and abandon the pretension to know things in themselves.
