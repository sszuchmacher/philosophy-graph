Feuerbach came out of the Hegelian school and turned its master upside down. Hegel had made **Spirit** — ultimately a kind of divine self-consciousness — the true reality, with the human being merely a vehicle through which Absolute Spirit comes to know itself. Religion, for Hegel, was a lower form of that same truth: God really exists as Spirit, and human self-consciousness is a moment in God's own life.

Feuerbach inverts subject and predicate. It is not that God comes to consciousness in man; it is that **man has projected his own essence outward and called it God**. Theology is really **anthropology** in disguise.

The mechanism is projection. The qualities religion ascribes to God — infinite love, wisdom, power, goodness — are in truth the perfections of the human *species*, abstracted from actual, limited individuals, magnified to infinity, and then set up as an independent being over against us. In worshipping God, humanity is unknowingly worshipping its own idealized essence.

> Man — this is the mystery of religion — projects his being into objectivity, and then again makes himself an object to this projected image.

The inversion has a cost and a promise. The cost is **alienation**: by pouring its own best qualities into an imaginary heavenly being, humanity impoverishes itself, growing poor and abject as God grows rich, submitting to a projection of its own making. The remedy is to reclaim the projection — to recognize that the divine predicates are human, to love and realize them in one another rather than surrendering them to heaven. Theology must become anthropology; the love of God must become the love of humanity.

This is the great **inversion** of Hegel that set the terms for what followed. Feuerbach keeps Hegel's insight that religion expresses a deep truth about self-consciousness, but relocates that truth entirely in the human being, dissolving the divine into the human. Marx would take the next step — asking *why* humanity needs to project, and locating the answer in material social conditions — but the founding move, God as the estranged essence of man, is Feuerbach's.
