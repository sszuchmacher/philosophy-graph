Before she was the theorist of gender performativity, Judith Butler was a scholar of **Hegel**: her first book, *Subjects of Desire*, examined the reception of Hegel in twentieth-century France, and Hegelian themes — desire, recognition, and the constitution of the subject through the Other — run through all her later work.

From Hegel, Butler takes the insight that the **subject is constituted relationally**, through its encounter with what is other than itself, and specifically through **recognition**. Hegel had shown, in the dialectic of self-consciousness, that the self does not exist as a self-sufficient given but comes to be only through being recognized by another, in a fraught struggle of mutual dependence. Butler makes this constitution-through-the-Other central: the subject, including the gendered subject, is not a prior foundation but is formed in and through its relations to others and to the norms that confer or withhold recognition.

This Hegelian frame shapes her account of how we become intelligible subjects at all. To count as a subject — indeed, to count as a livable, "recognizable" human being — one must be recognized within available norms; and gender norms are precisely the terms through which recognition is granted or refused. Those who fall outside the intelligible gender norms are, in a Hegelian sense, denied recognition, rendered abject or unreal. Butler's later ethical work — on precarity, grievability, and whose lives "count" — extends this Hegelian concern with recognition into a broader question of which lives are granted recognition as fully human.

But Butler **reinterprets** Hegel by refusing his reconciliation. Hegel's dialectic of recognition moves, through struggle, toward mutual recognition and a higher synthesis — the reconciliation of self and other in Spirit, the subject coming home to itself. Butler, following the darker French readings of Hegel (Kojève, and after him Sartre, Lacan, and the post-structuralists), denies this happy resolution. Recognition remains fraught, incomplete, and bound up with subjection: to be recognized is also to be subjected to norms one did not choose, and the desire for recognition can never be finally satisfied.

So Butler reinterprets Hegel by taking his profound insight that the subject is constituted through recognition and the Other, and detaching it from his teleology of reconciliation. She keeps the relational, recognition-dependent, desiring subject and the drama of intelligibility, while insisting — with the anti-Hegelian Hegelians of France — that recognition is inseparable from subjection and never achieves the reconciliation Hegel promised. Hegel gave Butler the subject formed through the Other; Butler made it the basis of a theory of how gender norms confer or deny the recognition that makes a life livable.
