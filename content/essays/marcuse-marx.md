Herbert Marcuse was the Frankfurt School thinker who kept Marx's **revolutionary** intent most alive — the one who, in the 1960s, became the philosopher of the New Left, his name chanted alongside Marx and Mao. But his Marxism, like his colleagues', was profoundly revised to fit a world Marx had not foreseen.

The Marxian foundation is firm. Marcuse retains Marx's critique of capitalism as a system of **domination and exploitation**, his concept of **alienation** (drawn especially from the early Marx of the 1844 Manuscripts, which Marcuse helped make central), and his goal of human **emancipation** — a liberated society beyond exploitation, in which human beings could develop freely. He keeps the conviction that the existing order is historical and changeable, and that theory should serve its transformation.

But Marcuse confronts the great problem of Western Marxism: the revolution did not come, and the working class, in advanced capitalist societies, was not revolting but *consenting*. His answer, in *One-Dimensional Man*, revises Marx accordingly. Advanced industrial society, he argues, has developed subtler forms of control — it buys off the proletariat with consumer goods and rising living standards, and manufactures false needs and a "happy consciousness" through mass culture and technology, producing a **one-dimensional** society in which genuine opposition is absorbed and neutralized. The very success of capitalism in delivering comfort has become the deepest form of domination, and the working class is no longer the revolutionary subject Marx expected.

So Marcuse relocates revolutionary hope. If the integrated proletariat will not act, the impulse to liberation must come from the **margins** — students, racial minorities, the unintegrated — and from forces Marx did not emphasize: **Eros**, imagination, art, and the refusal of a repressive "performance principle." Fusing Marx with **Freud** (in *Eros and Civilization*), Marcuse argues that liberation is not only economic but instinctual and aesthetic — the release of repressed desire and the reconciliation of humanity with its own sensuous being.

So Marcuse continues Marx as the Frankfurt School's great utopian — keeping the critique of domination, the concept of alienation, and the goal of emancipation — while updating the analysis for consumer capitalism, shifting the revolutionary subject from the integrated working class to the marginalized, and expanding the content of liberation from economic transformation to the liberation of Eros, imagination, and the senses. Marx's revolution, stalled in the affluent society, is reborn in Marcuse as a demand for total liberation that spoke directly to the rebellions of the 1960s.
