Before Wittgenstein went to Russell, he had visited Frege, and Frege's influence on him — acknowledged throughout his life with unusual reverence — runs even deeper than Russell's. The *Tractatus* opens under Frege's sign, and Wittgenstein said Frege's writing had a clarity and depth he found nowhere else.

From Frege, Wittgenstein took several foundational ideas. First, the new **logic** and the analysis of the proposition into function and argument, which underlies the *Tractatus*'s account of how propositions are built. Second, and crucially, the **context principle** — Frege's dictum that a word has meaning only in the context of a proposition, that one should never ask for the meaning of a word in isolation. This principle shapes both the early and the late Wittgenstein. Third, Frege's fierce **anti-psychologism**: that logic and meaning are objective, not descriptions of how we happen to think, and that the sense of a proposition is not a private mental image.

The *Tractatus*'s central concern — the distinction between what can be **said** and what can only be **shown**, and the nature of the proposition as a picture with sense — grows out of wrestling with Fregean problems about sense, reference, and the unity of the proposition.

But Wittgenstein also criticized Frege sharply. He rejected Frege's treatment of the logical connectives and of assertion as further "functions," and his view that logical propositions are substantive truths about a realm of logical objects. For Wittgenstein, logical truths are empty tautologies that *show* the structure of language rather than describe a special subject matter. And the later Wittgenstein turned against the whole Fregean-Russellian picture of meaning as fixed by logical analysis, replacing it with meaning as use in a form of life — though even there the Fregean context principle survives, broadened from the sentence to the language-game.

So Wittgenstein continues Frege as a devoted but independent heir. He took Frege's logic, his context principle, and his anti-psychologism as permanent possessions, built the *Tractatus* out of Fregean materials, and honored Frege as a master of clarity — while transforming and criticizing Frege's specific doctrines at every turn. Of the two logicians who formed him, it was Frege whom Wittgenstein revered most deeply, and whose fingerprints are on his work from first to last.
