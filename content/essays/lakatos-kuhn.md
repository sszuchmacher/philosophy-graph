Lakatos developed his methodology of research programmes explicitly against **Kuhn** — accepting the force of Kuhn's history while rejecting its apparent conclusion that scientific change is irrational. The stakes, for Lakatos, were nothing less than the rationality of science.

Lakatos granted Kuhn a great deal. He agreed that Popper's naive falsificationism was refuted by history — that scientists do not abandon theories at the first anomaly, that important theories persist despite counter-examples, that there is something like Kuhn's normal science conducted within a framework. On the descriptive facts, Kuhn was largely right, and Lakatos built these facts into his own account (the "hard core" protected by a "protective belt" is his version of the tenacity Kuhn observed).

But Lakatos was appalled by what he took to be Kuhn's **irrationalism**. If paradigm change is a "conversion," a matter of persuasion and generational change between incommensurable worldviews with no neutral standard to decide between them, then — Lakatos charges — Kuhn has reduced scientific revolution to "**mob psychology**," a matter of crowd behavior rather than reason. Kuhn's picture leaves no rational criterion for saying that one paradigm is objectively better than another; the switch becomes a kind of religious conversion, decided by which community prevails, not by reasons. This, for Lakatos the committed rationalist, was intolerable — it abandoned the demarcation between science and pseudo-science and made even the triumph of good science over bad a mere sociological fact.

Lakatos's solution is designed precisely to restore the **rationality** Kuhn seemed to surrender while keeping Kuhn's realism about tenacity. His distinction between **progressive** and **degenerating** research programmes provides the objective criterion Kuhn lacked: a programme is scientific and rationally preferable if it keeps predicting *novel facts* that are corroborated, degenerating if it merely patches anomalies with ad hoc excuses. Theory-choice is not a conversion but a reasoned appraisal — though one that can be made only over time, by looking at whether a programme is progressing or stagnating. Where Kuhn saw incommensurable paradigms and non-rational shifts, Lakatos sees competing programmes rationally comparable by their track record of novel predictions.

So Lakatos's relation to Kuhn is a critique that accepts the history and rejects the relativism. He agrees with Kuhn that science works through tenacious frameworks that resist single refutations, and that naive falsificationism is dead — but he denies Kuhn's implication that this makes scientific change irrational or a matter of mob psychology. By supplying an objective criterion (progressive versus degenerating programmes), Lakatos tries to have Kuhn's realism about scientific practice *and* Popper's rationalism about scientific progress — rescuing the rationality of science from the relativist reading that Kuhn's incommensurable paradigms seemed to invite.
