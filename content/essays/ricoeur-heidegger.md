Ricoeur admired Heidegger and drew on his hermeneutic ontology — the idea that understanding is a fundamental mode of human being — but he mounted a persistent, characteristically generous **critique** of Heidegger's method, faulting it for a shortcut that bypasses the concrete labor of interpretation.

Heidegger (and after him Gadamer) had made a decisive move: from hermeneutics as a *method* of the human sciences to hermeneutics as *ontology* — an account of understanding as the basic structure of Dasein's being-in-the-world. Ricoeur accepts the depth of this insight but resists its **directness**. Heidegger's path, he complains, is a philosophy of "short cuts": it leaps immediately to the ontological question of understanding as a mode of Being, vaulting over the actual, painstaking work of interpreting particular texts, symbols, and signs. It reaches the fundamental level of Being in one bound, and in doing so loses contact with the concrete methods of the human sciences — the philology, the exegesis, the confrontation with the resistant details of a text.

Ricoeur's alternative is the **long route** (*la voie longue*). Rather than leaping directly to ontology, he insists we must pass through the detour of interpreting concrete works — the analysis of language, symbol, metaphor, and narrative, and even a respectful engagement with the methods of the sciences of texts (structuralism, exegesis, historical criticism). Only by the patient labor of interpreting particular expressions of meaning can we approach the ontological questions Heidegger rushed toward. Understanding of being must be *earned* through the interpretation of what beings say and write, not seized in an ontological intuition.

Behind this lies a difference of temperament and value. Ricoeur wants to keep hermeneutics in dialogue with the human sciences, with method, with the concrete disciplines of interpretation, rather than dissolving them in a fundamental ontology that stands above them.

So Ricoeur's relation to Heidegger is a critique that preserves the insight while rejecting the impatience. He accepts that understanding is ultimately a matter of our being-in-the-world, but insists on reaching that truth by the long route through the interpretation of concrete symbols and texts — restoring to hermeneutics the methodological, disciplined, text-bound labor that Heidegger's leap to ontology had left behind. It is depth *and* rigor, ontology *by way of* interpretation, rather than ontology at the expense of it.
