Judith Butler's theory of gender is built on **Foucault** as much as on Beauvoir. Foucault's account of power, the subject, and the body gives Butler the framework within which she rethinks gender as something produced rather than expressed.

From Foucault, Butler takes above all the idea that **power is productive**, not merely repressive. Foucault had argued that power does not simply forbid and constrain a pre-existing subject; it *produces* subjects, identities, bodies, and "truths." Butler applies this directly to gender: gender is not a natural fact that power represses or regulates but is itself *produced* by regulatory practices, norms, and discourses. The gendered subject is an effect of power, constituted through the very norms that seem merely to describe it.

She also takes Foucault's **critique of the subject** — the rejection of the sovereign, self-founding subject in favor of a subject constituted within relations of power and discourse. Butler's "doer" behind the deed dissolves, as Foucault's autonomous subject dissolved, into an effect of the practices that produce it. There is no gender identity prior to the acts and norms; the subject of gender is constituted in and through them.

Foucault's analysis of the way power operates through the **regulation of bodies and sexuality** — his *History of Sexuality*, with its claim that sexuality is produced by discourses of power rather than liberated from repression — is the direct model for Butler's account of how bodies are materialized and sexed within regimes of power. And his attention to **normalization** underlies Butler's account of the gender norms whose compulsory repetition produces the appearance of natural sex and stable gender.

But Butler also extends Foucault where she finds him incomplete — bringing in psychoanalysis, performativity, and a sharper account of how norms are cited and can be subverted, and giving more thought than Foucault did to **agency and resistance**. If the subject is produced through the repetition of norms, she argues, then the necessity of *repetition* opens a space for repeating differently — for resignification and subversion — a hopeful twist on Foucault's sometimes airless picture of pervasive power.

So Butler continues Foucault by taking his productive conception of power, his critique of the sovereign subject, and his analysis of the regulation of bodies and sexuality, and using them to theorize gender as performatively produced by regulatory norms rather than expressing any natural essence. Foucault gave her the subject and the body as effects of power; Butler applied this to gender, and found in the repetition through which power produces us the very possibility of undoing it.
