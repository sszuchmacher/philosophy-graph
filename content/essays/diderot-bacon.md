When Diderot and d'Alembert set out to edit the *Encyclopédie*, the vast collaborative compendium of Enlightenment knowledge, they placed a single thinker at the head of their enterprise as its prophet: **Francis Bacon**. The great preliminary discourse hails him as the visionary who had, more than a century earlier, imagined exactly the project they were undertaking.

Bacon had dreamed of a total reform and reorganization of human knowledge. He called for a "great instauration," a systematic gathering of all that is known and could be known, torn free from scholastic authority and reoriented toward the **relief of man's estate** — knowledge as power to improve human life. He had even sketched a classification of all the branches of learning, mapped onto the human faculties of memory, reason, and imagination.

The *Encyclopédie* is the realization of this dream in print. Diderot adopts Bacon's very map of knowledge — the famous "tree" of human understanding dividing the sciences by faculty — as the organizing scheme of the whole work. More deeply, he takes over Bacon's *spirit*: the conviction that knowledge should be practical, cooperative, and cumulative; the dignity of the mechanical arts and crafts, which the *Encyclopédie* documented in loving detail with its famous plates of workshops and machines; and the confidence that assembling and diffusing knowledge would advance human happiness and break the hold of superstition.

There is also a shared subversive edge. Bacon's exaltation of experience and utility over inherited authority becomes, in Diderot's hands, a weapon of the Enlightenment against Church and tradition — the *Encyclopédie* smuggling skepticism and materialism between its useful articles.

The continuity, then, is the transformation of a Baconian *program* into an actual institution of print. What Bacon had projected as an ideal — the organized, practical, collective advancement and diffusion of all knowledge — Diderot and his hundreds of contributors built, making the *Encyclopédie* the monument of the Enlightenment and the concrete fulfillment of Bacon's founding vision of science as a public, progressive enterprise.
