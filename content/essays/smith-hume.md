Adam Smith and David Hume were the closest of friends — Hume the elder, Smith his devoted companion and, at Hume's death, his brave public eulogist — and Smith's moral philosophy grows directly out of Hume's.

The shared foundation is **sentimentalism**: the view, against the rationalists, that morality is rooted not in reason but in *feeling*. Hume had argued that reason alone can never move us to act or found a moral distinction — "reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions" — and that our approval of virtue and disapproval of vice arise from a natural sentiment, refined and extended by **sympathy**, our capacity to share in the feelings of others. Moral judgment is a species of feeling, not a deliverance of pure intellect.

Smith takes this Humean sympathy and makes it the center of a more elaborate system in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*. Where Hume treated sympathy as a communication of feeling that grounds our approval, Smith builds a whole mechanism of moral judgment upon it — and adds a device Hume lacked: the **impartial spectator**. We judge our own conduct, Smith argues, by imagining how a fair, well-informed, disinterested observer within us would feel about it. Conscience is this internalized spectator, the "man within the breast," formed from our experience of sympathizing with and being judged by others.

This lets Smith answer a difficulty in Hume: how mere sympathy, which naturally favors those near us, can yield *impartial* moral standards. The imagined impartial spectator corrects the partiality of raw feeling, generalizing sympathy into genuine moral judgment.

So the relationship is continuity and refinement. Smith inherits Hume's core thesis — that morality springs from sentiment and sympathy, not reason — and develops it into a richer social psychology of conscience. And the same figure of the impartial spectator quietly underlies the account, in *The Wealth of Nations*, of self-interested exchange coordinated for the common good. Humean sympathy, refined by Smith, stands behind both his ethics and his economics.
