Heraclitus taught that everything flows — that the world is a ceaseless becoming with no fixed state to be grasped. He meant it as cosmology: the deep truth of nature is change, ordered by a hidden logos.

Protagoras draws a startling consequence about *knowledge*. If things are always in flux, never resting in a stable being, then there is nothing fixed for a judgment to correspond to. What is left is only how things *appear* to each perceiver at each moment. And so: **man is the measure of all things** — of the things that are, that they are, and of the things that are not, that they are not.

The wind that feels cold to me and warm to you is not really one or the other; it is cold-for-me and warm-for-you, and both are true. Take away the changeless world behind appearances, and you remove any standpoint from which one perception could correct another.

This is the Heraclitean flux turned from a doctrine about nature into a doctrine about truth — relativism. Plato saw the lineage clearly and attacked them together: in the *Theaetetus* he ties the man-measure thesis directly to the secret teaching that all things move, and argues that if it were so, the sophist's own claim to teach wisdom would collapse, since no view could be better than any other.

What Heraclitus offered as the hardest of truths, Protagoras converts into the claim that there are no hard truths at all — only measures, and every man his own.
