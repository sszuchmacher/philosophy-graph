Aristotle's logical works were collected under the title the *Organon* — the "instrument" of reasoning — and for two thousand years they defined how Europe pursued knowledge: through the syllogism, deducing conclusions from accepted first principles. Francis Bacon set out to replace that instrument, and titled his manifesto the ***Novum Organum***, the New Instrument, in open challenge.

Bacon's charge is that the old logic produces words, not works. The syllogism can only rearrange what is already assumed; if the first principles are hasty or false — snatched from a few observations and vague common notions — no amount of valid deduction can repair them. Scholastic Aristotelianism, he argues, had spun elaborate systems that never touched nature and never yielded a single useful invention.

His alternative is a new **inductive method**: not leaping from a handful of instances to sweeping generalizations, but ascending patiently, by systematic observation and above all *experiment*, cataloguing instances and seeking negative cases, rising step by careful step toward general laws. Knowledge is to be built from the ground up, wrung from nature by active interrogation.

> Knowledge and human power are synonymous.

That equation names the deepest break. For Aristotle, the highest knowledge was contemplative — theory pursued for its own sake, the noblest activity of a free mind. For Bacon, knowledge exists "for the relief of man's estate," to give humanity command over nature and improve the conditions of life. Science becomes a collaborative, cumulative enterprise aimed at power.

Bacon first clears the ground by exposing the **idols** — the biases of mind, language, tradition, and dogma that distort inquiry. Then he lays out the method that would inspire the Royal Society and the whole program of experimental science. The Philosopher's instrument is retired; the modern one takes its place.
