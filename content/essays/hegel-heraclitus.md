In his lectures on the history of philosophy, Hegel paid Heraclitus the highest compliment he ever gave a predecessor:

> There is no proposition of Heraclitus which I have not adopted in my Logic.

The obscure Ephesian, writing more than two millennia earlier, had grasped, Hegel thought, the very principle of dialectic — and Hegel claimed him as a direct ancestor.

What Hegel found in Heraclitus was **becoming** as the truth of things. Where Parmenides had frozen reality into static Being and dismissed change as illusion, Heraclitus declared that everything flows, that reality is process and not fixed substance. For Hegel this is a decisive advance: the truth is not dead Being but *becoming*, the restless movement in which being and nothing pass into each other. His own *Logic* opens exactly here — with being and nothing collapsing into becoming as the first concrete category.

Even more, Hegel prized the Heraclitean **unity of opposites**. Heraclitus had said that the way up and the way down are one, that strife is justice, that opposites are identical — the harmony of the world is a tension of contraries. This is, for Hegel, the germ of the dialectic itself: that each thing contains its own negation, that opposites pass into and require each other, and that reality advances through contradiction rather than in spite of it.

So the continuity is Hegel finding, in the first thinker of flux and contradiction, the ancient anticipation of his own logic of development-through-opposition. What Heraclitus stated in dark, oracular fragments, Hegel claims to have unfolded into a complete systematic science.

Yet Hegel completes what Heraclitus left as flashing insight. Heraclitus intuited becoming and the unity of opposites but could not derive them systematically or show how the process is *rational* and directed. Hegel embeds the Heraclitean flux within a teleological dialectic that moves not in mere endless cycles but toward the self-knowledge of Spirit. The oldest philosopher of change becomes the acknowledged forerunner of the most ambitious philosophy of development ever built.
