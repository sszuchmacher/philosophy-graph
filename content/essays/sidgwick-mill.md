Henry Sidgwick inherited the utilitarian tradition of Bentham and Mill and gave it its most rigorous and self-critical form. His *Methods of Ethics* is utilitarianism grown scrupulously honest — a work that accepts the greatest-happiness principle while subjecting it, and its rivals, to an examination more careful than anything in his predecessors.

From Mill, Sidgwick takes the substance of utilitarianism: that the ultimate standard of right conduct is the maximization of happiness, impartially considered. But he found Mill's arguments loose and sometimes fallacious, and set out to rebuild the doctrine on firmer ground. He rejected Mill's notorious "proof" of the principle of utility — the slide from "each desires his own happiness" to "the general happiness is desirable" — as invalid, and abandoned Mill's attempt to distinguish *qualities* of pleasure as inconsistent with the hedonistic core.

Sidgwick's method is to compare the three great "methods of ethics" — egoism, intuitionism, and utilitarianism — not to demolish but to see how far each can be made coherent. His surprising result is that utilitarianism, properly understood, actually rests on **intuition**: the principle that one ought to aim at good on the whole, and that the good of any one person is no more important than another's, is itself a self-evident axiom grasped by rational insight. Utilitarianism does not oppose intuitionism but is grounded by it. And commonsense morality, examined closely, turns out to be "unconscious utilitarianism" — its rules roughly those that promote general happiness.

But Sidgwick's rigor also led him to a famous, troubling impasse: the **dualism of practical reason**. Reason, he concluded, seems to endorse *two* ultimate and independent principles — that I should promote the general happiness (utilitarianism) and that I should promote my own (rational egoism) — and it cannot fully reconcile them. Where Mill had breezily assumed the individual's good and the general good could be harmonized, Sidgwick honestly confronted the possibility that they cannot, leaving a crack at the foundation of ethics he could not close.

So Sidgwick continues Mill's utilitarianism by disciplining it — discarding its weak arguments, grounding it in rational intuition, reconciling it with common sense — and, in his very rigor, exposing a deep unresolved tension that Mill's more optimistic system had papered over. He is the hinge between classical utilitarianism and modern analytic moral philosophy.
