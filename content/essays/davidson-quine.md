Davidson was Quine's student, and he built his enormously influential philosophy of language and mind on foundations Quine had laid — carrying Quine's naturalism and holism forward while giving them a systematic, truth-theoretic form Quine never provided.

From Quine, Davidson takes several core commitments. First, the rejection of the **analytic-synthetic distinction** and of determinate meanings as entities — Davidson agrees there are no "meanings" to be grasped, and approaches language without them. Second, **holism**: that beliefs and meanings come only in interconnected systems, so no sentence has content in isolation. Third, the method of **radical translation** — Quine's thought-experiment of interpreting a wholly alien language from scratch, on the basis of a speaker's observable behavior and the situations that prompt assent. Davidson makes his own version, **radical interpretation**, the centerpiece of his philosophy.

But Davidson transforms Quine's framework in a crucial, constructive way. Quine's conclusions were largely skeptical — the *indeterminacy* of translation, the denial that there is a fact of the matter about meaning. Davidson turns the project positive: he asks what an adequate **theory of meaning** for a language would look like, and answers, drawing on Tarski, that it should take the form of a **theory of truth** — a systematic specification of the truth-conditions of every sentence ("'Snow is white' is true if and only if snow is white"). To know the meaning of a sentence is to know what would make it true. Where Quine was suspicious of meaning, Davidson reconstructs a rigorous account of it in terms of truth, without positing meanings as objects.

He also elevates the **principle of charity** into a foundational tool: to interpret others at all, we must assume they are largely rational and their beliefs largely true, for otherwise no interpretation could get started. This has deep consequences Quine did not draw — it implies that massive error and radical incommensurability are impossible, and it ties meaning, belief, and truth tightly together.

So Davidson continues Quine as the heir who takes his teacher's naturalism, holism, and method of radical translation and builds from them a positive, systematic philosophy of language. He keeps the Quinean suspicion of meanings-as-entities while showing how to give a rigorous theory of meaning through truth — turning Quine's skeptical results into the starting point for a constructive account of how understanding one another is possible at all.
