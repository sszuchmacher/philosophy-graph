Hume inherited from Berkeley the crucial empiricist weapon — the principle that every genuine idea must be traceable to an impression, and that notions failing this test are empty — and he turned it, remorselessly, on doctrines Berkeley had wanted to keep.

Berkeley had used the principle to abolish **material substance**: since we never perceive matter behind our ideas, matter is a meaningless posit, and only minds and their ideas exist. But Berkeley stopped there. He retained two things the same reasoning threatens: **spiritual substance** — the self, the mind that has the ideas — and God.

Hume refuses the exception. Apply the test to the self, he says, and look within: you never encounter a simple, continuing "I" that has your perceptions. You find only the perceptions themselves — a sensation, a feeling, a thought, in ceaseless succession. There is no impression of a self, and therefore no legitimate idea of one. The mind is not a spiritual substance but a **bundle of perceptions**, "a kind of theatre" with no stage and no spectator — nothing but the passing show.

The same corrosive principle dissolves **causation**. We never perceive a necessary connection between cause and effect, only one event following another; the "power" we ascribe to causes is merely a habit of expectation in the mind.

So Hume radicalizes Berkeley by removing the stopping-points. Berkeley had used empiricism to eliminate matter while sheltering the soul and God; Hume lets the same logic consume the substantial self as well, and leaves the mind a mere bundle, the world a sequence of impressions bound only by habit.

Where Berkeley's immaterialism was meant to serve piety — a world sustained by God, minds as real substances — Hume follows the premises wherever they lead, into a thoroughgoing skepticism. Consistent empiricism, he shows, undermines not only matter but the self, causation, and finally the pretension of reason to know anything necessary about the world at all.
