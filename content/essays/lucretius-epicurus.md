Three centuries after Epicurus, a Roman poet gave his philosophy its greatest monument. Lucretius's *On the Nature of Things* is Epicureanism entire — atoms and void, the mortality of the soul, the serene indifferent gods, the counsel against fearing death — set into six books of Latin hexameter of extraordinary power.

Lucretius adds no new doctrine; he is a disciple, and says so, hailing Epicurus as the man who first broke the crushing weight of religion and traveled in mind beyond "the flaming walls of the world." What he adds is *fire*. The dry maxims of the Garden become vivid argument and image: dust motes dancing in a sunbeam as evidence of unseen atomic motion, the swerve that frees the will, the plague of Athens that ends the poem in darkness.

> So potent was religion in persuading to evil deeds.

The line captures the mission. Lucretius, like Epicurus, treats philosophy as liberation from fear, and aims his poem like a weapon at superstition — the terror of the gods and of punishment after death that makes human beings miserable while they live.

The very form embodies a paradox he embraces: poetry, he says, is the sweet honey smeared on the rim of the cup so that we will drink the bitter medicine of truth. Epicurus had distrusted poetry; his Roman follower makes it the vehicle of the doctrine.

Through this poem — lost for centuries and famously rediscovered in 1417 — ancient atomism survived to help seed the scientific revolution. What Epicurus argued, Lucretius made unforgettable, carrying the materialist vision across the abyss of the Middle Ages.
