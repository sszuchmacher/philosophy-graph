Robert Nozick's *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974) was written as the great libertarian answer to Rawls's *A Theory of Justice*, published three years earlier. The two books define the central debate of late-twentieth-century political philosophy: the clash between Rawls's egalitarian liberalism and Nozick's libertarianism.

Nozick's core critique targets Rawls's **difference principle** and, more broadly, all "**patterned**" and "**end-state**" theories of distributive justice. Rawls had argued that a just distribution is one that satisfies a principle about *how things should be arranged* — inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged. Nozick rejects the whole idea that justice concerns the *pattern* of a distribution at all. Justice in holdings, he argues, is **historical** and about **entitlement**: a distribution is just if it *arose* justly — through legitimate original acquisition and voluntary transfer — regardless of the pattern it produces. If people justly acquire and freely exchange, whatever distribution results is just, however unequal.

His famous **Wilt Chamberlain** argument makes the point. Start from any distribution you consider just — even a perfectly equal one. Now let many people each freely choose to pay a small sum to watch Chamberlain play basketball. He grows rich; the distribution becomes unequal. But no one was wronged — every transfer was voluntary. So, Nozick concludes, to *maintain* Rawls's preferred pattern, you would have to **continuously interfere** with people's free choices, forbidding the capitalist acts between consenting adults that constantly upset any pattern. "Liberty upsets patterns."

Behind this lies a charge Nozick turns against Rawls: that redistributive taxation to benefit the worst-off treats the talented as **means** to others' ends, using their labor and gifts as a resource for society — even, he provocatively says, a kind of "forced labor." Individuals have inviolable **rights**, and the redistributive state violates them.

So Nozick's relation to Rawls is a foundational critique from the libertarian right. He rejects Rawls's difference principle and the whole project of patterning distribution according to a favored end-state, replacing it with a historical, entitlement theory in which justice is a matter of how holdings came about, not how they are arranged. Where Rawls asks how to arrange society to benefit the least advantaged, Nozick asks only whether holdings arose through just acquisition and free exchange — and argues that Rawlsian redistribution can be sustained only by continuously overriding the liberty of individuals. The Rawls–Nozick debate — patterned equality versus historical entitlement — became the defining axis of modern political philosophy.
