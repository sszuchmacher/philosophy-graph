Berkeley's immaterialism is usually read as pure British empiricism, but it owes a striking debt to the Continental, Cartesian theology of Malebranche — so much so that Berkeley took pains to distinguish himself from a thinker whose conclusions he half shared.

Malebranche had already loosened the grip of the material world. Through his **occasionalism**, he denied that bodies have any causal power at all: God is the sole true cause, and physical objects are mere "occasions" on which He acts. And through his doctrine that **we see all things in God**, he located the immediate objects of knowledge not in matter but in ideas within the divine mind. Matter, for Malebranche, is causally inert and epistemically remote — its existence, he admitted, known more by faith than by reason.

Berkeley takes these tendencies to their conclusion and simply removes the now-idle material world. If bodies cause nothing, and if what we directly perceive are ideas rather than matter, why retain matter at all? He keeps the crucial Malebranchean moves — that only spirits (minds) are genuine causes, and that the order of nature is God's activity — while dropping the inert material substratum Malebranche had strained to preserve.

The result is that **God plays the same central role** in both systems. For Malebranche, God causes our perceptions on the occasion of bodily events; for Berkeley, God directly produces the orderly train of ideas we call the physical world, so that nature is a kind of divine language spoken to finite minds. The regularity of experience is God's constancy.

Berkeley insisted he was no Malebranchean — he rejected the "vision in God" and grounded ideas in ordinary perception, not the divine intellect. But the lineage is clear: Malebranche had made matter causally powerless and epistemically dispensable, and Berkeley, pressing that logic, let it disappear entirely, reaching idealism from the Continental side even as he argued it from the empiricist one.
