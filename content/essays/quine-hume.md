Quine saw himself as a modern empiricist working in the great tradition of **Hume**, and he treated Hume's unsolved problems not as embarrassments to be explained away but as the honest starting point for a naturalized theory of knowledge.

The clearest continuity is over **induction** and the limits of empiricism. Hume had shown that our belief in causal necessity and in the uniformity of nature — that the future will resemble the past — has no rational justification: it cannot be proved by reason or experience without circularity, resting only on **habit**. Quine accepts this verdict fully and refuses to pretend it can be answered. Instead he draws a radical methodological conclusion: if the traditional project of *justifying* our knowledge of the world on secure foundations cannot succeed — if Hume's problem is genuinely unsolvable — then we should abandon that project and instead study empirically *how* human beings actually form beliefs from sensory input. This is **naturalized epistemology**: epistemology becomes a chapter of psychology, describing the causal process from "the meager input" of sensory stimulation to "the torrential output" of our theory of the world.

Quine also inherits Hume's **empiricism** about the origins of knowledge — that all our information about the world comes through the senses — and his skepticism about entities that outrun experience. And he echoes the Humean problem of the **self** and of objects: Hume had dissolved the self into a bundle of perceptions and worried that we never observe the connections we posit; Quine similarly treats physical objects as "posits" — useful conceptual additions we make to systematize sensory experience, comparable, he provocatively says, to the gods of Homer, differing only in degree of usefulness.

But Quine equips Humean empiricism with what Hume lacked: modern **logic**, **holism**, and the resources of natural science. Where Hume analyzed the mind with introspective psychology and rough associationism, Quine reconstrues empiricism as a scientific study of language-learning and theory-formation, and replaces Hume's atomistic impressions with the holistic "web of belief" that meets experience as a whole.

So Quine continues Hume as the empiricist who takes the tradition's deepest problem — the ungroundability of our knowledge of the world — with complete seriousness, and responds not by solving it but by transforming the discipline. Hume's skeptical result becomes Quine's charter for naturalizing epistemology: since we cannot justify our knowledge from outside science, we should study it from within, as the natural process by which a physical organism, bombarded by stimulation, comes to construct a theory of its world.
