Karl Popper's philosophy of science begins from **Hume's problem of induction** — which Popper regarded as one of the most important problems in philosophy, and which his own theory of falsification was designed to solve, or rather to dissolve.

Hume had shown that **induction** — inferring general laws or future predictions from past observations — cannot be rationally justified. No number of observed instances (all swans so far are white) logically guarantees a universal conclusion or the next case; and any attempt to justify the assumption that the future will resemble the past is either circular (it assumes induction) or unfounded. Our expectations, Hume concluded, rest on **habit** and psychological custom, not on reason. This is a devastating result: science, which seems to proceed by inductive generalization from evidence, would have no rational foundation.

Popper **accepts Hume's logic entirely**. Induction, he agrees, is invalid; there is no valid inference from particular observations to universal laws, and no amount of confirming instances can prove a scientific theory. On the purely logical point, Hume was simply right, and Popper never disputes it.

But Popper rejects Hume's despairing, psychological conclusion — that science therefore rests only on irrational habit — by **denying that science uses induction at all**. This is his radical solution. Science, Popper argues, does not proceed by generalizing from observations to laws; it proceeds by **conjecture and refutation**. Scientists boldly *guess* universal theories (by whatever creative, non-logical means) and then try to *refute* them by testing. And here logic is back on our side: while no number of observations can *prove* a universal law, a single counter-observation can **disprove** it (one black swan refutes "all swans are white"). Falsification is deductively valid where induction is not.

So Popper dissolves Hume's problem rather than solving it. We never needed induction: knowledge grows not by accumulating confirmations but by proposing falsifiable conjectures and eliminating the false ones through severe testing. Rationality lies not in justifying theories from evidence (impossible, as Hume showed) but in subjecting them to criticism and refutation.

So Popper continues Hume by taking the problem of induction as his starting point and accepting Hume's proof that induction is logically invalid — while overturning Hume's conclusion. Where Hume, unable to justify induction, fell back on habit and a mitigated skepticism, Popper argues that science never rested on induction in the first place, and replaces inductive justification with the deductive logic of falsification. Hume showed that we cannot prove our theories; Popper answered that we do not need to — we need only be able to refute them, and that is enough for reason and for science.
