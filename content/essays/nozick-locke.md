Nozick's libertarianism is, at its foundation, a modern revival and radicalization of **Locke**. *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* builds its theory of the minimal state and its entitlement theory of justice directly on Lockean natural rights and the Lockean account of property.

From Locke, Nozick takes the framework of **natural rights** possessed by individuals prior to and independent of government — rights to life, liberty, and above all **property** — which set moral "side constraints" that no one, including the state, may violate. Individuals are inviolable, self-owning beings with rights that function as absolute limits on how they may be treated; this Lockean rights-based individualism is the moral bedrock of Nozick's whole position.

He also takes over Locke's account of the **origin of property**: that individuals come to own unowned things by mixing their labor with them, acquiring legitimate title through original acquisition. Nozick's **entitlement theory** — that a distribution is just if it arose through just acquisition and just transfer — is a direct descendant of Locke's labor theory of appropriation and his emphasis on the legitimacy of freely transferred holdings. And Nozick engages carefully with the famous **Lockean proviso**, Locke's condition that appropriation is legitimate so long as "enough and as good" is left for others, reworking it into his own weaker version.

Most distinctively, Nozick reconstructs Locke's **social contract** in reverse. Where Locke argued that individuals leave the state of nature to form a government that protects their rights, Nozick tells an "invisible hand" story showing how a **minimal state** — a "night-watchman" state limited to protection against force, theft, and fraud, and the enforcement of contracts — could arise from a Lockean state of nature through legitimate steps, without violating anyone's rights. But he insists, against the more expansive liberal tradition, that only this minimal state is justified: any **more extensive state**, one that redistributes wealth or provides welfare, necessarily violates the very natural rights that justified forming a state in the first place.

So Nozick continues Locke as the libertarian heir who takes Lockean natural rights, self-ownership, and the labor theory of property to their most rigorous conclusion. He inherits the inviolable individual with rights that constrain the state, the acquisition of property through labor and free transfer, and the contractarian justification of government — while radicalizing them into a defense of the minimal state alone and a rejection of all redistribution as a violation of rights. Locke grounded limited government in natural rights; Nozick uses the same rights to argue that any state beyond the barest protective minimum is unjust.
