For all his debts to German phenomenology, Sartre is at bottom a French philosopher in the line of Descartes, and he embraced the label. His existentialism begins, like Descartes's philosophy, from the immediate certainty of **consciousness** — the *cogito* — and makes the free, self-aware subject the center of everything.

The Cartesian starting point is explicit. Sartre insists that philosophy must begin from the individual consciousness's certainty of itself: "there can be no other truth to take off from than this: I think, therefore I am." Existentialism, he says, is the only theory that gives man dignity by starting from the subject's own consciousness rather than dissolving him into nature or history. The reflective self-presence of consciousness is the ground on which Sartre builds his ontology of the "for-itself."

He also inherits Descartes's radical dualism, transformed. Descartes had split reality into thinking substance (*res cogitans*) and extended substance (*res extensa*), mind and matter. Sartre's fundamental division of **being-for-itself** (consciousness — free, a nothingness, never coinciding with itself) and **being-in-itself** (the dense, self-identical being of things) is a recognizable descendant — the conscious subject set radically apart from the massive, inert plenitude of the world.

Above all, Sartre takes over and radicalizes the Cartesian theme of **freedom**. Descartes had held that the will is infinite, that our freedom is boundless even though our understanding is limited — the one respect in which we resemble God. Sartre seizes this and makes absolute freedom the very being of consciousness: because the for-itself is a nothingness with no given essence, it is "condemned to be free," the author of itself and its world, with a freedom as unconditioned as Descartes's infinite will.

But Sartre cuts away Descartes's God, who had guaranteed truth and grounded the self. In a godless world the Cartesian free subject stands alone, without divine backing, wholly responsible for the meaning it creates.

So Sartre continues Descartes by making the self-certain, free conscious subject the foundation of philosophy — the cogito, the mind/world dualism, the infinite freedom of the will — while stripping away the God who once secured it. Existentialism is, in large part, Cartesianism after the death of God: the free subject left utterly on its own.
