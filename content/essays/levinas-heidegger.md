Levinas was among the first to bring Heidegger to France, and he never ceased to regard *Being and Time* as one of the greatest philosophical works ever written. But his own philosophy is a profound and morally charged **revolt** against Heidegger — a revolt sharpened by history, since Heidegger's embrace of Nazism confirmed for Levinas, a Jew who lost most of his family in the Holocaust, that something was catastrophically wrong at the root of Heidegger's thought.

The core of the quarrel is the primacy of **Being** versus the primacy of **ethics**. Heidegger had made the question of Being the fundamental question of philosophy: everything is to be understood in terms of Being, and the human being, Dasein, matters as the site where Being is disclosed. Ontology — the understanding of Being — is first philosophy.

Levinas reverses this order absolutely. **Ethics**, he insists, is first philosophy — prior to ontology, prior to Being. What is most fundamental is not my relation to Being but my relation to the **Other person**, encountered in the **face**. The face of the other human being confronts me with an infinite ethical demand — "thou shalt not kill," the summons to responsibility — that comes before and grounds all knowing and all understanding of Being. Before I comprehend the world, I am already claimed, called into responsibility, by the Other.

From this standpoint Heidegger's philosophy looks not merely incomplete but dangerous. By subordinating the individual to the anonymous horizon of Being, Levinas argues, Heidegger's ontology dissolves the singular Other into a totality, treating persons as instances of Being rather than as absolute ethical claims. A philosophy in which Being comes first has no place for the irreducible, commanding singularity of the other person.

So Levinas's critique is a moral inversion of Heidegger. He keeps the phenomenological depth and much of the language, but overturns the hierarchy: not Being but the Other, not ontology but ethics, not the disclosure of the world but responsibility for another's face. Against Heidegger's philosophy of Being, Levinas sets a philosophy in which the ethical relation to the Other is the first and deepest fact of human existence — and in which philosophy must begin not with wonder at Being but with responsibility before another's vulnerability.
