Camus is unimaginable without Nietzsche. The problems he wrestles with — the death of God, the collapse of inherited meaning, the threat of nihilism, and the search for a way to affirm life without illusions — are Nietzsche's problems, and Camus takes them up as the defining crisis of the modern age.

From Nietzsche, Camus inherits the starting point: **God is dead**, and with him the transcendent guarantees of meaning, value, and cosmic justice. The universe is silent, indifferent, without inherent purpose. This is the ground of what Camus calls the **absurd** — the collision between the human craving for meaning and a world that offers none. Nietzsche had faced the same abyss and named the danger: nihilism, the conclusion that if there is no God and no given meaning, then nothing matters and life is worthless.

And from Nietzsche, Camus takes the essential task: to overcome nihilism *without* fleeing back to God or illusion — to find, in a meaningless universe, a way of saying **Yes** to life. Nietzsche's *amor fati*, his affirmation of existence in all its suffering, his call to create value in the absence of given meaning, is the deep source of Camus's own project. Camus's answer to the absurd — **revolt**, living fully and lucidly in defiance of meaninglessness, and his image of Sisyphus, who must be imagined happy — is a Nietzschean affirmation: embracing this life, this earth, without appeal to any beyond.

But Camus is a gentler, more humane heir. He recoils from the harder edges of Nietzsche — the will to power, the contempt for the weak, the aristocratic cruelty — and in *The Rebel* he explicitly worries about how the death of God can lead to murder and tyranny when human beings try to play god themselves. His affirmation is bound to solidarity, limits, and a Mediterranean love of ordinary life and human decency, not to the overman's self-overcoming.

So Camus continues Nietzsche by taking up his diagnosis — the death of God, the absurd, the peril of nihilism — and his imperative to affirm life without transcendence, while softening it into a humanist ethic of revolt, moderation, and solidarity. Nietzsche taught him the question and the demand for affirmation; Camus answers in a warmer, more compassionate voice, seeking to say Yes to life while refusing the cruelty a Nietzschean liberation might unleash.
