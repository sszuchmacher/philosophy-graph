Rawls's theory of justice belongs to the **social contract** tradition that Hobbes inaugurated, and though Rawls's conclusions are worlds apart from Hobbes's absolutism, he inherits from Hobbes crucial elements of contractarian method and the analysis of justice as the product of rational agreement.

From Hobbes, Rawls takes the founding move of modern political philosophy: that the principles governing society are to be justified by imagining what rational individuals would **agree to** from a suitably characterized starting situation, in order to secure benefits they could not achieve alone. Hobbes derived the sovereign from what self-interested people in the state of nature would rationally consent to for the sake of peace; Rawls derives principles of justice from what parties in the original position would rationally choose. The idea that legitimacy and justice rest on rational agreement among individuals concerned to advance their interests is a Hobbesian inheritance.

Rawls also takes seriously the Hobbesian emphasis on the **rationality of the contracting parties** and on justice as a matter of mutual advantage and the terms of cooperation. His parties in the original position are, like Hobbes's individuals, rational agents seeking to advance their own conception of the good; the theory is built on the machinery of **rational choice**, asking what it would be prudent to agree to under specified conditions.

But Rawls decisively corrects and constrains the Hobbesian starting point, and the difference defines his project. Hobbes's contractors know their strengths, weaknesses, and advantages, so the bargain reflects the balance of power — the strong get more, and the outcome, Rawls thinks, is a mere *modus vivendi* reflecting existing inequalities rather than genuine justice. Rawls imposes the **veil of ignorance**: the parties choose in ignorance of their own position, talents, and circumstances, so that no one can rig the principles to favor themselves. This transforms a Hobbesian bargain among unequally situated egoists into a genuinely **fair** procedure yielding principles of justice rather than a truce dictated by power. And Rawls's parties, unlike Hobbes's, are moved by a sense of justice, not by fear alone.

So Rawls continues Hobbes as an heir of contractarianism — justice and legitimacy grounded in rational agreement among individuals seeking the benefits of cooperation, analyzed through rational choice. But he reforms the tradition by adding the veil of ignorance, converting Hobbes's power-laden bargain into a fair choice-situation, and so turning the contract from a justification of sovereign power for the sake of security into a derivation of principles of justice for a society of free and equal persons. Hobbes gave him the contractarian engine; Rawls rebuilt it to yield fairness rather than mere order.
