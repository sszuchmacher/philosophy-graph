Descartes had grounded knowledge in **innate ideas** — truths the mind possesses prior to any experience: the idea of God, of the self, of substance, the axioms of mathematics, stamped into reason by its very nature. Certainty was to be found by turning away from the deceptive senses toward these clear ideas within.

Locke opens his *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* by demolishing this doctrine. The mind at birth, he argues, is a **blank slate** — *tabula rasa* — with no writing on it at all. There are no innate principles, speculative or moral. His evidence is empirical: if certain ideas were truly stamped on every mind, they would be universally acknowledged, yet children have no grasp of the supposed innate axioms, and different cultures hold wildly different moral principles. What looks innate is merely learned early and its origin forgotten.

Where, then, does the mind's furniture come from? **Experience** — and experience alone. All our ideas derive from two fountains: **sensation**, the input of the outer senses, and **reflection**, the mind's observation of its own operations. Even the most abstract notions are assembled out of simple ideas ultimately traceable to these sources.

The reversal reorients philosophy. Descartes had made reason the source of substantive knowledge and treated the senses with suspicion; Locke makes the senses the source and confines reason to combining and analyzing what experience supplies. Knowledge is not read off from innate ideas but built, painstakingly, from perception.

This is the founding gesture of British **empiricism**, and it will run through Berkeley and Hume into the whole modern debate about the limits of knowledge. Against the inborn mind, Locke sets the mind written on by the world.
