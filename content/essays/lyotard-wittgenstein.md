Alongside Kant, the later **Wittgenstein** is a crucial resource of Lyotard's mature philosophy — above all the concept of **language games**, which Lyotard adapts to describe a world after the collapse of unifying grand narratives.

From Wittgenstein, Lyotard takes the idea that language is not one uniform system representing reality but a plurality of **language games** — different kinds of utterance (describing, commanding, questioning, promising, judging), each governed by its own rules, its own criteria of what counts as a legitimate move, with no single meta-game standing above them all. Meaning is use, bound to particular practices and forms of life, irreducibly many.

Lyotard makes this **plurality and heterogeneity** central to *The Postmodern Condition*. The "postmodern" situation, for him, is one in which the grand **metanarratives** — the Enlightenment story of progress, the Hegelian-Marxist story of emancipation, the speculative story of the unity of knowledge — that once legitimated knowledge and tied all discourses together have lost their credibility. What remains is a dispersed multiplicity of language games and "little narratives," local and heterogeneous, with no overarching narrative to unify or adjudicate them. Wittgenstein's irreducible plurality of games becomes Lyotard's picture of a fragmented postmodern knowledge that cannot be totalized.

He radicalizes Wittgenstein in the direction of **incommensurability and conflict**. In *The Differend*, Lyotard develops the idea that different "phrase regimes" and "genres of discourse" are heterogeneous — governed by incompatible rules — so that there is often *no common rule* by which to settle a dispute between them. To force one game's criteria onto another is to commit a wrong, a **differend**: a conflict that cannot be justly resolved because the parties lack a shared idiom, and where applying a single measure silences the wronged party. This is Wittgenstein's plurality of games hardened into an ethics and politics of irreducible difference: the task is not to unify the games under a master discourse but to respect their heterogeneity and to "bear witness" to the differends any imposed consensus would suppress.

So Lyotard continues Wittgenstein by taking the plurality of language games — each with its own rules, no meta-game above them — and making it the framework for understanding knowledge and justice after the metanarratives. He inherits the anti-totalizing, pluralist picture of language and use, and extends it into a philosophy of incommensurability, dissensus, and the differend. Where Wittgenstein described the multiplicity of games with a certain quietist calm, Lyotard turns it into a critical weapon against every attempt to impose a single narrative or consensus.
