Aquinas is remembered as the great Aristotelian, but he was raised in an intellectual world that was overwhelmingly **Augustinian**, and he never left it behind. For eight centuries, to do Christian theology was to think with Augustine — on grace, sin, the Trinity, the soul's ascent to God. Aquinas's originality was to graft Aristotle onto that Augustinian trunk without cutting it down.

On the essentials of the Christian life, Aquinas remains Augustine's heir. Human nature, wounded by original sin, cannot save itself; it needs **grace**, freely given, to be healed and raised toward God. The final happiness of the human person is not Aristotle's contemplative life but the Augustinian vision of God, in which the restless heart at last finds rest. The priority of grace, the reality of the Fall, the supernatural destiny of the soul — all are Augustinian bedrock beneath the Aristotelian structure.

What Aquinas changes is the account of **nature**. Augustine, shaped by Neoplatonism, tended to see the natural world as a shadow to be transcended and knowledge as divine illumination of the mind. Aquinas, armed with Aristotle, grants nature its own solidity: the senses really do give knowledge, the mind abstracts truth from things rather than receiving it by illumination, and natural reason can travel a long way on its own before grace completes it.

The famous formula captures the synthesis: **grace does not destroy nature but perfects it**. Augustine supplies the grace, the fallen will, the beatifying end; Aristotle supplies a nature robust enough to be perfected rather than merely eclipsed. Aquinas holds the two together, keeping the Augustinian soul while giving it an Aristotelian body — and so reconciling the two greatest authorities the Latin West possessed.
