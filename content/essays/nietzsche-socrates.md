For Nietzsche, Socrates is one of the great turning points — and disasters — of Western culture. In *The Birth of Tragedy*, and again at the end of his life, he returns obsessively to the Athenian, treating him not as the hero of philosophy but as a **symptom** of decline.

Nietzsche's Greece is animated by two forces: the Dionysian (intoxication, instinct, the tragic acceptance of suffering and flux) and the Apollonian (form, measure, image). Great tragic culture held the two in tension. Socrates, Nietzsche argues, destroyed that balance by introducing a third, alien principle: **rationalism** — the demand that everything be justified before the tribunal of reason, that to be good one must give reasons, that knowledge is virtue and the unexamined life not worth living.

This "theoretical optimism" — the faith that reason can penetrate and correct existence, that the world is fundamentally intelligible and can be made good through knowledge — is, for Nietzsche, a betrayal of life. It devalues instinct and the tragic affirmation of existence in favor of a bloodless dialectic. Socrates could no longer *live* on instinct; he had to justify life at the bar of reason, which Nietzsche reads as a sign of decadence — an organism whose instincts have fallen into anarchy and must be tyrannized by consciousness.

> Is the resolve to be so scientific about everything perhaps a kind of fear of, an escape from, pessimism?

There is even an ugly, ambivalent fascination: Nietzsche calls Socrates the "buffoon who got himself taken seriously," and reads his cheerful acceptance of death as possibly a will to die, a weariness with life masked as wisdom.

The critique is a diagnosis. Socrates marks the moment when Western thought turned against instinct and tragic affirmation and enthroned reason, science, and morality as the meaning of existence — the beginning of the long decadence that runs, for Nietzsche, through Plato and Christianity down to modern rationalism. To overcome nihilism, one must, in a sense, overcome Socrates.
