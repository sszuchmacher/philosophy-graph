Beauvoir drew on Heidegger as well as Sartre, and *The Second Sex* deploys Heideggerian concepts — thrownness, situation, and above all the tension between transcendence and immanence — to analyze the condition of women.

From Heidegger comes the picture of human existence as **being-in-the-world**: not a detached consciousness but a concrete existence thrown into a situation, defined by its projects and its finitude, always already engaged in a world it did not choose. Beauvoir uses this to insist that woman must be understood not as a fixed essence or biological given but as a *situation* — a way of existing in a world, shaped by circumstances, into which she is thrown and within which she must project herself.

The key existentialist opposition she wields is between **transcendence** and **immanence**. Authentic existence, for this tradition, is transcendence — the free surpassing of the given toward chosen projects and an open future, the making of oneself. Its opposite is immanence — a sinking into passivity, repetition, and the mere maintenance of life, a falling away from freedom into thing-like existence.

Beauvoir's central charge is that women have been systematically confined to **immanence**. Man has claimed transcendence — the roles of action, creation, and projection into the world — while confining woman to immanence: the endless, repetitive labor of the household and reproduction, the maintenance of life rather than the transcending of it toward projects that reshape the world. Woman is condemned to be an object where she should be a free subject, denied the transcendence that existentialist thought holds to be the meaning of authentic existence.

So Beauvoir continues the Heideggerian-existentialist analysis by turning it into a diagnostic of oppression. She takes being-in-the-world, thrownness, and the transcendence/immanence structure and shows how patriarchal society has distributed them by sex — granting transcendence to men and imposing immanence on women. The abstract categories of existential ontology become tools for exposing a concrete injustice, and the demand for authentic existence becomes a demand for women's liberation into the transcendence they have been denied.
