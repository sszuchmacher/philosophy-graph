Spinoza began as a Cartesian — his first published work was a geometric exposition of Descartes's *Principles* — and his mature philosophy can be read as Descartes carried through with a rigor that shatters the original system. He takes Descartes's own definitions and follows them where Descartes dared not go.

The crux is **substance**. Descartes had defined substance as that which needs nothing else in order to exist — and then, awkwardly, spoke of many substances: God, and countless created minds and bodies. Spinoza pounces on the inconsistency. If a substance is truly self-sufficient, depending on nothing else, then created things — which depend utterly on God — cannot be substances at all. By Descartes's own definition, there can be only **one substance**: God, the sole self-caused, independent reality. Everything else — every mind, every body — is not a thing in its own right but a *mode*, a modification of the one substance.

This single stroke collapses the Cartesian architecture. Descartes's two created substances, thinking mind and extended matter, become in Spinoza two **attributes** of the one God — two ways the single reality expresses itself. The mind-body problem dissolves: mind and body are not separate substances needing to interact but the same thing conceived under two attributes. And Descartes's transcendent Creator becomes **Deus sive Natura**, God-or-Nature, immanent in everything, not standing outside the world but constituting it.

The results were, for the seventeenth century, monstrous — pantheism, the denial of a personal providential God, strict determinism, the loss of free will and personal immortality. Spinoza was excommunicated and reviled as an atheist.

Yet the philosophy is Descartes's own logic set free. Spinoza keeps the geometric method, the demand for clear self-sufficient first principles, the mechanistic determinism — and, refusing Descartes's compromises, drives them to the one God-substance Descartes's definitions implied but his piety would not allow.
