There is a startling symmetry in Stoicism's late flowering: its two most read voices are a freed slave and a Roman emperor — and the emperor learned from the slave. Marcus Aurelius, ruler of the known world, kept Epictetus's teaching close and named him among those to whom he owed his soul.

The *Meditations* are Epictetus applied under the heaviest of burdens. Marcus repeats the master's central discipline — attend only to what is up to you, your own judgments and will; let go of what is not — but now the man practicing it commands armies, judges the empire, and cannot lay the weight down. Here Stoicism is tested at the opposite extreme from slavery: not powerlessness but total power, which proves to be its own kind of trap.

> You have power over your mind — not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength.

That is Epictetus's dichotomy of control, written by a man who could order anything and still could not control fate, illness, betrayal, or death. The *Meditations* are not a treatise but a private notebook — Marcus reminding himself, each morning, of doctrines he already knows and must renew against grief and exhaustion.

The transmission shows Stoicism doing exactly what it promised: leveling the distance between slave and emperor. If the only good is virtue and the only freedom is inner, then a lame ex-slave and the master of Rome face the same task with the same resources. Marcus reading Epictetus is the philosophy proving its own claim — that where you stand in the world is an "indifferent," and the work of living well is the same for everyone.
