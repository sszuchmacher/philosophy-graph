Feyerabend began as one of **Popper's** most brilliant students and followers, a member of the Popperian circle in London — and became one of Popper's most devastating apostates, turning critical rationalism inside out into "epistemological anarchism." His relation to Popper is that of a rebel heir who used the master's own weapons to destroy the master's system.

From Popper, Feyerabend inherited the anti-inductivism, the emphasis on bold conjecture, the importance of **criticism** and of confronting theories with alternatives, and above all Popper's insistence on the **proliferation** of competing theories as vital to progress. Popper had argued that we test and criticize theories most effectively when we have rivals to compare them with; Feyerabend seized on this pluralist strand and made it central. In a sense, he claimed to be *more* Popperian than Popper — pushing the demand for proliferation and criticism further than Popper was willing to go.

But Feyerabend turned on the core of Popperianism: the belief that there is a **rational method** demarcating science, embodied in the rule of **falsification** and critical testing. Popper had offered falsificationism as *the* methodology of science and the criterion separating science from pseudo-science. Feyerabend argues, from detailed history (especially the Galileo case), that this and *every* proposed methodological rule is regularly and rightly **violated** in real scientific practice — that if scientists had obeyed Popper's rules, or any fixed rules, the great advances would never have happened. There is no method that science follows or should follow: "**anything goes**." Falsificationism, far from capturing the logic of science, would have strangled it; Galileo advanced by propaganda, ad hoc hypotheses, and the flouting of the evidence and the rules.

Feyerabend also rejects Popper's sharp **demarcation** between science and non-science, and Popper's implicit elevation of science as the paragon of rationality. If science has no unique method and proceeds like any other tradition, then it has no special claim to authority over myth, magic, or other forms of knowledge — and in a **free society**, Feyerabend provocatively argues, science should be separated from the state and stripped of its privileged monopoly, just as the church once was.

So Feyerabend's relation to Popper is a critique from within that becomes a repudiation. He keeps Popper's emphasis on criticism, alternatives, and the proliferation of theories — radicalizing the pluralism — while demolishing Popper's central claim that science is governed by a rational method of falsification and demarcated from non-science by it. Where Popper made falsifiability the badge of scientific rationality, Feyerabend concludes that there are no binding rules, that "anything goes," and that science deserves no privileged authority. The student took Popper's love of bold conjecture and open criticism and used it to argue that Popper's own methodology was one more rule to be broken.
