Early in his career Russell wrote a book on Leibniz — *A Critical Exposition of the Philosophy of Leibniz* — that is one of his most revealing works, for what he admires and for what he rejects. His central thesis is that Leibniz's whole extraordinary metaphysics follows from his **logic**, and specifically from a mistake about it.

Russell argues that Leibniz was committed to the view that every proposition is ultimately of **subject-predicate form** — that every truth consists in attributing a predicate to a subject, so that all real properties are monadic, belonging to a single substance. And from this logical doctrine, Russell shows, Leibniz's astonishing metaphysics flows with a certain inevitability: if all propositions are subject-predicate, there can be no irreducible **relations** between substances, so substances cannot really interact (hence the windowless monads and the pre-established harmony), and each substance must contain within its own complete concept everything true of it (hence the mirroring of the universe in each monad).

Russell's critique, then, is that Leibniz's fantastical system — monads, harmony, the best of all possible worlds — rests on a **logical error**: the failure to recognize genuine, irreducible **relations**. Once you admit, as Russell insists, that "a is greater than b" is a real relational fact not reducible to predicates of a and b separately, the entire monadology loses its foundation. The same doctrine of external relations with which Russell overthrew Hegel he here turns on Leibniz.

But there is deep admiration mixed with the critique. Russell prized Leibniz as a pioneer of **logic** — a thinker who glimpsed the project of a universal formal calculus of reasoning (the *characteristica universalis*) that Frege and Russell himself would finally realize. He saw Leibniz as an analytic philosopher born too soon, whose metaphysics was the flawed by-product of genuine logical insight.

So Russell's relation to Leibniz is a critical diagnosis by a kindred logical mind. He credits Leibniz with grasping the centrality of logic to philosophy — the very conviction that defines Russell's own work — while arguing that Leibniz's specific logic, blind to relations, generated a whole baroque metaphysics that a correct logic dissolves. It is analytic philosophy reading its own prehistory: Leibniz as a great logician led astray by an impoverished theory of the proposition.
