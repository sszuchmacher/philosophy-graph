Marcuse's Marxism was thoroughly **Hegelian** — he was among the foremost interpreters of Hegel in the twentieth century, and his study *Reason and Revolution* (1941) was a landmark defense of Hegel at a moment when Hegel was being smeared as a forerunner of totalitarianism.

Against that slander, Marcuse insisted that Hegel's philosophy was, at its core, a philosophy of **freedom and critical reason** — and that its true heir was not reaction but Marx and revolutionary theory. The key is the Hegelian concept of **negativity** and the critical power of reason. For Hegel, Marcuse argued, "reason" is not a passive acceptance of what exists but a power that negates the given, measures reality against its rational potential, and drives beyond every limited, unfree state of affairs toward the realization of freedom. Dialectical reason is inherently *critical*: it refuses to take the existing world as final, seeing in every actual thing the contradiction that propels it toward something higher.

This "power of negative thinking" is the thread Marcuse draws from Hegel through Marx to his own critical theory. Marx's dialectic — the analysis of capitalism's internal contradictions driving toward its overcoming — is, on Marcuse's reading, Hegelian negativity made concrete and material. And Marcuse's own critique of "one-dimensional society" is essentially a lament that advanced capitalism has flattened precisely this Hegelian **negativity**: it has produced a one-dimensional thought that can no longer conceive the negation of the given, that has lost the critical, transcending power of reason and can only affirm and administer what is. The whole diagnosis presupposes the Hegelian standard of a reason that ought to negate and surpass the existing order.

Marcuse also takes from Hegel (and the early Marx) the vision of a reconciliation of subject and object, humanity and nature — the idea that freedom means overcoming alienation and achieving a rational, unalienated relation between human beings and their world — which grounds his utopian hope for liberation.

So Marcuse continues Hegel by defending and deploying the dialectic as a philosophy of freedom and critical, negating reason. He reads Hegel not as the apologist of the Prussian state but as the thinker of negativity whose critical rationalism passes into Marx and into critical theory — and he turns the Hegelian power of negative thinking into the very measure by which he condemns a modern society that has extinguished it.
