For most of the early Middle Ages, the Latin West knew Aristotle almost entirely through one man. Boethius, a Roman senator in the twilight of the empire, set himself the immense task of translating all of Plato and Aristotle into Latin and reconciling them. He was executed before he got far — but the fragment he completed shaped a thousand years of thought.

What he finished was Aristotle's **logic**: the *Categories* and *On Interpretation*, with his commentaries, plus Porphyry's introduction. Through the long centuries when the rest of Aristotle — the *Metaphysics*, the *Physics*, the ethical and biological works — was simply *unavailable* in the West, this slender logical corpus, the so-called "old logic," was the whole of Aristotle that survived. Every medieval schoolboy learned to reason from Boethius's Aristotle.

He also handed the Middle Ages one of its defining problems. Commenting on Porphyry, Boethius raised, without finally settling, the **question of universals**: do genera and species — humanity, animality — exist in reality, or only in the mind, or only as words? That question would fuel the great scholastic quarrel between realists and nominalists from Abelard to Ockham.

And in his own voice, awaiting death, he wrote the *Consolation of Philosophy*, which carried Greek ideas about fortune, providence, and the highest good to the entire medieval world in the guise of a Latin dialogue.

Boethius is thus the crucial bridge. Standing between antiquity and the Middle Ages, he saved as much of Aristotle as the coming centuries could hold, framed the problems they would argue, and kept a lamp of Greek philosophy burning through the darkest stretch of Western thought.
