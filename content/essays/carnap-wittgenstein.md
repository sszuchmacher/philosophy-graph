The Vienna Circle, with Rudolf Carnap at its center, read Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* aloud line by line and made it foundational for **logical positivism** — while transforming it into something Wittgenstein disowned.

From the *Tractatus*, Carnap and the Circle took several decisive ideas. First, that **philosophy is not a body of doctrine but an activity of logical clarification** — the analysis of language, not a source of substantive truths about reality. Second, that the propositions of logic and mathematics are **empty tautologies**, true by their form alone. Third, and most explosively, the idea that meaningful language is limited to what can picture facts — that claims outside this range are literally **nonsense**. Wittgenstein had written that the propositions of ethics, metaphysics, and religion cannot be *said*; the Circle seized on this to declare traditional **metaphysics meaningless**.

But Carnap radicalizes and reverses the spirit of the *Tractatus*. Wittgenstein had held that what cannot be said — the ethical, the mystical, the meaning of life — is precisely what matters *most*, showing itself in silence; the unsayable was, for him, the higher region. Carnap keeps the criterion of meaninglessness and throws away the reverence. What cannot be verified is not a sublime beyond to be honored in silence but simply **cognitively empty** — pseudo-statements to be eliminated. Metaphysics is not a mystery gestured at but a disease of language to be cured.

Carnap then makes the criterion positive and technical where Wittgenstein had left it mystical. He develops the **verification principle** — a statement is meaningful only if empirically verifiable (or analytic) — and builds formal systems, a "logical syntax of language," to carry out the clarification the *Tractatus* had only announced. Philosophy becomes the logic of science.

So Carnap continues Wittgenstein by taking the *Tractatus*'s picture of language, its tautological view of logic, and its exclusion of metaphysics as the charter of logical positivism — while radicalizing it into a militant, anti-metaphysical scientific philosophy. He drops Wittgenstein's mysticism entirely: what the *Tractatus* revered as the unsayable, Carnap dismisses as meaningless noise. Wittgenstein, for his part, felt the Circle had fundamentally misunderstood him.
