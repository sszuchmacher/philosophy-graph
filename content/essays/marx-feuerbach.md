Feuerbach had been Marx's great liberator. By inverting Hegel — showing that God is the projected essence of man, that theology is really anthropology — Feuerbach had brought philosophy "down to earth," replacing the self-development of Spirit with the concrete, sensuous human being. Marx embraced this materialism gratefully. And then he turned on its limits.

His critique is compressed into the brilliant *Theses on Feuerbach*. Feuerbach's materialism, Marx argues, is **contemplative** — it conceives reality only as an object *perceived*, and the human being only as a passive, sensuous receiver, not as an *active*, practical agent who transforms the world through labor. Feuerbach observes and interprets; he does not grasp human beings as producers who make and remake their conditions.

This blinds Feuerbach in two ways. First, on **human nature**: he treats the human "essence" as something abstract, inhering in each isolated individual. But the human essence, Marx insists, "is no abstraction inherent in each single individual"; it is "the ensemble of the social relations." We are what our concrete, historical, social conditions make us — not a fixed species-essence to be reclaimed but a set of relations to be transformed.

Second, on **religion**: Feuerbach was content to *explain* religion as human self-projection and stop there, as if unmasking the illusion dissolved it. Marx asks the further question — *why* do human beings need to project a heaven at all? The answer lies not in consciousness but in a defective, suffering material world that drives people to seek consolation in an imaginary one. To abolish religion you must change the conditions that produce the need for it; criticism of heaven becomes criticism of earth.

All of this crystallizes in the eleventh thesis, the most famous sentence Marx ever wrote:

> The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it.

That line is the whole critique. Feuerbach — like the entire tradition — remained an *interpreter*, a materialism of contemplation. Marx demands a materialism of **practice**: one that understands human beings as active, social producers, that traces illusions to their material roots, and that aims not to explain the world but to overthrow and remake it.
