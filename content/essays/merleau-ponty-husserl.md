Merleau-Ponty was the most faithful of Husserl's French heirs and, at the same time, the one who most radically transformed phenomenology — by relocating it from consciousness to the **body**.

From Husserl he takes the whole phenomenological project: the return to lived experience, the analysis of intentionality, the critique of scientific abstractions that lose the concrete world we actually inhabit. And crucially, he draws on the *late* Husserl — the Husserl of the **lifeworld** (*Lebenswelt*), the pre-theoretical world of everyday experience that underlies and is presupposed by science — which was only being published as Merleau-Ponty wrote. He also seizes on Husserl's late analyses of the **lived body** (*Leib*), the body as experienced from within rather than as an object.

But Merleau-Ponty radicalizes these to overturn the Husserl of the transcendental reduction. Husserl's dominant project had been to trace all meaning back to a pure, disembodied **transcendental consciousness** — the ego for which the world is constituted. Merleau-Ponty argues this is impossible and self-undermining. Intentionality is not primarily the act of a pure mind directed at objects; it is **bodily**. We are directed toward the world first and foremost through our moving, perceiving, skillful *bodies*, which grasp and inhabit their environment below the level of explicit thought. The "I think" rests on an "I can" — a bodily know-how that precedes and grounds reflective consciousness.

This makes the famous phenomenological **reduction** impossible to complete. Husserl had hoped to bracket the world entirely and retreat to pure consciousness; Merleau-Ponty replies that "the most important lesson of the reduction is the impossibility of a complete reduction." We can never fully detach from the world and the body to reach a pure spectator-consciousness, because we *are* body-subjects, always already engaged, incarnate, in the world.

So Merleau-Ponty continues Husserl by carrying phenomenology into the description of lived experience and the lifeworld, while radicalizing it past Husserl's own idealism. The transcendental ego gives way to the **lived body**; consciousness is dethroned in favor of embodied perception; and the dream of a presuppositionless pure consciousness is abandoned for a phenomenology of our incarnate, ambiguous, world-involved existence.
