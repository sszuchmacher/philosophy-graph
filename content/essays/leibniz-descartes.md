Leibniz admired Descartes as a pioneer of the mechanical philosophy but judged his physics and metaphysics fundamentally shallow — and the core of the quarrel is Descartes's account of **matter as mere extension**.

Descartes had reduced the material world to *res extensa*: body is nothing but extension in length, breadth, and depth, and all physical phenomena are to be explained by size, shape, and motion alone. Leibniz argues this is not enough to make a *body* at all. Pure extension is geometrical and passive; it cannot account for the **force**, the activity, the resistance and inertia that real bodies actually exhibit. A world of mere extension would be inert and, worse, infinitely divisible into nothing but more extension — with no true unities anywhere, no genuine individual things.

So beneath extension Leibniz restores something Descartes had banished: **force**, and ultimately **substantial form**. Real substances are not parcels of extension but centers of activity — the monads — and what is fundamental in nature is not passive geometry but dynamic power. This lets Leibniz correct Cartesian physics on a technical point he made famous: Descartes held that the quantity conserved in the universe is *quantity of motion* (size times speed); Leibniz shows by argument and example that the conserved quantity is what we would call kinetic energy — *vis viva*, "living force" — a decisive step toward modern dynamics.

The metaphysical stakes are large. By making extension the essence of matter, Descartes had produced a dead, purely mechanical nature and could explain neither force nor the unity of individual things. By reinstating force and form, Leibniz re-enchants nature with activity and restores true individuals.

It is a striking pattern: Leibniz, the modern, defends a version of the very **Aristotelian forms** Descartes had swept away — not out of nostalgia but because he thinks the purely geometrical body of Cartesian physics cannot even be a body.
