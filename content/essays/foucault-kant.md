Foucault's engagement with Kant runs through his whole career and takes a surprising, two-sided form. His doctoral secondary thesis was a translation of and commentary on Kant's *Anthropology*, and one of his last essays was a meditation on Kant's "What Is Enlightenment?" — bookending a lifetime of critical, unorthodox appropriation.

On one side, Foucault is deeply **anti-Kantian**. Kant had sought the *universal, necessary* conditions of knowledge and experience — the a priori structures of any possible mind, fixed for all rational beings. Foucault historicizes this completely. His archaeology looks for the conditions of knowledge too, but they are not universal and necessary; they are the **historical a priori** — the contingent, changing rules of a particular episteme that make knowledge possible in a given epoch and then give way to another. Where Kant's transcendental conditions are timeless, Foucault's are historical, local, and mutable. He thus turns Kant's transcendental inquiry into a historical one, and rejects the universal subject Kant placed at the center.

But on the other side, Foucault reclaims a *different* Kant — the Kant of the little essay "What Is Enlightenment?", which he reads not as doctrine but as inaugurating a new philosophical *attitude*. In that text Kant asks about the present moment, the "today," and defines enlightenment as humanity's exit from its self-imposed immaturity through the daring public use of reason. Foucault seizes on this as the birth of a **critical ethos**: philosophy as a diagnosis of the present, an ongoing interrogation of who we are now and how we have been constituted, aimed at the possibility of thinking and being otherwise.

He reinterprets Kant's "critique" accordingly. Kantian critique asked about the *limits* of knowledge — what we cannot know — in order to police reason's boundaries. Foucault transforms it into a **historical-practical critique**: not an inquiry into universal limits we must respect, but a genealogical analysis of the contingent limits history has imposed on us, undertaken precisely in order to **transgress** them — to show that what seems necessary about our present is the product of particular history and could be otherwise.

So Foucault reinterprets Kant by splitting him in two and turning him against himself. He rejects the Kant of universal transcendental conditions and the sovereign subject, replacing them with the historical a priori and the constituted subject — while embracing and radicalizing the Kant of enlightenment, the philosopher of the present and of critique. He converts Kantian critique from an inquiry into eternal boundaries into a genealogical practice of freedom that maps our historical limits in order to breach them. Not a doctrine but a permanent critical relation to one's own present — that, for Foucault, is the vital Kantian legacy.
