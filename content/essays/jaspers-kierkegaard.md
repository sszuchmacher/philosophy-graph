Karl Jaspers, alongside Heidegger, brought Kierkegaard into twentieth-century German philosophy and made him a founder of existentialism. Jaspers openly named Kierkegaard (with Nietzsche) as the great exception who had broken the spell of academic system-building and reopened the question of the individual's existence — and his own *Existenzphilosophie* is a sustained development of Kierkegaardian themes.

From Kierkegaard, Jaspers takes the central category of **Existenz** — the authentic self that is not an object to be known but a possibility to be realized through free choice and commitment. Like Kierkegaard, he opposes this to the pretension of philosophical systems to capture reality: existence, always in the making, decided by the concrete individual, escapes objective knowledge. Truth about who I am is not theoretical but a matter of how I exist.

He develops the insight in his famous doctrine of **limit situations** (*Grenzsituationen*). Kierkegaard had shown how anxiety, guilt, and despair throw the individual back on the radical choice of the self. Jaspers systematizes this: it is in the boundary situations of existence — death, suffering, struggle, guilt, the unreliability of the world — that we run up against the limits of our everyday selves and are awakened to Existenz and to **transcendence**, the encompassing reality beyond all objects.

And he keeps Kierkegaard's sense that the deepest truth points beyond itself to something ungraspable. Kierkegaard's individual stands before God; Jaspers, more philosophically and less confessionally, speaks of the individual's relation to "transcendence" or "the Encompassing," reached not by proof but by a kind of faith and read through "ciphers" rather than doctrines.

So Jaspers is a faithful, if more temperate and academic, heir. He carries Kierkegaard's existing individual, the priority of choice over system, and the awakening of authentic selfhood in extreme situations into a full philosophy of Existenz — softening the Christian intensity into a broader, humane meditation on freedom, communication, and transcendence, but keeping the Kierkegaardian conviction that what matters most about a human being can never be captured as an object of knowledge.
