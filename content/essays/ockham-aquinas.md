William of Ockham took the razor to the elaborate metaphysical world that Aquinas and the thirteenth century had built. Where the great synthesis was crowded with real essences, universal natures, formal distinctions, and analogies of being, Ockham's instinct was relentless economy: **do not multiply entities beyond necessity.**

The heart of the quarrel is **universals**. Aquinas, a moderate realist, held that the universal natures we grasp — humanity, animality — are really *in* things, grounding our general knowledge; the mind abstracts a common form that things genuinely share. Ockham denies it. Only individuals exist. There is no common "humanity" shared by Socrates and Plato, neither in the world nor in some formal middle realm — there are just Socrates, Plato, and the rest, each utterly singular. Universals are merely **concepts**, natural signs in the mind, and words that stand for many individuals at once.

With one stroke this dissolves much of the apparatus Aquinas relied on. If there are no real universal natures, there is far less for metaphysics to describe; knowledge becomes the study of individuals and the logic of the terms we use to talk about them. Ockham accordingly pours his energy into logic and the analysis of language, where Aquinas had built cathedrals of being.

Behind the parsimony lies a theological motive: a fierce sense of **God's absolute freedom and power**. Aquinas's ordered cosmos of natures and ends could seem to bind God to a rational structure; Ockham insists God could have created and commanded otherwise, so the moral and natural order depend on God's free will, not on necessary essences. What reason can prove of God shrinks accordingly; more is left to faith.

The critique marks a turning point. With Ockham the confident marriage of faith and reason begins to come apart — metaphysics contracts, individual things and revealed faith stand out, and a path opens toward the empiricism and nominalism of the modern age.
