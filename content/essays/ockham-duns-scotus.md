Ockham was trained in the world Duns Scotus had reshaped, and much of his philosophy is a critical settling of accounts with his fellow Franciscan. Nowhere is this sharper than over the problem of **individuation**: what makes an individual the unique individual it is?

Scotus had given a famous and subtle answer. Beyond a thing's common nature — the humanity it shares with others — there is in each individual a further positive principle that contracts that nature to *this* singular thing: its **haecceity**, its "thisness." Socrates is human like everyone else, but his haecceity makes him *this* human and no other. To make room for such a principle, Scotus posited his celebrated *formal distinction* — a distinction between the common nature and the thisness that is less than a real distinction between separate things, yet more than a mere distinction of reason.

Ockham rejects the whole construction as needless multiplication. There is no common nature *in* the individual waiting to be contracted, and so no need for a haecceity to contract it. Every thing is *already*, of itself, individual — singularity is not added to a shared nature but is simply what a thing is. To ask what makes an individual individual is, for Ockham, a pseudo-problem generated by wrongly supposing there were real universals in the first place.

He is equally scornful of the **formal distinction**. Distinctions are either real (between separable things) or purely of reason (in our minds); Scotus's intermediate distinction, neither fully in the thing nor merely in thought, Ockham regards as incoherent — a phantom entity conjured to prop up a phantom problem.

The exchange is nominalism against realism at their most refined. Scotus, still a realist, multiplies formal features within things to explain how the universal becomes particular; Ockham, the nominalist, sweeps them away, insisting the individual needs no explanation because the individual is all there ever was.
