Erich Fromm was the Frankfurt School's great synthesizer of **Marx and Freud**, and his humanistic reading of Marx — centered on alienation and the concrete human being — shaped a whole popular understanding of Marxism in the mid-twentieth century.

From Marx, Fromm took above all the early, **humanist** Marx of the 1844 *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts* — the Marx of **alienation** rather than the later Marx of economic laws. Fromm insisted that these early writings held the key to Marx's whole project: that Marx's deepest concern was not merely the redistribution of wealth but the **estrangement** of human beings from their labor, their products, their fellows, and their own creative nature under capitalism, and the vision of a society in which human beings could become whole and free. Fromm's Marx is a moralist of human self-realization, and he fought against both the capitalist and the Soviet caricatures that reduced Marxism to economics or state power.

To this Fromm added **Freud**, but a Freud revised. He rejected Freud's biological determinism and reinterpreted psychoanalysis socially: human character is shaped not by universal instincts alone but by the specific social and economic conditions in which people live. This let him fuse the two masters — Marx explains the social conditions, a revised Freud explains how those conditions form the psyche. In *Escape from Freedom* he used the synthesis to explain the psychological roots of fascism: how the anxieties of modern freedom drive people toward authoritarian submission.

Fromm's own ethical vision grows directly from the humanist Marx. His famous contrast of **"having" versus "being"** — the acquisitive mode oriented to possession versus the productive mode oriented to loving, creative activity — is a psychological rendering of Marx's critique of alienation and commodity fetishism. And his emphasis on **love, solidarity, and productive work** as the fulfillment of human nature translates Marx's vision of unalienated life into a personal, moral register.

So Fromm continues Marx as the humanist and psychologist of the tradition. He recovers the early Marx of alienation and self-realization, fuses it with a socially revised Freud to explain how society shapes character, and turns the whole into an ethic of being over having and love over possession. Where other Marxists stressed class and economics, Fromm made Marxism a doctrine of human wholeness — and carried it, in accessible books, to a vast general audience.
