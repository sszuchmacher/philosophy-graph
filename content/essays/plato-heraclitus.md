Aristotle reports that Plato, in his youth, was steeped in the Heraclitean teaching of his associate Cratylus: that all sensible things are always flowing, so that one cannot even step *once* into the same river, because it is changing as you speak. If that is the whole truth, knowledge is impossible — for knowledge needs a stable object, and everything slips away before you can grasp it.

Plato does not reject Heraclitus. He *confines* him. The Heraclitean flux is perfectly true — but only of the **sensible world**, the realm of becoming, where nothing simply *is* but is always coming to be and passing away, hot turning cold, large turning small. About this world Cratylus was right: there is no knowledge, only opinion.

But Plato refuses to conclude that knowledge is impossible. Instead he splits reality in two. Above the flowing world of the senses stands the changeless world of **Forms**, and it is these that reason knows — Beauty that never fades, Equality that is never also unequal. Heraclitus describes appearances; Parmenides describes the Forms.

> The things we see are always becoming and never are; the things we think are always, and never become.

This division is Plato's founding move, and it is a marriage of his two great predecessors. He grants Heraclitus the visible world and Parmenides the intelligible one, and makes philosophy the ascent from the first to the second. The flux is not denied — it is demoted, made the shadow-play from which the soul must turn to find what truly is.
