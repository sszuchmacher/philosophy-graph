Before he was a Marxist, Marcuse was a student of Heidegger. He studied at Freiburg in the late 1920s and belonged to the first generation stunned by *Being and Time*. Though he broke with Heidegger — decisively and bitterly, when Heidegger joined the Nazi party in 1933 — the early encounter left a lasting mark, and Marcuse's first major project was an attempt to fuse Heidegger with Marx.

What drew the young Marcuse to Heidegger was the promise of grounding Marxism in a **concrete philosophy of human existence**. The Marxism of the Second International had become an economistic, deterministic doctrine of objective laws, in which the human being all but disappeared. Heidegger's analysis of Dasein — human existence as care, as thrownness, as the concern with authentic and inauthentic ways of being — seemed to supply exactly what orthodox Marxism lacked: a rich account of the concrete human being whose alienation and liberation Marx cared about. Marcuse's early essays tried to build a "Heideggerian Marxism," anchoring the theory of revolution in an existential understanding of authentic human life.

He also absorbed Heidegger's **critique of the technological understanding of the world** — the reduction of beings to mere resources for calculation and control. This would later feed Marcuse's own critique, in *One-Dimensional Man*, of a technological rationality that dominates nature and humanity alike, flattening existence into administered forms.

But Marcuse came to judge Heidegger's philosophy politically and socially **empty and dangerous**. Heidegger's "authentic existence" was abstract, indifferent to real material conditions and the concrete structures of oppression; its resoluteness pointed nowhere in particular and — as 1933 seemed to confirm — could be filled with disastrous content. The discovery of Marx's early *Manuscripts*, with their concrete concept of alienated labor, gave Marcuse a materialist grounding for the analysis of human existence that Heidegger's ontology floated above.

So Marcuse's relation to Heidegger is a formative continuity turned into a break. He took from Heidegger the demand for a concrete philosophy of human existence and the critique of technological domination, and tried to wed them to Marx — before concluding that Heidegger's ontology, detached from material conditions, was politically hollow and worse. The existential concern with authentic life passed into Marcuse's Marxism; the master himself was repudiated.
