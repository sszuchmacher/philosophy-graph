Kripke's short book *Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language* is one of the most influential — and controversial — interpretations in modern philosophy, so much so that its central figure is often called "Kripkenstein," a hybrid whom neither Kripke nor Wittgenstein would fully own.

Kripke extracts from Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* what he takes to be its deepest argument: a **skeptical paradox** about **rule-following** and meaning. Suppose you mean *addition* by "+." You have computed only finitely many sums. What fact about you — in your past behavior, your mental images, your dispositions — determines that you meant *plus* rather than some deviant function ("quus") that agrees with plus on every case you have done but diverges on new ones? Kripke argues that *no* such fact exists. Nothing in your finite history or your mind fixes which rule you were following, and hence what you meant. There is no fact of the matter about meaning.

This is a startling, radical result — meaning-skepticism — and Kripke offers a Wittgensteinian "skeptical solution." We cannot ground meaning in facts about the individual; instead, our talk of meaning and rule-following is licensed by the **community**. To mean addition is to be disposed to give answers the community accepts and to be corrected when one deviates; meaning is a matter of communal agreement in a shared "form of life," not of a fact inside the head. Meaning is irreducibly public.

The relation to Wittgenstein is a **critique** in a peculiar sense — a critique by interpretation. Many Wittgenstein scholars protested that Kripke had foisted on Wittgenstein a skeptical *thesis* he never held: Wittgenstein, they argue, was dissolving a confusion, not proving that there is no fact about meaning. Kripke half-acknowledges this, presenting the argument as one that "struck" him in reading Wittgenstein rather than as strict exegesis.

Either way, the engagement is decisive. Kripke took the later Wittgenstein's remarks on rules — obscure, aphoristic, easily passed over — and forged from them a sharp, powerful argument that put meaning-skepticism and the communal nature of meaning at the center of the philosophy of language. Whether or not it is the real Wittgenstein, it is one of the most fertile readings in the tradition.
