Baudrillard's theory of **simulation** and the **sign** grows out of Saussure's linguistics — and is, in effect, Saussure pushed to an apocalyptic extreme. Where Saussure analyzed how signs work within language, Baudrillard describes a whole society that has become a free-floating play of signs detached from any reality.

From Saussure, Baudrillard takes the foundational idea that the **sign** is a relation between a **signifier** and a **signified**, and that meaning arises from **differences** within a system rather than from any natural bond to things. Saussure had already loosened the tie between language and the world by stressing the *arbitrariness* of the sign and the internal, differential nature of meaning: signs signify through their relations to each other, not through reference to reality.

Baudrillard radicalizes this into a historical thesis about signs cutting loose from reality altogether. In his account, the sign passes through stages: once it *reflected* a reality; then it *masked* a reality; then it masked the *absence* of a reality; and finally, in the age of **simulation**, it bears no relation to any reality at all — it is a **simulacrum**, a copy with no original. Signs now refer only to other signs, circulating in a self-referential system, and this system becomes more real to us than reality — **hyperreality**. The map precedes the territory; the model precedes and generates the "real."

This is Saussure's differential sign — meaning from relations among signs, not from reference — inflated into a total condition of culture. Saussure had said that within language there are only differences without positive terms; Baudrillard says that within contemporary society there are only signs referring to signs, with no positive reality anchoring them. The Saussurean severing of signifier from any natural referent becomes the severing of the *whole order of signs* from reality.

But Baudrillard also breaks with Saussure's structuralist stability. Saussure's system was orderly, a stable *langue* underlying speech; Baudrillard's universe of simulacra is unstable, proliferating, self-consuming — the code running wild. And he turns a technical linguistics into a dark cultural diagnosis of media, consumerism, and a society drowning in images.

So Baudrillard radicalizes Saussure by taking the differential, arbitrary, self-referential sign and generalizing it into a theory of a society where signs have absorbed and replaced reality. Saussure showed that meaning is internal to the system of signs; Baudrillard concludes that the system of signs has swallowed the world, producing hyperreality and simulation — Saussurean semiology turned into the metaphysics of a culture where the real has disappeared behind its own signs.
