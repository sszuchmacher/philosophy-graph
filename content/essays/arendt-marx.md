Arendt engaged Marx seriously and critically throughout her career, and *The Human Condition* is in large part a confrontation with him. She respected Marx as one of the great thinkers of the modern age but judged that he had, catastrophically, **glorified labor** and thereby helped obscure the very political freedom she cared about most.

Her central critique concerns Marx's exaltation of **labor**. Marx had made labor the essence of humanity ("man produces himself"), the source of all value, and the key to both alienation and liberation; the human being, for Marx, is fundamentally a **laboring** animal, and emancipation means the liberation of productive labor. Arendt regards this as a profound error rooted in the modern age's obsession with the life-process. In her tripartite scheme, **labor** is the lowest of human activities — the endless, repetitive toil that merely serves biological necessity and leaves nothing behind, bound to the cycle of consumption. To make labor the essence of humanity is to define the human being by its least free, least distinctively human activity — the activity we share, in principle, with animals.

By exalting labor, Arendt argues, Marx lost sight of **action** — the free deed and word among plural others in the public realm — which for her is the truly human and political activity, the site of freedom and beginning. Marx's vision culminates not in a realm of political freedom but, ironically, in a society of laborers freed to consume — the *animal laborans* triumphant, with the public realm and genuine action withering away. His dream of the withering of the state and the end of politics is, for Arendt, the abolition of the space of freedom, not its fulfillment.

She also criticizes Marx for reducing politics to a function of the economy, thereby denying politics its autonomy, and for thinking of action on the model of **making** (*poiēsis*) — as the fabrication of a future society according to a blueprint, which licenses violence as the "midwife" of history. To treat political action as production, Arendt warns, is to introduce violence and instrumentality into the realm where free action should reign.

So Arendt's relation to Marx is a critique that inverts his hierarchy of human activities. She refuses his exaltation of labor as the human essence, restoring instead the primacy of free political action; she rejects his reduction of politics to economics and his modeling of revolution on fabrication; and she reads his utopia of liberated labor as the loss, not the achievement, of political freedom. Where Marx made humanity a producer whose liberation lay in labor, Arendt made humanity an actor whose freedom lies in the public deed.
